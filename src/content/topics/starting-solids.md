Somewhere around the middle of the first year, the breast/bottle-only chapter starts to share the table with mush, mess, and a very surprised little face. It's a big, fun milestone — and also one with a few real safety rules worth getting right. This page pulls together what Canada's guidance (Health Canada and the Canadian Paediatric Society lead here) actually says, in plain language, so you can start with confidence and not overthink it.

The short version: aim for **around 6 months**, lead with **iron-rich foods**, offer **texture and finger foods early** rather than months of smooth purée, introduce the **common allergens** on purpose, and keep the **hard/round/sticky choking foods** off the tray. Everything below is the detail behind those five ideas.

## When to start

The target is **around 6 months** — not before 4 months, and not much past 6. Health Canada frames complementary foods as beginning at six months, alongside continued breastfeeding ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). The Canadian Paediatric Society puts it simply: "At about 6 months, most babies are ready for solid foods," and notes that waiting *longer* than 6 months actually raises the risk of iron deficiency ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).

Go by the calendar *and* the baby. The readiness signs to watch for:

- **Sits with support and holds their head steady** — good, controlled head and neck muscles are the foundation for safe swallowing ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year), [AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)).
- **The tongue-thrust reflex has faded** — early on, babies automatically push anything solid back out with the tongue; readiness means they can "hold food in their mouth without pushing it out" ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year), [Nemours](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/solid-foods.html)).
- **They're interested in food** — watching you eat, reaching for it, opening their mouth when a spoon approaches ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year), [Nemours](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/solid-foods.html)).
- **They can move food to the back of the mouth** — i.e. get it from the spoon into the throat and swallow, rather than just wearing it ([AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)).

If those aren't all there yet at exactly 6 months, that's fine — a week or two either way is normal. Breastmilk or formula is still doing the heavy lifting for now.

## Iron first

Here's the single most useful thing to know about first foods: **make them iron-rich.** Around six months a baby's own iron stores — built up before birth — run down, and their rapid growth means iron needs are climbing right as the tank empties ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Breastmilk is wonderful but low in iron, so those first bites have a real job to do.

Health Canada's recommendation is to introduce iron-rich meat, meat alternatives, and iron-fortified infant cereal *as the first complementary foods*, and to offer iron-rich foods **a few times each day** ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Good iron-rich starters:

- **Well-cooked meat, poultry, and fish** (finely minced, ground, or mashed; deboned) ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html), [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year))
- **Iron-fortified infant cereal** (a classic, easy first food; thin it with breastmilk or formula to start) ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year), [Nemours](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/solid-foods.html))
- **Well-cooked, mashed legumes** — lentils, beans, chickpeas ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year))
- **Cooked whole egg** and **tofu** ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year))

After iron-rich foods are on the menu, there's no required order for everything else — vegetables, fruit, and dairy products like cheese and yogurt can come in anytime between 6 and 9 months, right alongside the iron foods ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). (The one exception is fluid cow's milk as a *drink* — see [Water & milk](#water--milk).)

## Purées vs. baby-led weaning

You'll hear two camps: spoon-fed purées, and "baby-led weaning" (skipping purées and offering soft finger foods the baby feeds themselves). **Both are valid**, and you don't have to pick a team — plenty of families blend the two.

- **Spoon-fed purées.** Start with a tablespoon or two of a smooth food thinned with breastmilk or formula, offered on a small spoon, and thicken the texture over the coming weeks ([Nemours](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/solid-foods.html), [AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)). It's tidy-ish, easy to gauge amounts, and a gentle on-ramp.
- **Baby-led weaning / self-feeding.** Health Canada actively encourages offering **finger foods from six months** to build self-feeding, chewing, and hand skills — and reassures that this is an experimental process where the baby may not eat much at first ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Offering finger foods among the very first foods takes advantage of a natural window for oral and motor development ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).

Whichever way you lean, the guiding principle is **responsive feeding**: follow the baby's hunger and fullness cues rather than a target amount on the spoon — offer, watch, and stop when they turn away ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Relaxed, pleasant, screen-free family meals — where the baby just gets to be there and watch you eat — do a lot of the teaching ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).

## Textures & progressing

A common trap is getting stuck on smooth purée for months. Don't. Health Canada wants a **variety of soft textures from six months** — lumpy, tender-cooked and finely minced, mashed, or ground — plus soft finger foods, and it says **lumpy textures should be offered no later than nine months** ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). By around one year, aim to have progressed to a variety of textures modified from your own family meals ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). CPS agrees that a range of textures — lumpy, tender-cooked and finely minced, puréed, mashed — plus soft finger foods are recommended from the start ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).

Why the push? Babies develop up-and-down "munching" jaw movements early — they can manage some soft solids like toast and soft-cooked pieces even before teeth arrive — and offering texture on time helps those skills mature ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Safe early finger foods include soft-cooked vegetables and fruit, ripe banana, minced or mashed cooked meat and deboned fish, grated cheese, and toast/bread crusts ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Keep nudging the texture forward as they get the hang of it.

## Allergens

The old advice to *delay* peanut, egg, and other allergenic foods has been reversed. Introduce the **common allergens on purpose, around 6 months**, once a few first foods are going down well — and then keep them in the rotation. There's no evidence that waiting to introduce baby-safe forms of eggs, dairy, soy, peanut products, or fish prevents allergy ([AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)).

Practical approach, per the Canadian Paediatric Society:

- **Introduce allergens one at a time**, so if there's a reaction you know the culprit — but without long gaps between new foods ([CPS](https://cps.ca/en/documents/position/allergenic-solids)).
- **Keep offering it.** Once a food is tolerated, offer it **a few times a week to maintain tolerance** — introducing then dropping it may not help ([CPS](https://cps.ca/en/documents/position/allergenic-solids), [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).
- **Make it baby-safe.** For peanut, thin smooth peanut butter with water or mix it into a purée the baby already eats — never a spoonful of straight peanut butter (see [choking](#choking-safety-vs-gagging)) ([CPS](https://cps.ca/en/documents/position/allergenic-solids)).
- **Do it at home, earlier in the day.** Try a new allergen when you can watch the baby for a few hours and get help easily if needed — no more than one new food per day ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).

The common allergens to make a point of introducing include peanut, egg, cow's-milk dairy, wheat, soy, tree nuts (as butters/flours, not whole), fish, and shellfish ([Nemours](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/solid-foods.html)).

> **A note for higher-risk babies.** If your baby has severe eczema or an already-diagnosed food allergy (or a strong family history), the timing and testing may be different — CPS still points to *around* 6 months but not before 4 months, and recommends talking to your doctor first. This page is general information, not medical advice for a high-risk infant — ask your care provider ([CPS](https://cps.ca/en/documents/position/allergenic-solids), [AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)).

## Choking safety vs. gagging

These two feel the same to a nervous parent but are completely different, and knowing the difference will save you a lot of panic.

**Gagging is normal and protective.** It's a reflex that moves food forward and away from the airway while a baby is learning to eat — noisy, red-faced, sometimes with watery eyes, but the baby is moving air and sorting it out themselves. Health Canada explicitly calls gagging "a natural reflex" of this stage that parents should expect ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Resist the urge to reach in — let the gag do its job.

**Choking is silent and needs help** — the airway is blocked, so there's little or no sound, cough, or breath. That's the one you act on.

To lower the risk, avoid the shapes that block a small airway — **hard, small-and-round, or smooth-and-sticky** foods ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Specific hazards to keep off the tray include whole nuts and seeds, popcorn, hard candy, marshmallows, fish with bones, and snacks on toothpicks or skewers ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html), [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)). Prep the borderline ones safely:

- **Grapes and cherry tomatoes:** quarter them lengthwise, never whole or in coin-shaped slices ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).
- **Hot dogs / sausages** (the food most often linked to fatal child choking): dice or cut lengthwise, never in round coins ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **Raw carrot and hard apple:** grate or cook soft ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **Peanut butter:** spread thin on toast — a glob on a spoon can seal the airway ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **Stringy foods** like celery or pineapple: finely chop ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).

And the rules that apply to *every* meal: baby is **always seated, upright, and supervised** while eating — never propped alone, never eating in a moving car or car seat, where you can't respond and a sudden stop adds risk ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). No preparation prevents every incident, so it's genuinely worth taking an **infant CPR / choking-response** course before or soon after starting solids — Health Canada recommends exactly that ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).

## Foods to avoid

A short "not yet" list that's mostly about safety, not nutrition:

- **No honey before 12 months — full stop.** Honey can carry *Clostridium botulinum* spores that cause infant botulism; pasteurization doesn't kill them, and the risk is real even in baked goods containing honey or honey on a soother. Health Canada's advice: no honey for a child under one year ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html), [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).
- **No cow's milk as a *drink* before 12 months.** Fluid cow's milk shouldn't be the baby's drink before about 9–12 months (delay it), and even then keep milk to no more than 750 mL/day. Cooking with it and dairy foods like cheese and yogurt are fine earlier — it's the *drink* that waits (see [Water & milk](#water--milk)) ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **No added salt or sugar.** Prepare baby's food with little or no added salt or sugar, and limit juice and sweetened drinks — offer water for thirst instead ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **No raw/undercooked meat, fish, or eggs, and no unpasteurized milk or juice** — babies are especially vulnerable to food-borne illness ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).
- **The choking-hazard foods** from the section above.

## Water & milk

**Breastmilk or formula stays the main nutrition through the whole first year** — solids at this stage are about learning to eat, adding iron, and exploring flavours, not replacing milk feeds ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html), [AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx)). Keep breastfeeding or offering formula as you always have.

Once solids start, you can offer **small amounts of water with meals — in an open cup**, helping the baby at first. Open-cup drinking is a safe, easily learned skill in infancy, and it's the recommended way to introduce fluids other than breastmilk ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Exclusively breastfed babies don't *need* extra water, so keep it to sips, not a source of fullness that crowds out milk ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year)).

## Keep it low-pressure

Finally, breathe. Early meals are gloriously inefficient — most of the food ends up on the face, the tray, and you — and that's not a failure, it's the actual learning. Health Canada is clear that self-feeding is a **developmental, experimental process**, and that a baby may not eat a meaningful amount at first; the *exposure* to tastes, colours, and textures is the point ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)).

So let them squish, drop, and explore. Offer the food, follow their cues, and end the meal when they're done rather than coaxing "three more bites" ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Watch the baby, not the clock — pressure and distraction (including the TV) work against building a relaxed, healthy eater ([Health Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-food-guide/resources/infant-feeding/nutrition-healthy-term-infants-recommendations-birth-six-months/6-24-months.html)). Some days they'll devour everything; some days they'll wear it. Both are completely normal.
