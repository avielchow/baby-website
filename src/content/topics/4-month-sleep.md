If your baby was sleeping in long, blissful stretches and then — right around the four-month mark — started waking every couple of hours again, you have not done anything wrong, and neither has the baby. This is one of the most talked-about moments of the first year, usually called the "4-month sleep regression." We think that name is a little unfair. Almost nothing is going backwards. What's really happening is that your baby's sleep is growing up.

A quick honest note on the term: **"sleep regression" is a popular parenting phrase, not a medical diagnosis.** You won't find it in a paediatrician's chart. It's a useful shorthand for a very normal developmental change in how babies sleep — so that's how we'll treat it here.

## What's actually happening

Newborns sleep in a fairly simple, all-or-nothing way. Somewhere around 3 to 6 months, that changes: your baby's brain starts organizing sleep into the mature, cyclical pattern that you and I have — drifting between lighter and deeper stages across the night. The [American Academy of Pediatrics notes that babies don't have regular sleep cycles until around 6 months of age](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/default.aspx), and the four-month window is often when that reorganization becomes obvious to exhausted parents.

Here's the key part. At the end of each of these new sleep cycles, everyone — babies and adults alike — surfaces into a brief, near-waking moment before dropping into the next cycle. Adults do this dozens of times a night and don't remember it; we roll over, fluff the pillow, and go back down. A baby has to do the same thing, but they're just learning how. The AAP describes it plainly: [it is normal for a baby around this age to wake during the night and then go back to sleep after a few minutes](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Getting-Your-Baby-to-Sleep.aspx).

The catch is what your baby associates with falling asleep. If they only ever fall asleep *one specific way* — nursed down, rocked, bounced, held — then when they surface between cycles at 2 a.m., they may need that exact same thing to get back down, and they'll call for you to provide it. The AAP puts it directly: [if babies are held or rocked all the way to sleep, they may struggle to go back to sleep on their own when they wake during the night](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Getting-Your-Baby-to-Sleep.aspx). So the extra wakings aren't a sign that sleep is broken. They're a sign that a new, more grown-up sleep system has come online — and that your baby is still practising the "get myself back down" part.

## What it looks like

Every baby runs their own version of this, and some breeze through it with barely a ripple. But when it shows up, the usual cast of characters is:

- **More frequent night wakings.** The long stretches get chopped up again as your baby surfaces between those new cycles and needs help resettling.
- **Shorter, choppier naps.** The same cycle-surfacing happens in the daytime, so a baby who used to nap 90 minutes might start "cat-napping" 30–45 minutes.
- **Fussiness and clinginess.** Broken sleep makes for a tired baby (and tired parents), so days can feel more emotional.
- **Appetite changes.** Feeding can get erratic — some babies want to nurse or bottle more, sometimes because it's genuinely hunger and sometimes because it's comfort.

For a sense of the normal backdrop this is happening against: Nemours notes that [most babies at 4–7 months sleep about 12–16 hours a day, and by 6 months many manage a 9-hour-plus stretch at night with only brief awakenings](https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html). The Canadian Paediatric Society similarly says [babies at 4 months average around 14 hours of sleep across the day, though anything a bit less or more can be perfectly normal](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child). If your baby doesn't hit those numbers on a given week, that's within the range — these are averages, not targets.

## What helps (safely)

There's no magic switch, but there are calm, low-pressure things that genuinely make this stretch easier. Most of them are less about "fixing" the baby and more about giving them the conditions to figure sleep out.

- **A consistent, calming bedtime routine.** The same short sequence every night tells your baby's body that sleep is coming. The CPS suggests the easy-to-remember **"3 Bs" — bath, book, bed** — for babies in the 4-to-12-month range ([CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child)). Keep it short and boring-in-a-good-way.
- **A chance to practise falling asleep drowsy-but-awake.** Both the CPS and AAP recommend putting your baby down [when they are drowsy but still awake, so they can learn to fall asleep on their own in their own bed](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child). It's a skill, and skills take reps — you don't have to nail it every time.
- **A moment before you rush in.** When your baby stirs, the CPS advises [giving them a few minutes to try to settle on their own before going to them](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child). Sometimes that mid-cycle noise resolves itself; if it builds, of course you go.
- **A consistent, dark, safe sleep environment.** Same crib, same room, dark and quiet, kept the same for naps and nights so your baby knows exactly where they are when they surface.
- **Watch wake windows and overtiredness.** An overtired baby is often a *worse*-sleeping baby. The AAP notes that [keeping babies appropriately active and awake during the day helps them sleep for longer stretches at night](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/Getting-Your-Baby-to-Sleep.aspx) — full days, good naps, not skipping so many naps that they're wired by bedtime.
- **Feed well during the day.** Solid daytime feeds mean less of the night waking is truly hunger-driven.
- **Patience.** This is temporary. As your baby's sleep cycles settle in, most families find the wakings ease over a couple of weeks. The CPS notes that [sometime after 3 months, sleep habits generally become more predictable and a more regular schedule emerges](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child).

**On "sleep training":** you'll hear a lot of strong opinions about specific methods. Different families make different choices here, and there's a wide, respectful range — from responsive, gradual approaches to more structured ones — that reasonable, loving parents land on. Our take is simple: do what fits your family's values and your baby's temperament, lean on the gentle basics above, and if you're weighing a particular method or you're worried, talk it through with your doctor or public health nurse. We're not going to tell you one method is the answer, because the Tier-1 sources don't either.

## Safe sleep still applies

The sleep shift changes *how* your baby sleeps. It changes **nothing** about the safe-sleep rules — those hold every night, for every nap, all the way through the first year. When you're bleary and desperate at 3 a.m., these are the ones that matter most:

- **Back to sleep, every time.** Always place your baby on their back, for every sleep — [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies), and the [AAP notes back-sleepers are far less likely to die suddenly and unexpectedly than stomach- or side-sleepers](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx).
- **A firm, flat surface.** A crib, cradle, or bassinet with a firm, flat mattress and a tightly fitted sheet — [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies). The [AAP adds that the surface shouldn't incline more than about 10 degrees](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx) — no couches, no propped inclines.
- **Baby's own sleep space, in your room.** The CPS recommends [room-sharing — baby in their own crib/bassinet in your room — for the first 6 months](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies); the [AAP notes room-sharing can lower SIDS risk by up to 50% and is much safer than bed-sharing](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx).
- **Nothing loose in the sleep space.** No blankets, pillows, bumper pads, sleep positioners, or toys — [CPS](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies) and [AAP](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx). Warmth comes from a sleep sack, not a blanket.

One thing that often coincides with this age: many babies start rolling around now. That's expected. Keep starting them on their back; a bare, empty crib is exactly why an independent roll is safe.

## When it's more than the shift

The sleep shift is a *behaviour* change in an otherwise well, happy, growing baby. It should not come with signs of illness. Trust your instincts — you know your baby's normal better than anyone — and reach out if the wakings come alongside any of these:

- **Fever, especially in a young baby.** The CPS is unambiguous here: [a baby younger than 3 months old with a fever should be seen urgently, and any baby under 6 months with a fever should see a doctor](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking). Fever is never "just the sleep regression."
- **Poor feeding, or dropping off their growth/weight curve.** Broken sleep shouldn't come with a baby who won't eat or isn't gaining.
- **Any trouble breathing**, or a baby who is [unusually sleepy, lethargic, hard to rouse, or not responding normally](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking) — that's a call-now sign, not a sleep sign.
- **Extreme, inconsolable distress** that's out of character and doesn't settle the way your baby's fussing usually does.

When in doubt, you don't have to figure it out alone at 3 a.m. In Alberta you can call **Health Link at 811** any time to talk to a nurse, or contact your own doctor or public health nurse. A quick "is this normal?" call is always a reasonable thing to do.

## A word for the parents

Let's be honest: this stretch is *hard*. Just when you thought you'd earned some sleep back, it evaporates — and doing anything on broken sleep is brutal. A few things that help the grown-ups survive it:

- **Tag-team the nights.** If there are two of you, split the night into shifts so each person gets one protected block of real sleep. If you're solo, lean on whatever help you can — a relative, a friend for a daytime nap-swap.
- **Lower the bar on everything else.** The dishes can wait. Nap when you can. This is a season for keeping everyone fed and loved, not for a spotless house.
- **Remember it's temporary.** For most families the worst of it passes within a couple of weeks as the new sleep cycles settle. You are not doing this forever.

And one important thing. Being wrecked and touched-out from broken sleep is normal. But if the exhaustion tips into something heavier — a low mood or anxiety that won't lift, feeling hopeless or unable to care for your baby, panic, or just not feeling like yourself for more than a couple of weeks — that deserves attention too. The CPS notes that [postpartum depression affects about 13% of new mothers, is treatable, and that it's important to get help rather than wait it out](https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/depression_in_pregnant_women_and_mothers). Please talk to your doctor, midwife, or public health office, or call **811**. Looking after yourself *is* looking after your baby.
