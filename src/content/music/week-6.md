theory:
  title: "Secondary dominants — any chord can rent a V"
  points:
    - "The rule: any major or minor chord in the key can be preceded by ITS own dominant 7th, even though that dominant doesn't live in the key. V of vi (E7→Am), V of ii (A7→Dm), V of IV (C7→F), V of V (D7→G — week 12's star). The notation V/x reads 'five of x.'"
    - "How to spot them in the wild: an out-of-key sharp in the sheet music or under your fingers is almost always a secondary dominant pointing somewhere. Find what it points at (up a 4th from the chord's root) and you've read the composer's mind."
    - "Why E7→Am lands so hard: G♯ is Am's leading tone — a note whose entire personality is 'one half step below home.' Borrowed urgency, applied to a chord that usually just gets walked into."
    - "The tonicization idea: for one bar, Am gets treated like a key of its own — spotlight, leading tone, arrival. Too short to be a key change (that's modulation, week 12), just long enough to matter. Composers call it tonicizing; you can call it renting."
    - "Week map so far: weeks 4–5 borrowed *chords* from parallel minor (color), this week borrows a *function* (direction). Color changes how a bar feels; function changes where it's going. The best progressions spend both currencies."

Weeks 4 and 5 borrowed *color* from the parallel minor. This week you borrow something
sneakier: **direction**. A secondary dominant is an ordinary chord renting a spotlight —
one sharpened note (G♯) that swings the room's attention toward A minor and makes an
arrival you've played five weeks running suddenly feel like an entrance. It's the oldest
warmth in the songbook — Elvis promising he can't help it runs on exactly this — and once
you can hear the rented light, you'll find it in nearly every song you love.
