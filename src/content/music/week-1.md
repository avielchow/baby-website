lh:
  name: "The 1–5–10 spread"
  description: "The single biggest upgrade from root-position broken chords. Instead of C–E–G clumped in one fist, you open the hand across a 10th: root, 5th, then the 10th (the 3rd, one octave up). It's the default left hand of every pop ballad and most of Yiruma — wide, warm, and it leaves the middle of the piano free for your melody."

melody:
  technique: "Chord tones on the strong beats, color between"
  description: "The reason melodies feel 'uninspired' over chords is usually that they never commit to the chord — or never leave it. The fix is a rhythm of commitment: land on a chord tone when the chord changes (beat 1), wander through the in-between notes (the 2nd, 4th, 6th) on the weak beats, then land again. The wandering notes are called passing tones, and they're where the singing happens."

theory:
  title: "What slash chords, 7ths, and add9s actually are"
  points:
    - "A slash chord (G/B) is not a new chord — it's a G chord with a specific note on the bottom. Composers use them to make the BASS sing a line (here: C, B, A, F — nearly a scale) while the harmony stays simple."
    - "A 7th chord (Am7) = the triad plus the note a 7th above the root — but the useful way to hear it: it's usually a note HELD OVER from the previous chord. Sevenths are memory."
    - "add9 (Fadd9) = triad + the 9 (same letter as the 2), with no 7th. The 9 adds shimmer without jazz. If it has the 7 too, it'd be Fmaj9 — next week's business."
    - "The common-tone trick: before moving your hand for a new chord, check what can STAY. Every note you keep is glue, and glued chords sound expensive."
    - "Roman numerals are just the chord's address in the key (C major: I=C, ii=Dm, iii=Em, IV=F, V=G, vi=Am). Knowing the address is what lets you move a progression to any key later."

Ten years of classical hands and no names for what they know — this section fixes the
second part. Each week is one progression worth living in, and this first one is the
gentlest deep idea in pop harmony: **a bass line that walks down by step while one right-hand
note refuses to move.** It's the harmonic heart of "For Good," and by Friday it'll be under
your fingers with your own tune on top — played quiet, over a bassinet, for the most
forgiving audience you'll ever have.
