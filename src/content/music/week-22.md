theory:
  title: "Compound time — why 12/8 rocks and 4/4 walks"
  points:
    - "Simple meter splits beats in two; compound meter splits them in three. 12/8 = four beats of three — a 4/4 song wearing a rolling gait. The chords, keys, and voice-leading rules all survive untouched; only the space between beats changes shape."
    - "Threes feel physical because they're asymmetric: two-splits produce marching (left-right), three-splits produce swaying (down-around-up). Rocking chairs, waves, and adults soothing infants all move in threes. This is embodied music theory — the baby knew it before you did."
    - "I–vi–IV–V vs I–vi–ii–V (week 20): the doo-wop and the standard turnaround differ by one chord — IV is open-hearted (subdominant), ii is aimed (pre-dominant). Slow dances prefer IV; jazz prefers ii. Both loop forever; they just lean differently."
    - "The 6/8-vs-3/4 distinction in one sentence: 6/8 is TWO big beats of three (sway), 3/4 is THREE beats of two-ish (waltz) — same six eighth-notes, different grouping, completely different dance. 12/8 is 6/8's full-length coat."
    - "Repertoire unlocked by this one skill: Hallelujah, Earth Angel, A Whiter Shade of Pale, Can't Help Falling in Love (week 6's song — secretly 12/8 all along!), and virtually every lullaby in the Western canon. One left-hand pattern, an entire genre of gentleness."

Twenty-one weeks of WHAT to play; this week is HOW it moves. **The doo-wop turnaround
in 12/8** — the fourth, the fifth, the minor fall, the major lift, exactly as Hallelujah
narrates — swapped out of marching time and into the triplet sway that rocking chairs,
ocean waves, and every parent's arms discovered independently. The chords are week 1's.
The left hand learns to roll in threes, the melody learns to breathe in threes, and the
course finally admits what it's been building since the first bar: you are constructing
a repertoire for an audience of one, who is rocked to sleep at precisely this tempo.
