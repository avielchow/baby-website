theory:
  title: "ii–V–I: the sentence every song is secretly writing"
  points:
    - "ii–V–I is harmonic grammar: preparation (ii), tension (V), resolution (I). Once you hear it, it's everywhere — jazz standards run entire songs on chains of it, and week 7 will."
    - "Why ii beats IV on the way to V: Dm7 and G share no urgency, but D's root falls a 5th to G, and root motion by falling 5ths is the strongest gravity in harmony. ii is IV with a destination. (Dm7 even contains the whole F chord — look at your right hand.)"
    - "A suspension is defined by three moments: preparation (the note belongs), suspension (the chord moves, the note stays), resolution (the note steps home). All three matter — an unprepared suspension is just an appoggiatura (week 2's move)."
    - "sus4 vs add4: sus REPLACES the 3rd (open, unresolved sky), add keeps both (denser, jazzier). G7sus4 = G–C–D–F, no B anywhere."

Two weeks of color; now the engine that aims it. **ii–V–I** is the sentence structure of
tonal music — subject, verb, full stop — and this week you'll play it the way ballads
actually do: with the V held back by a suspension, so the sentence catches its breath
mid-phrase. The whole week is really about one lesson: **tension you prepare is tension
the listener trusts.** Rush the resolution and it's an exercise; delay it one beat past
comfortable and someone in the room stops talking. At 3 a.m., over a bassinet, aim for
the second one.
