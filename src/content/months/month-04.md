monthNumber: 4
title: "Four months — rolling, belly laughs, and the great sleep shake-up"
description: "The start of the golden age: your baby holds their head steady, reaches for and grabs everything (straight to the mouth), chuckles and squeals on cue — and, right on schedule, sleep gets bumpier as it reorganizes into more grown-up cycles."
milestones:
  - title: "Rolling begins — usually tummy-to-back first"
    detail: "Somewhere around now many babies pull off their first roll, and the easier direction — stomach to back — tends to come first, with back-to-tummy following in the weeks after. It often looks accidental at first: a big push-up in tummy time tips them over and surprises everyone, including the baby. Give it plenty of floor time to practise, and treat the first roll as a safety turning point, not just a milestone — a baby who can flip is a baby who can no longer be left on a bed or change table for even a second. Some perfectly on-track babies don't roll until closer to 6 months, so this is a wide window, not a deadline."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
  - title: "Head held steady, and pushing up in tummy time"
    detail: "By four months most babies hold their head steady without support when you hold them upright, and on their tummy they push up onto the forearms — some onto straight arms — lifting the chest to look around. This is the core strength that rolling, and later sitting, are built on, so keep offering short tummy sessions through the day. Getting down face-to-face turns the work into a game and buys you more minutes before the fussing starts. Once head control is solid and reliable, propping them up for a new view of the world (a stroller, a floor seat) becomes an option too."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Reaches for toys, grabs them, and brings everything to the mouth"
    detail: "Hands have become tools: babies this age swing at dangling toys, reach out and grab what interests them, hold a rattle you place in their palm, and study their own hands with real fascination. And whatever they catch goes straight to the mouth — the mouth is their most sensitive tool for exploring, so mouthing is learning, not just teething. This new reach-and-grab skill is exactly why small objects, cords, and anything breakable now have to stay out of arm's length. Offer lightweight, easy-to-grasp, washable toys with no small parts."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Very social: chuckles, squeals, coos, and copies you"
    detail: "This is the month babies get genuinely chatty and charming — they coo in long 'ooh' and 'aah' strings, squeal, turn their head toward your voice, make sounds back when you talk, and chuckle when you try to make them laugh (full belly laughs are right around the corner). They smile to get your attention and will move, coo, or fuss to keep it once they have it. Answer every sound with a sound of your own and leave a gap for their reply — that back-and-forth is real conversation practice. Ranges are wide here, and the milestone checklist is a conversation-starter for the checkup, not a scorecard."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Help the rolling along — and keep tummy time a no-cry zone"
    detail: "The single best thing for rolling and the strength behind it is still tummy time, in short frequent bursts a few times a day, built up to your baby's tolerance. You can coach the motion gently: lay them on one side and hold a toy just out of reach so they follow it over, or guide them at the hips (not the arms) through a slow roll so their upper body does the work. Keep it playful — get down on the floor with them — and if they're genuinely upset rather than just working hard, scoop them up and try again later. Grabbing for their own toes, which shows up around now, is the same core-strengthening move in disguise."

  - title: "Feed the new grip: sensory toys, mirrors, and books"
    detail: "Now that reaching and grasping have arrived, offer things worth grabbing — lightweight rattles, crinkly and squeaky sensory toys, and activity gyms with danglers they can bat and catch. A baby-safe mirror is still a favourite (that fascinating 'other baby'), and high-contrast board and cloth books hold their gaze as you turn the pages, so read aloud even though the words mean nothing yet — it's the voice, faces, and rhythm that land. Cause-and-effect is the current thrill: a toy that rattles or lights up when they move it teaches 'I did that,' which they find genuinely amazing. One or two good toys beat a bin full — they can still only attend to one thing at a time."

  - title: "Talk, sing, and make faces — they're copying you now"
    detail: "Your baby is watching your mouth and expressions closely and starting to mimic sounds and faces, so narrate your day, sing, and play face games — stick out your tongue, raise your eyebrows, make an exaggerated 'oh!' and wait to see it echoed back. Name what they're looking at and pause for their coo before you answer; those turn-taking 'conversations' are how early language is built long before the first word. This is also the age they realize there's a whole world beyond your face, which is delightful but also why feeds can get distracted — a quiet, boring room helps at mealtimes."

feeding:
  - title: "Still milk only — and the amounts have crept up"
    detail: "Nothing on the menu changes this month: breast milk or formula is still all your baby needs. Breastfed babies typically nurse around 6 to 8 times in 24 hours, often with fewer middle-of-the-night feeds than before. Formula-fed babies are usually taking somewhere between 4 and 6 oz (120–180 ml) per feeding — on the way to the 6–8 oz feeds the AAP describes by six months — and as a rule babies don't need more than about 32 oz (960 ml) of formula a day. These are averages — feed to your baby's appetite and let steady weight gain and plenty of wet diapers, not a target number of ounces, tell you it's working."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/formula-feeding/Pages/Amount-and-Schedule-of-Formula-Feedings.aspx" }

  - title: "Not ready for solids yet — the signs are still building"
    detail: "Even though the reaching and mouthing make it look like your baby is dying to eat, experts recommend waiting until around 6 months for solids, and starting sooner isn't better. The readiness signs are assembling now but usually aren't all there yet: good head and neck control, being able to sit up with support, the tongue-thrust reflex (which pushes food back out) fading, and real interest — staring at your food, leaning in, opening the mouth. Milk still covers all their nutrition for now; solids arrive to add iron and zinc later. When the time comes, check with your doctor first and start slow — the Starting Solids deep dive in the guide covers the whole thing."

  - title: "The drool has arrived — and it usually isn't teething"
    detail: "Around now many babies turn into little fountains, soaking bibs and blowing bubbles, and it's tempting to blame teeth. Usually it isn't: while teething can begin as early as 3 months, most babies don't get a first tooth until sometime between 4 and 7 months, and the early drool is mostly maturing salivary glands plus all that hand- and toy-chewing. A little chin rash from constant wetness is common — pat (don't rub) it dry and a barrier cream helps. If a tooth genuinely is coming, you'll usually also see gum-rubbing and wanting to chew hard on things."

sleep:
  - title: "The 4-month sleep shift — real, developmental, and temporary"
    detail: "If your good sleeper suddenly starts waking more, fighting naps, and surfacing every couple of hours, you've likely hit the so-called '4-month sleep regression' — and it's better understood as a permanent maturing of how sleep works. Around now a baby's sleep reorganizes from the simpler newborn pattern into more adult-like cycles of light and deep sleep, so they briefly wake between cycles and haven't yet learned to link them back together. It isn't a step backward or anything you did wrong, and it usually settles over a few weeks. Total sleep is around 12 to 16 hours a day including naps, and many (not all) 4-month-olds manage a 5- to 6-hour stretch at night. The deep-dive guide covers what actually helps."

  - title: "Safe sleep still applies — and now, stop swaddling"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, firm flat surface, own crib or bassinet in your room, nothing loose in the space — none of that changes. Two things do change with rolling: once your baby can roll both ways confidently you don't have to keep flipping them back if they turn over in the night, but the crib must be bare of blankets, pillows, bumpers, and toys so any position is safe. And the moment your baby looks like they're trying to roll, stop swaddling — a swaddled baby who rolls to their stomach can't push up or free their arms, which raises the suffocation risk. Move to a sleep sack with the arms out."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "A baby who can roll is never left on a high surface"
    detail: "This is the big one this month. The instant your baby can flip — and it can happen the very first time, with no warning — the change table, the bed, the couch, and the daybed all become fall hazards. Keep one hand on your baby at every diaper change, gather your supplies before you start, and if you ever need both hands, put them on the floor. 'Just for a second' is exactly how the falls happen, because the first roll is usually the one nobody saw coming."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/Your-Childs-Checkups/Pages/Your-Checkup-Checklist-4-Months-Old.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
  - title: "Everything goes in the mouth — clear the small stuff"
    detail: "Now that your baby reaches, grabs, and mouths whatever they can catch, the floor and every surface within arm's reach need a sweep: small objects, coins, button batteries, magnets, cords, and anything that fits through a toilet-paper tube are choking or strangulation risks. Keep toys lightweight, washable, and one-piece with no parts that can break off, and keep blind cords, charging cables, and drawstrings well out of range. This is the start of babyproofing — it only scales up from here as they get mobile."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Lower the crib mattress and check the crib itself"
    detail: "As your baby gets stronger and starts pushing up, set the crib mattress to its lowest position so a future sit-or-stand can't tip them over the rail, and make sure the crib meets Health Canada's standards (no drop-sides, snug mattress with no gaps, tightly fitted sheet). Keep the crib clear of anything they could grab, mouth, or later use to climb — including bumper pads and large toys. If you use a floor seat or bouncer, it belongs on the floor, never up on a table or counter."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "The 4-month well-child visit"
    detail: "Expect the familiar rhythm: weight, length, and head circumference plotted on the growth chart, a head-to-toe exam (eyes, heart, lungs, and a hip check), and a look at how your baby moves and interacts. The provider will review feeding, sleep, peeing, pooping, and development, and — as at two months — will ask how the parents are doing, often with a short postpartum-depression screening questionnaire. Bring your baby's Alberta health card and a written list of questions; anything from the drool to the sleep to a sound they aren't making yet is fair game, and none of it is too small to raise."

  - title: "The 4-month shots — with one new vaccine this visit"
    detail: "Alberta's 4-month routine is the second dose of DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Hib, hepatitis B in one needle), the second pneumococcal conjugate (PNEU-C-15), and rotavirus by mouth again — plus one that's new at this visit: the meningococcal C conjugate (Men-C-C), given at 4 months and again at 12. These are given at your public health / community health centre. Aftercare is the same as two months: breastfeed or hold your baby skin-close during and after, expect a possible low-grade fever, fussiness, and a sore red spot for a day or two, and treat discomfort with cuddles, a cool cloth on the site, and the correct weight-based dose of infant acetaminophen — never aspirin."

  - title: "Growth: the curve matters more than the number"
    detail: "What the doctor is looking for isn't a particular weight but a steady climb along your baby's own curve — a chunky baby and a long, lean baby can both be perfectly healthy as long as weight and length track together over time. Many babies are around double their birth weight by four to five months, and a single percentile means far less than the trend across visits. If your baby seems suddenly to be crossing several percentile lines up or down, that's the thing worth flagging, not a plump belly or slim thighs on their own."

redFlags:
  - title: "Development: things to mention at the visit"
    detail: "The four-month checkup is exactly the place to raise a milestone your baby hasn't reached — as a conversation, not an alarm. Worth mentioning: not holding the head steady, not bringing hands or objects to the mouth, not reaching for or grabbing toys, not turning toward voices or reacting to sounds, not watching things as they move, or not smiling and engaging socially. Any one of these on its own often just means your baby is on the later end of a wide normal range, and premature babies are tracked by their adjusted age; the point is to flag it so your provider can keep an eye on it, not to score a pass or fail."

  - title: "Fever: a low one after shots is expected; otherwise call"
    detail: "A mild fever in the first day or two after the vaccines is a common, expected reaction — dress your baby lightly, offer extra feeds, and use the correct weight-based dose of infant acetaminophen for comfort. Outside of that window, a baby under 6 months with a fever should be seen by a doctor, and Health Link 811 can guide you any hour. Take the temperature rectally for accuracy at this age (36.6–37.9°C is the normal rectal range), and seek care promptly for a fever that climbs high, lasts beyond about 48–72 hours, or comes with a baby who seems genuinely unwell, unusually sleepy, or hard to wake."

  - title: "Your own mental health — the visit screens for it again"
    detail: "The baby blues are long gone by four months, so lingering sadness, hopelessness, loss of pleasure, constant anxiety, rage, or trouble bonding is postpartum depression or anxiety — it can start or resurface months after birth, it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers (partners can develop it too), and it's very treatable. The four-month visit deliberately screens for it, so answer the questions honestly; it's the system working, not a judgment. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811 if a low mood is hanging on, and treat any thought of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency — call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Welcome to the golden age"
    detail: "Four months kicks off what a lot of parents remember as the sweetest stretch of the first year: your baby is sociable, smiley, and endlessly interested in you, sleeping a bit more at night, and — crucially — still stays roughly where you put them, since real mobility hasn't arrived. Enjoy this window while it lasts; the reaching and rolling are the opening act of a very busy year, and 'plop them down and they're still there' does not survive crawling. Soak up the belly laughs."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
  - title: "The great changing-table wriggle (and seat protests)"
    detail: "An increasingly active baby who's just discovered they can turn over is not going to lie still for a diaper, and the squirming only grows from here. Be quick — have everything ready before you lay them down — and distract with a song, a toy kept just for the change, or a few belly raspberries. The same restlessness shows up as fussing in the car seat or bouncer once they'd rather be moving; a mirror, a rotated toy, or simply more time free on a play mat usually helps more than another gadget."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
  - title: "Thumb and finger sucking is fine — let them"
    detail: "Around now the hands-to-mouth habit often settles into dedicated thumb or finger sucking, and it's a healthy way for your baby to self-soothe — no need to stop it. There's no evidence it harms the alignment of the permanent teeth as long as it winds down by about age 5, and the large majority of kids give it up on their own well before then. If a breastfed baby seems to be sucking fingers instead of feeding enough, offer a longer nurse; otherwise, let them find their own comfort."
