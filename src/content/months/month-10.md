monthNumber: 10
title: "Ten months — cruising, waving, and into everything"
description: "The tenth month is all about mobility and connection: pulling up and cruising along the furniture, a neat thumb-and-finger pincer grasp, first real gestures like waving and pointing, and a baby who understands far more than they can say — which is exactly why the babyproofing has to keep pace."
milestones:
  - title: "Pulling up and cruising along the furniture"
    detail: "By now most babies pull themselves to standing at every chance they get, and once they feel steady they start to cruise — shuffling sideways along the couch or coffee table while holding on with both hands. The AAP describes this sequence directly: once your baby feels secure standing, he'll try tentative steps while holding on for support, and when your hands aren't available he'll cruise alongside furniture. Some will let go to stand wobbling on their own for a second or two; actual first independent steps usually come around the first birthday, but it's completely normal for that to land earlier or later. Learning to lower back down often lags behind pulling up, so expect some stuck-and-frustrated moments while they figure out the sitting-down half."

  - title: "A neat pincer grasp — thumb and finger"
    detail: "Somewhere around 9 to 10 months the grasp sharpens from a whole-hand rake into a true pincer — picking up small things between the thumb and the side of the index finger — and by the first birthday it refines further into a precise fingertip pinch. The Canadian Paediatric Society lists finger-feeding using thumb and forefinger, and poking with an index finger, as skills for this stretch. This is why self-feeding suddenly gets more efficient and why anything small on the floor now goes straight into the mouth. The upside is real fine-motor progress; the flip side is that this is exactly the age to get serious about keeping small objects off the floor and out of reach."

  - title: "First real gestures — waving, clapping, pointing"
    detail: "Communication is going gestural: many babies this age begin to wave bye-bye, clap, lift their arms to be picked up, and start pointing at things they want you to look at. Nemours notes the older infant lifting their arms to be picked up as a clear communication milestone, and the CPS milestone list adds copying you during play. Pointing is a particularly big one — it's shared attention, the baby saying 'look at that, with me,' which is a foundation for language. Wave back, clap along, and follow their point with words ('yes, the dog!'); answering a gesture teaches that communication gets a response."

  - title: "Babbling with inflection — and 'mama'/'dada' starting to mean something"
    detail: "The babble is getting speech-like: long strings such as 'ba-ba-ba' and 'ma-ma-ma' with the rise-and-fall inflection of real sentences, as if they're already having a conversation. Nemours lists making sounds like 'ba-ba-ba' or 'ma-ma-ma' among the sounds to expect, and around this age the CPS milestones describe saying 'mama' or 'dada' with meaning. For some babies 'mama' and 'dada' start to land on the right parent this month; for others those are still just fun sounds for a while yet, and both are normal. Keep narrating your day and leaving pauses for their reply — the back-and-forth is how the sounds turn into words."

  - title: "Understanding more than they can say — and hunting for hidden things"
    detail: "Receptive language is racing ahead of talking: babies this age respond to their own name, follow simple familiar requests like 'give it to me' or 'wave bye-bye,' and clearly register the word 'no' (even if obeying it is another matter entirely). Object permanence is now solid — the AAP explains that by 10 months a baby is so certain a hidden toy still exists that they'll keep searching for it, where months earlier 'out of sight' meant gone for good. That's also why peekaboo is endlessly delightful and why they'll drop a toy over the high-chair edge again and again to watch what happens. Their attention span is stretching too, from a couple of minutes on a toy toward sitting with a favourite for much longer by the first birthday."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "From cruising to walking — let them do the hard part"
    detail: "The job this month isn't to make your baby walk sooner, it's to give them safe things to pull up on and cruise between. Arrange sturdy, stable furniture into a little 'cruising circuit,' and offer a push-along walker they stand behind (never a sit-in one — see the gear note). Resist the urge to constantly rescue them: getting stuck at a piece of furniture and figuring out how to get unstuck is exactly the problem-solving that builds toward independent steps. Holding both their hands to 'walk' them around is fine fun, but the real work happens when they're supporting their own weight."

  - title: "Social games that teach big ideas"
    detail: "Peekaboo, pat-a-cake, 'so big,' and rolling a ball back and forth aren't just cute — they're teaching object permanence, turn-taking, and imitation all at once. The AAP specifically recommends peekaboo as the way to help a baby cement the idea that things (and people) still exist when they can't see them, and suggests switching up the variations to keep it interesting almost indefinitely. Pat-a-cake and waving are copying games, and around now babies love to imitate — you'll catch them holding a toy phone to their ear because they've worked out what phones are for. Narrate and demonstrate; they're watching everything you do and filing it away."

  - title: "Talk, name, point, and read together"
    detail: "Since understanding is running well ahead of speech, the best language input is simply talking through your day in real words: naming what they point at, giving simple one-step instructions, and pausing to let them 'answer' with a babble or a gesture. Point at pictures in a board book and name them; follow their pointing finger and supply the word. This turn-taking — you say something, you wait, they respond — is the engine of early language, and it feels sillier and works better than any app. The CPS notes babies this age come when called and copy you during play, so lean into being copied."

  - title: "Drop it, dump it, fill it — cause-and-effect play"
    detail: "This is the age of the little scientist: the AAP describes babies who never tire of dropping, rolling, throwing, and waving objects just to find out how they behave, and who love a container they can fill and empty over and over. Feed the experiment with safe props — a cup and some blocks to drop in and tip out, nesting bowls, a ball to chase, low pillows to climb over. Stacking rings and shape sorters are great now, though don't expect them to actually solve either without your help for a while. The repetition that drives you a little crazy is genuine learning about how the world works."

feeding:
  - title: "Three meals plus snacks — with milk still doing the heavy lifting"
    detail: "By now solids have become a real part of the day: a typical rhythm is three meals plus a couple of snacks, alongside breast milk or formula. The AAP puts total daily needs around 750 to 900 calories, of which roughly 400 to 500 — about 24 ounces (720 mL) a day — still comes from breast milk or formula. In other words, milk remains the nutritional anchor this month even as the plate gets busier; the balance keeps shifting toward food over the next couple of months. Don't chase exact amounts — offer a variety of healthy foods on a predictable schedule and let their appetite, which swings a lot day to day, do the rest."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/sample-one-day-menu-for-an-8-to-12-month-old.aspx" }
  - title: "Self-feeding finger foods — that new pincer at work"
    detail: "With the pincer grasp online, this is prime finger-food time: the CPS recommends offering soft finger foods and a variety of textures (lumpy, tender-cooked and finely minced, mashed) to encourage self-feeding. Good early picks are soft, gum-able pieces — well-cooked vegetable sticks, soft fruit, cooked pasta, cereal O's, small bits of scrambled egg, grated or soft cheese. Keep pieces small and soft, always stay within arm's reach at meals, and know that gagging (noisy, red-faced, self-correcting) is a normal part of learning to manage textures, unlike true choking (silent). Iron-rich foods — meat, egg, iron-fortified cereal, well-cooked lentils and beans — matter now that milk is a smaller share of the diet."

  - title: "Cup practice, water, and a couple of hard limits"
    detail: "This is a great month to keep offering an open cup (with help at first) or a straw cup, alongside sips of water with meals — the CPS advises starting to offer water occasionally in an open cup once your baby is eating other foods. Two firm rules still stand: no honey before 12 months, because of the risk of infant botulism, and if you offer juice at all, cap it at 125 mL (4 oz) a day — babies don't actually need juice, and too much crowds out better food and feeds tooth decay. Water and milk are the drinks that matter. Expect a lot of the cup's contents to end up on the tray while they practise; that's the learning, not a failure."

  - title: "Mess is the method — and appetite bounces around"
    detail: "Babies this age eat like a mouse one day and a horse the next, and mealtimes are at least as much about exploring texture, temperature, and cause-and-effect as about nutrition — the squishing, smearing, and dropping is genuine learning, not bad manners. You can contain the chaos without shutting down the learning: a mat under the chair, a bib with sleeves, and rationing just a few bites onto the tray at a time so there's less to fling. Try not to take over the spoon — that neat-but-frustrated route skips the self-feeding practice they need. Applaud the calm bites, stay neutral about the theatrics, and call the meal done when playing clearly outpaces eating."

sleep:
  - title: "Two naps and a longer night — but waking is still normal"
    detail: "Most babies this age have settled into two naps a day, a morning and an afternoon one, having dropped from three somewhere in the 6-to-12-month window; the CPS notes nap needs vary widely, from 20-minute cat-naps to stretches of three hours. Plenty of babies are sleeping through the night by now, but plenty aren't, and night waking at ten months — often tied to all the new mobility being 'practised' at 2 a.m., or to separation feelings — is still within normal. An overtired baby actually sleeps worse, so skipping naps to 'tire them out' backfires. Watch that afternoon nap doesn't run too late, since a late nap eats into night sleep at this age."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child" }
  - title: "Keep the routine boringly consistent"
    detail: "A predictable, repeated wind-down — bath, book, quiet cuddle, bed, in the same order each night — is one of the most reliable things you can do for sleep at this age; the CPS emphasizes a regular daytime and bedtime schedule and a consistent bedtime routine. Rhythmic self-soothing at bedtime (rocking, even a bit of head-banging against the crib) looks alarming but is a common, harmless way babies wind themselves down, and usually fades on its own. Fill the comfort tank with cuddles before sleep, and try to brake gradually into bedtime rather than going straight from a wild game to the crib. Consistency beats any single sleep trick."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/healthy-living/healthy_sleep_for_your_baby_and_child" }
  - title: "Safe-sleep basics haven't changed"
    detail: "A mobile, standing baby doesn't change the fundamentals: back to sleep, on a firm flat surface, in their own crib with nothing loose in it. Once your baby can pull to standing, lower the crib mattress to its lowest setting so they can't topple over the rail, and make sure the crib sits away from windows, blind cords, and furniture they could use to climb out. They may now roll or shift to their favourite position on their own, which is fine — you still always place them down on their back. Keep the sleep space empty of pillows, bumpers, and heavy blankets."
    sources:

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Anchor furniture and TVs — pulling up meets tip-over risk"
    detail: "The single most important new job this month: a baby who pulls to stand will haul on anything within reach, so bookshelves, dressers, and TVs must be secured to the wall with anti-tip straps or brackets. Tip-overs are a leading cause of serious injury at exactly this stage, because the baby now has the strength to pull and the height to be under a falling object. Also remove wobbly or top-heavy furniture from their cruising routes, and never leave drawers open (they make an irresistible ladder). Get down at their eye level and look for the next thing they'll try to climb — because they will."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Safety-for-Your-Child-6-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Gates on the stairs, guards on the windows"
    detail: "Now that they can cruise and crawl fast, the AAP advises gates at the top and bottom of stairways and operable window guards on any window above the first floor. Babies learn to climb up long before they can safely climb down, so an unguarded staircase is a real hazard even for a baby who isn't walking yet. Choose a hardware-mounted gate for the top of stairs rather than a pressure-mounted one, which can give way under a leaning baby. Keep chairs and stools away from windows and railings so there's nothing to boost them up."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Safety-for-Your-Child-6-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Hot, sharp, and small — move it up and out of reach"
    detail: "A cruising baby can reach counter and table edges, so the AAP is blunt: never leave cups of hot coffee or tea near an edge, cook on the back burners with handles turned in, and keep your baby in a high chair or playpen while you're at the stove. Keep small objects and anything that fits through a toilet-paper tube completely out of reach — the new pincer grasp finds them instantly and they head straight for the mouth. Pad sharp table and hearth corners, and move household cleaners, medicines, and chemicals up high and behind latched doors. Cords from blinds and drapes strangle — tie them up out of reach or switch to cordless."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Safety-for-Your-Child-6-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "The one 'walker' to never buy — sit-in baby walkers are banned in Canada"
    detail: "As your baby works toward walking, it's tempting to reach for a wheeled seat they sit inside — don't. Sit-in baby walkers have been banned in Canada since 2004 — the first country in the world to do so — and it is illegal to import, advertise, or sell them, including secondhand at garage sales. They're banned because a baby in one can move faster than an adult can react, reach hazards they otherwise couldn't (hot drinks, stove handles), and tumble down stairs — and the AAP likewise says to avoid them entirely. The safe alternative is a push-along walker the baby stands behind and pushes (see the gear note below); those support real walking practice without the danger."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "No routine vaccines this month"
    detail: "There are no routine immunizations scheduled at ten months on Alberta's infant schedule. After the 2-, 4-, and 6-month rounds, the next set of routine shots comes at 12 months — MMR-Var (measles, mumps, rubella, varicella), meningococcal conjugate (Men-C), and the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine. So this is a quiet month on the needle front; if you're due for anything it's more likely a catch-up dose your public health nurse flagged, or a seasonal flu shot in the fall for babies 6 months and older. If you're unsure where your baby stands, your community health centre or Health Link 811 can check the record."

  - title: "Between visits — you're the monitor now"
    detail: "Alberta's routine well-baby and immunization visits cluster at 2, 4, 6, and 12 months, so ten months typically falls between scheduled appointments. That doesn't mean you wait — if something's on your mind (a feeding worry, a skill you haven't seen, sleep, a rash, your own mood), call your family doctor or Health Link 811 rather than sitting on it until the one-year visit. Keep noticing growth and development in the meantime; the milestone lists are conversation-starters for that next visit, not a test to pass. Jot questions down as they come up so they're ready when the 12-month checkup rolls around."

redFlags:
  - title: "Not moving to explore, or not bearing weight on the legs"
    detail: "Mobility comes in many flavours — crawling, scooting, rolling, bum-shuffling — but by around now a baby should be finding some way to move themselves toward what they want, and should bear weight on their legs and pull toward standing when supported. Mention it to your doctor if your baby isn't moving to explore at all, can't sit steadily, or doesn't take weight on their legs / doesn't try to pull up when you help them. The CPS advice is to raise it if your child seems behind in more than one area, and the AAP flags a baby not sitting on their own well past nine months as worth a conversation. Ranges are wide and premature babies are measured by their adjusted age, so this is a 'bring it up,' not a panic."

A baby who seems not to hear you, or who has stopped making sounds they used to make, deserves a hearing check. When in doubt, say something sooner rather than later."

That means babbling that stops, waving or clapping that disappears, or a baby who was social and engaged pulling away. This isn't about a temporary off-week during teething or illness; it's a real, sustained loss of a skill, and it's always worth a prompt call — trust your gut, because you know your baby's baseline better than anyone."

  - title: "How you're doing counts too"

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Clinginess and stranger wariness are a good sign"
    detail: "If your once-happy-with-anyone baby now clings, cries when you leave the room, and eyes strangers with suspicion, that's not a step backward — it's their new understanding that you're a specific, permanent person who continues to exist when you walk away. Nemours lists being shy, clingy, or fearful of strangers, and crying when caregivers leave, as expected social behaviour for this stage. It reflects a strong, healthy attachment to you plus the cognitive leap of object permanence. Ease goodbyes with a quick, cheerful, consistent routine rather than sneaking off, and give a wary baby time to warm up to new faces on their own terms."

  - title: "New fears are a sign the brain is getting smarter"
    detail: "Around now some babies develop sudden fears — of the vacuum, the blender, a barking dog, the bath drain — after happily ignoring those things for months. It's counterintuitive, but the fear is a sign of growing intelligence: as memory and understanding sharpen, a baby starts connecting 'loud and sudden' with 'possibly dangerous,' where before they simply hadn't made the link. Don't force them nose-to-nozzle with the scary thing to 'get over it' — that tends to dial the fear up. Acknowledge it calmly, keep your own reaction relaxed (they read your face), and let them approach on their own timeline; most of these fears fade as quickly as they arrived."

  - title: "They understand 'no' — so childproof more, say 'no' less"
    detail: "Your baby now genuinely understands 'no,' which is a milestone — and also the start of testing where the limits are, sometimes with a grin as they reach for the forbidden outlet again. The realistic strategy isn't a running commentary of 'no's, which just becomes background noise; it's to childproof thoroughly so the house is a yes-space, and save a firm 'no' for the few things that truly matter. The AAP's whole message about this age is to make the environment safe enough that you're not policing constantly — latch the cabinets, block the stairs, move the hazards, and then let them explore. Redirect to something allowed rather than only forbidding, and keep your tone matter-of-fact."
