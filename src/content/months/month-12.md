title: "One year — cruising, first words, and the graduation to whole milk"
description: "The first birthday: pulling up and cruising with the first wobbly steps close by, real words like 'mama' and 'dada' with a gesture or two, the big Alberta 12-month checkup and shots, and the graduation from formula and the bottle to whole cow's milk in a cup."
milestones:
  - title: "Pulling up, cruising, and maybe the first wobbly steps"
    detail: "Most babies this age pull to stand and cruise — sidestepping along the couch and coffee table with their hands for balance — and some let go to stand alone for a second or take a first shaky step or two. It's tempting to treat walking-by-one as a deadline, but it isn't: first steps land anywhere across roughly 9 to 15 months, and the majority of babies are still happily crawling and cruising into their second year, with most walking well by 18 months. When a baby walks says nothing about how well they'll walk later, so give plenty of barefoot floor time to practice and resist comparing — bare feet grip better than slippery socks. Preemies are measured from their adjusted age, not the calendar."

  - title: "A neat pincer grasp and the great drop-and-fetch game"
    detail: "Hands are precision instruments now: the pincer grasp — thumb against forefinger — lets your baby pick up a single pea or piece of cereal, though at exactly twelve months it's still a little clumsy and gets smoother over the coming weeks. Expect a lot of putting objects into a container and dumping them back out, banging two things together, and — the new favourite — deliberately dropping food off the high chair to watch it fall and wait for you to fetch it. That maddening drop-and-fetch loop isn't defiance; letting go on purpose is a genuine new skill, and each drop is a tiny physics experiment. A couple of stackable blocks or a bin to fill and empty will keep those busy fingers going for a surprisingly long time."

  - title: "First real words — plus a lot of expressive jargon"
    detail: "Around the first birthday many babies say 'mama' and 'dada' with meaning and maybe one or two other words, but far more of their talk is jargon: strings of babble that carry the rhythm, tone, and rise-and-fall of real sentences without any actual words yet. They understand much more than they can say — most follow a simple one-step request like 'give me the ball,' especially paired with a gesture, and grasp 'no.' Answer every sound with words of your own and narrate the day out loud; hearing language is exactly how they build it. A baby with only one word, or none quite yet, is still well within normal at twelve months — comprehension comes before speech."

  - title: "Waving, pointing, and shaking their head"
    detail: "Gestures are early language, and this is the month they take off: waving bye-bye, pointing at what they want or find interesting, shaking their head, and copying the little motions you make while you talk. Pointing in particular is a big one — it means your baby wants to share attention with you ('look at that!'), which is the social engine underneath talking. Name whatever they point to and wave back every time; you're closing the loop that turns a gesture into a word. Using gestures and simple signs doesn't slow speech down — it does the opposite."

  - title: "Object permanence: they look for what you hide"
    detail: "Your baby now knows a thing still exists when it's out of sight, so hide a toy under a cloth and they'll pull the cloth away to find it, and games like peekaboo and pat-a-cake are riotously funny because they can anticipate what comes next. This is also why they tirelessly drop, roll, throw, and submerge objects — each one is a small study of how the world behaves. It's the same cognitive leap, though, that fuels separation anxiety: they now understand you've gone somewhere when you leave the room. Play hiding games and narrate 'I'll be right back' when you step away — you're teaching them that things (and you) come back."

  - title: "Stranger wariness and separation anxiety at their peak"
    detail: "The baby who once beamed at everyone may now bury their face in your shoulder around new people and dissolve into tears when you leave — even for the next room. This is a developmental milestone, not a step backward or a sign you've spoiled anyone: stranger anxiety and separation anxiety are normal and typically peak somewhere between about 10 and 18 months. The clinginess is a sign of a healthy, secure attachment, and it eases with time and gentle, predictable goodbyes. Keep departures short and warm rather than sneaking off — a quick, confident 'bye, back soon' teaches them the pattern faster than a drawn-out one."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Read together every single day"
    detail: "Nothing you can buy beats a lapful of board books read out loud — the more stories a baby hears, the more words they bank for later, and at this age they'll start handing you a favourite to read again (and again). Name what's on the page, point to pictures and let them point too, and don't worry about finishing; a one-year-old 'reads' by grabbing, chewing, and flipping back to the same page. Chunky board and cloth books they can handle themselves make it interactive rather than a performance. Fold it into the bedtime routine and it does double duty as a wind-down cue."

  - title: "Containers to fill and dump, and blocks to stack"
    detail: "The toys that earn their keep now are the simplest: a bin or box and some blocks to drop in, dump out, and drop in again, plus nesting cups and stacking rings. Filling and emptying is exactly the fine-motor and cause-and-effect work your baby is driven to do this month, and knocking down a tower you've built together is peak entertainment. You don't need a playroom's worth — one or two open-ended toys beat a bin of flashing plastic, since a one-year-old can really only focus on one thing at a time. Rotate a few in and out to keep old favourites feeling new."

  - title: "Push toys for a new walker — and skip the baby walker"
    detail: "A weighted push toy or a sturdy toy shopping cart is perfect for a baby just finding their feet: it gives cruisers something to hold and march behind, building the balance and leg strength that lead to independent steps. Give lots of open floor space to pull up, cruise, and let go, and roll a ball back and forth to get them moving toward a target. One important safety note: skip the seated baby walker on wheels — paediatric groups advise against them because they roll toward stairs and hot stoves and don't actually help babies learn to walk. A stationary activity centre or plain floor time is the safer, better choice."

  - title: "Talk through the day and encourage those first words"
    detail: "Your voice is the single best language tool your baby has, so narrate what you're doing — 'we're putting on your blue socks,' 'here comes the banana' — and leave pauses for them to babble back. Name objects they point at, add the word to every gesture, and repeat back their attempts ('yes, dog!') so they hear the grown-up version. Simple baby signs for 'more,' 'milk,' and 'all done' can cut frustration in the gap before speech and don't hold talking back. This everyday back-and-forth matters far more than any app or program."

  - title: "Screens can wait — you're the better toy"
    detail: "It's a genuinely tempting age to hand over a phone while you make dinner, but the guidance is to skip screen media (video chatting with family aside) before about 18 months, because a one-year-old can't follow what's on a screen and it displaces the human back-and-forth their brain is built to learn from. As the AAP puts it, your voice, books, and objects your baby can grab are always more effective teaching tools than screen time. If a screen does happen — and sometimes it will — keep it short and watch alongside them, talking about what you both see. The habit is easier to keep small now than to shrink later."

feeding:
  - title: "The big switch: whole cow's milk at the first birthday"
    detail: "Once your baby turns one, homogenized whole (3.25% M.F.) cow's milk can become their main milk drink — Canadian guidance allows introducing it any time from about 9 to 12 months if breast milk is no longer available, and the first birthday is the classic moment to move off formula. Choose whole 3.25%, not skim, 1%, or 2%: lower-fat milks are too low in energy for a child under two and can slow growth, so save them for after age two. Keep it to roughly 500–750 mL (about 2–3 cups) a day at most — more than that fills a small tummy, crowds out solid food, and is a leading cause of iron-deficiency anemia in toddlers. If you're breastfeeding, there's no need to switch to cow's milk at all; nursing can carry on to two years and beyond alongside family food."

  - title: "Weaning from the bottle to a cup"
    detail: "Twelve months is the moment to start retiring the bottle. Offer milk and water in an open or straw cup rather than a bottle — breastfed babies pick up cup drinking easily, and the aim is to finish the bottle-to-cup transition somewhere between about 12 and 18 months, with an open cup the goal by age two. Weaning off the bottle protects growing teeth (milk pooling in a bottle, especially at night, feeds decay) and keeps milk from displacing food. Go gradually: drop one bottle at a time, start with the daytime ones, and keep any lingering nursing or comfort at bedtime for last."

  - title: "Honey is finally on the menu"
    detail: "After the first birthday, honey is safe — the ban before age one is about infant botulism, and by twelve months a child's gut has developed the helpful bacteria that guard against the Clostridium botulinum spores honey can carry. Before this milestone, honey of any kind (raw, pasteurized, or baked into food) was off-limits; now a drizzle is fine for a healthy one-year-old. It's a small graduation, but a real one. As always, it's a sweet treat, not a staple."

  - title: "Family food, self-feeding, and a shrinking appetite"
    detail: "Solids are now the main event, not the sideshow: aim for three meals plus a couple of snacks of the same food the family eats, chopped soft and small, and let your baby feed themselves with fingers (and a practice spoon) even though it's spectacularly messy. Keep offering iron-rich foods — meat, well-cooked egg, tofu, beans and lentils, iron-fortified cereal — a couple of times a day, since iron needs stay high through the second year. Don't be alarmed when their once-bottomless appetite shrinks around now: growth slows sharply in the second year, toddler portions are genuinely tiny (think tablespoons), and a busy new walker would rather move than sit. Your job is to offer good food on a schedule; how much they eat is theirs — pressure and 'one more bite' backfire."

  - title: "Choking foods still need prep — and keep the vitamin D going"
    detail: "A mouthful of teeth and new pincer skills don't change the choking rules: keep avoiding whole nuts, popcorn, hard or gummy candy, and raw hard vegetables, and make the classic round hazards safe by slicing grapes and hot dogs lengthwise and cooking firm fruit and veg until soft. Spread nut butters thinly rather than serving by the spoonful, and always stay with your baby while they eat. If you're breastfeeding or partly breastfeeding, keep giving the daily 400 IU vitamin D supplement — breast milk alone doesn't supply enough, and Canadian guidance continues it well past the first year."

sleep:
  - title: "Most nights are (finally) sleeping through"
    detail: "By the first birthday a child needs roughly 11 to 14 hours of sleep across a full day, and most are sleeping through the night — though 'most' isn't 'all,' and teething, illness, or a leap in mobility can scramble a good sleeper for a stretch. Separation anxiety can show up at bedtime too, as your baby now knows you're still out there when they're left in the crib. A consistent, calm wind-down — bath, PJs, a book or two, lights low — remains the most reliable sleep tool you have. If night waking flares, it's usually a phase riding on top of all this development, not a permanent slide."

  - title: "One or two naps, in transition"
    detail: "Around a year most babies are down to two naps — a morning and an afternoon one — after dropping the third somewhere in the second half of the first year. The move to a single nap usually comes later, well into the second year, so if your one-year-old still takes two, that's exactly on track. Watch the baby, not the clock: fighting the morning nap for several days running is often the first hint they're ready to consolidate, but there's no rush. Total daytime sleep of a couple of hours, however it's split, is the thing to aim for."

  - title: "Safe-sleep basics still hold — pillow and blanket can wait"
    detail: "Even with a birthday and a mouthful of teeth, the crib stays a firm, flat, bare surface: back to sleep, and no pillow, quilt, comforter, or bumpers yet. It's tempting to cozy things up now that the highest-risk SIDS window has passed, but most experts still see no reason to add a pillow, and a loose blanket is a tangle-and-fall hazard for a baby who now stands and cruises in the crib. Dress them for the room's temperature — a sleep sack or footed pajamas over a lighter layer — instead of covers. A small, breathable comfort lovey is generally fine, but the pillow-and-duvet upgrade belongs later in the second year, once they're in a bed."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Babyproof for a climber, not just a crawler"
    detail: "A pulling-up, cruising baby can reach a whole new shelf of the world, so re-scan your home at their eye level. Put secured gates with vertical metal or wood bars at the top of stairs, fixed to wall studs, and anchor top-heavy furniture — dressers, bookcases, and especially the TV — to the wall, because tip-overs are a leading cause of serious injury once babies climb. Latch low cabinets, cover outlets, and move anything small enough to choke on up out of reach. Get down on your hands and knees and crawl a lap; you'll spot cords, coins, and corners you'd never notice standing up."

  - title: "Water is a one-second danger — never leave them alone in it"
    detail: "A curious one-year-old can drown in as little as 2.5 cm (one inch) of water, silently and in the time it takes to answer the door, so never leave your baby alone in the bath, a wading pool, or near any water even for a moment, and empty tubs, buckets, and basins right after use. In and around water, keep a toddler within arm's reach — close enough to touch, not just to watch. Bath seats and rings give a false sense of security and don't prevent drowning; your hands do. Around backyard pools and hot tubs, four-sided fencing with a self-latching gate is the single most protective step."

  - title: "Lock up poisons — and keep the poison line handy"
    detail: "Medicines, cleaning supplies, laundry pods, cosmetics, and any corrosive products belong up high, out of sight, in their original containers, and ideally behind a locked latch — a walking baby will find, open, and taste things faster than you'd believe. In the kitchen, turn pot handles toward the back of the stove and use a cup with a tight-fitting lid for hot drinks, since scald burns from tipped mugs and pots are common at this age. Secure blind and curtain cords out of reach, too. healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "The one-year well-child visit"
    detail: "The 12-month checkup is a milestone in itself: length, weight, and head circumference measured and plotted on the growth chart, a head-to-toe physical exam, and a review of development, feeding, sleep, and safety. In Alberta this visit happens with a public health nurse at your local community health or public health centre, where the routine immunizations are also given. Expect the nurse or doctor to watch your baby in action — pulling to stand, picking up a small object, waving, babbling — and to ask what you're seeing at home; a hemoglobin (iron) check is sometimes done, especially if milk intake has been high. Bring your baby's Alberta health card and a written list of questions, because they evaporate the moment you're in the room."

  - title: "Alberta's 12-month vaccines"
    detail: "At the one-year visit Alberta's routine schedule gives three vaccines: MMR-Var (a single combined shot covering measles, mumps, rubella, and varicella/chickenpox), the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine, and the meningococcal conjugate vaccine (MenconC). These are milestone doses — the pneumococcal and meningococcal shots complete or boost earlier series, and MMR-Var is the first measles-family dose, which is why it waits until the immune system is ready at a year. They're free and given by public health nurses at the well-child clinic. A day or two of low-grade fever, fussiness, or a sore leg is a common, expected reaction; note that the MMR-Var can occasionally cause a mild fever or faint rash about 7 to 12 days later."

  - title: "Tripled birth weight, and a slowing curve"
    detail: "Most babies have roughly tripled their birth weight by the first birthday — an astonishing year of growth. What comes next is a deliberate slowdown: through the second year a child adds far less weight and height, which is precisely why appetite so often drops around now. As long as your baby is following their own curve, a smaller eater is a normal eater, not a worry. The growth chart, not any single meal, is what tells the real story."

  - title: "Time for the first dental visit"
    detail: "The Canadian recommendation is a first visit to a dental professional within six months of the first tooth appearing, or by 12 months of age at the latest — so the first birthday is a natural prompt to book it if you haven't. At home, brush those new teeth once a day (bedtime is ideal) with a soft baby brush and a smear of fluoride toothpaste, and skip letting your baby fall asleep with a bottle of milk, which pools on the teeth and causes decay. Weaning off the bottle around now helps here too. Even a mouthful of just a few teeth is worth that first checkup."

redFlags:
  - title: "Movement: not standing, cruising, or using a pincer grasp"
    detail: "Mention it to your doctor or public-health nurse if by 12 months your baby doesn't pull up to stand, can't bear weight or stand when you support them, doesn't cruise or move along furniture, or can't yet pick up small objects between thumb and finger. These are conversation-starters for the one-year visit, not a scorecard — ranges are wide and preemies are assessed on their adjusted age — but they're worth raising rather than waiting. Bringing them up early is exactly what the visit is for, and most of the time you'll simply be reassured."

  - title: "Check in on your own well-being, too"
    detail: "A full year in is a good moment to be honest about how you're doing.
