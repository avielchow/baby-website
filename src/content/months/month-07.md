monthNumber: 7
title: "Month 7 — sitting steady and getting into everything"
description: "The big shift this month is a baby who can sit and look around, rake up anything within reach, and babble long chains of sound — which is exactly why the small-object sweep and the babyproofing start now."
milestones:
  - title: "Sitting steadily — often without a hand down"
    detail: "Most babies are sitting well by now: propped upright, they hold the position without leaning forward on their arms, and many can sit hands-free for stretches, freeing both hands to play. The AAP frames the seventh month as sitting 'with, and then without, support of her hands,' and rolling reliably in both directions. There's a very wide normal window — some babies sit at 5 months, others closer to 8 or 9 — so this is a range, not a deadline, and premature babies are measured by their adjusted age. Give lots of floor time to practise; the milestone checklist is a conversation-starter for your provider, not a scorecard."

  - title: "Babbling in chains, and answering to their name"
    detail: "Language takes an audible leap: babbling turns into repeated chains of consonants — 'bababa,' 'dadada,' 'mamama' — and babies start responding to their own name, reading the emotion in your tone of voice, and beginning to react to 'no.' The AAP lists babbling chains of consonants, responding to own name, and distinguishing emotions by tone among the seventh-month language milestones. These sounds aren't words yet, but they're the raw material of them, so answer every babble back and leave a pause for the reply. A baby who coos and takes turns with sound is doing exactly the right work."

  - title: "Stranger wariness may start to flicker"
    detail: "The easy baby who beamed at everyone may start to tense up, go quiet, or cling when an unfamiliar face gets close — the first hints of stranger anxiety, which usually builds over the next few months. The AAP explains this happens because a baby is now, for the first time, able to tell familiar from unfamiliar, and that wanting you is a sign of secure attachment, not a step backward. Let new people approach slowly and on your baby's terms rather than swooping in. If it hasn't shown up yet, that's fine too — the timing varies a lot."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Cause and effect is the whole game right now"
    detail: "Banging, dropping, and shaking aren't random — your baby is running tiny experiments about what makes noise, what falls, and what happens when they act on the world. Nemours suggests offering toys that move or make a sound when your baby plays with them, which rewards that budding sense of 'I did that.' Simple wins beat busy plastic light-up panels: a cup to bang, a ball that rolls just out of reach, nesting or stacking cups (they'll knock them down long before they build). Narrate what happens — 'you dropped it!' — so the action and the words arrive together."

  - title: "Peekaboo, and the dawn of object permanence"
    detail: "This is the month peekaboo becomes hilarious, because your baby is starting to grasp that things still exist when they're out of sight — the AAP notes seventh-month babies can find a partially hidden object. Hide a toy under a cloth with a corner poking out and let them pull it free; hide your face and pop back. That 'you disappeared and came back' loop is the same reassurance that underlies separation anxiety later, so playing it is genuinely useful, not just fun. Repeat it as many times as they demand — repetition is how the lesson sticks."

  - title: "Read, name, and talk in turns"
    detail: "Even the simplest board book is a pile of new words, so open one often, point to single objects, and name them — 'dog,' 'ball,' 'nose' — and make the sounds. Nemours recommends talking to your baby and responding to the sounds they make, which teaches them to be social and take turns in conversation. Chunky, sturdy books with faces and textures survive gumming and hold attention. You don't need a script: describe what you're doing as you do it, and treat every babble as their half of the chat."

  - title: "Texture is a toy"
    detail: "Reaching for and inspecting different objects is how babies learn about touch, shape, and texture, so a basket of safe household bits — a wooden spoon, a silicone lid, a crinkly cloth, a cold metal cup — often out-plays anything from the toy aisle. Rotate a small handful at a time; two or three things is plenty, and more just overwhelms at this age. Keep everything bigger than the toilet-paper-roll test, because it's all going straight to the mouth. Supervised 'container play' — filling and dumping a bin — will entertain them for surprisingly long stretches soon."

feeding:
  - title: "Milk is still the main event"
    detail: "Solids are getting real, but breast milk or formula still supplies most of your baby's nutrition and calories this month — food is added around it, not instead of it. Formula-fed babies typically take about 6 to 8 oz (180–240 ml) at each of 4 or 5 feedings, and should average no more than about 32 oz (960 ml) in 24 hours; breastfed babies keep nursing several times a day. As solids ramp up over the coming months the milk will gradually taper, but not yet. Let appetite lead — a baby who wants a little more or less at a given feed is self-regulating."

  - title: "More textures, and the first soft finger foods"
    detail: "If you started around 6 months, this is the month to move past thin purée toward lumpier, mashed, and minced textures and soft finger foods your baby can pick up — the CPS specifically recommends offering a variety of textures plus soft finger foods, adding more texture as baby gets older. Around 7 to 9 months babies want to feed themselves, so hand over soft strips they can grip: well-cooked vegetable sticks, ripe banana, avocado, soft-cooked egg, flaked fish. Advancing texture on schedule actually matters — staying on smooth purée too long can make lumps harder to accept later. Our Starting Solids guide covers readiness, first foods, and safe sizes in full."

  - title: "Offer water in an open cup with meals"
    detail: "Once solids are underway, mealtimes are the moment to introduce a small open cup with a little water — the CPS advises offering water occasionally in an open cup, and the AAP suggests an open, sippy, or straw cup with water capped at about 1 cup (8 oz) a day. Sipping from a rim (or a straw) builds different mouth muscles than a bottle and is worth starting early even though it's messy; expect most of it to end up down the chin at first. A tiny weighted or short open cup, or a straw cup, both work — help guide it to the lips at first. Save juice: water and milk are all a baby this age needs to drink."

  - title: "Keep iron front and centre — and keep offering the allergens"
    detail: "A baby's iron stores from birth are running low by now, so iron-rich foods should be on the plate at least twice a day: meat, poultry, cooked whole egg, fish, tofu, well-cooked beans and lentils, or iron-fortified infant cereal. Keep offering the common allergenic foods you've introduced — egg, peanut, dairy, wheat, soy, fish — regularly rather than once and stopping, since consistent early exposure is what helps prevent allergies. Both threads (iron and allergens) are covered in depth in the Starting Solids guide, so this month is really about keeping them going as textures expand. No honey until after the first birthday, ever — it carries a botulism risk for babies."

  - title: "Gagging is loud and normal; choking is silent"
    detail: "As lumps and finger foods arrive, gagging will happen — and it's a protective reflex, not an emergency: a gagging baby coughs, sputters, goes red, and pushes the food forward, handling it themselves, so the move is to stay calm and let them work. Choking is the opposite — silent, with no cough or cry — and needs immediate action. Cut round or firm foods to break the shape (grapes quartered lengthwise, no whole nuts, no hot-dog coins, no popcorn or hard raw carrot), always feed with your baby upright and buckled in the high chair, and never leave them alone with food. Take an infant-CPR/choking refresher so the response is muscle memory."

sleep:
  - title: "About 12–16 hours, settling into two naps"
    detail: "Most babies this age sleep roughly 12–16 hours across the full day, including a longer stretch at night and at least two daytime naps — usually a morning and an afternoon one — totalling around 3–4 hours of day sleep. By 6 months most babies can sleep about 9 hours or longer at night with only brief wake-ups. Watch your baby's tired cues (eye-rubbing, yawning, fussing) rather than forcing the clock, and keep the wind-down predictable. Naps often consolidate from three down to two around now, which can shuffle the schedule for a week or two."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "Night wake-ups usually aren't hunger anymore"
    detail: "Plenty of babies still surface at night, but by this age most no longer need a middle-of-the-night feeding, and the waking is often about learning to resettle rather than being hungry. Nemours suggests giving your baby a few fussy minutes before you respond, and putting them down drowsy but still awake so they practise falling asleep without your help. If you choose to work on this, a consistent bedtime routine — bath, book, feed, bed, same order every night — does more than any single technique. Breastfed babies and those in the middle of a developmental leap may still wake more, and that's within normal."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "The safe-sleep rules don't relax"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, on a firm flat surface, in their own crib or bassinet, with nothing loose in it — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners — stays the rule right through the first year, even now that your baby can roll and sit. Room-sharing without bed-sharing is recommended for at least the first 6 months. A baby who can roll both ways will find their own sleep position, and you don't need to flip them back, but you still always place them down on the back. If they fall asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move them to the flat crib when you reasonably can."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Do the small-object sweep — get down to their level"
    detail: "A baby who rakes, sits, and mouths everything, and is about to become mobile, turns the whole floor into a hazard field. The CPS rule is simple: if an item fits through an empty toilet-paper roll, it's a choking hazard — coins, buttons, beads, pen caps, bits of dry food, older siblings' small toys. Get down on hands and knees for a baby's-eye view and clear the sightlines under couches, in corners, and along baseboards. Watch especially for button batteries and small magnets, which the AAP flags as causing severe internal injury if swallowed — treat any suspected ingestion as an emergency."

  - title: "High-chair harness, every single time"
    detail: "Now that your baby sits and pushes, the high chair becomes a fall risk: always fasten the crotch and waist straps, even for a quick meal and even if they seem too little to climb out, and the AAP recommends keeping your baby buckled in the high chair when you can't have hands on them. Get everything you need — food, cup, cloth — before you sit down so you never have to step away from a strapped-in baby who could still lunge or tip. Keep the chair clear of tables, counters, and walls they could push off from. Never rely on the tray alone to hold them in."

  - title: "Hot drinks, cords, and stove out of reach"
    detail: "A grabbing, reaching baby changes how you handle anything hot or dangling. The CPS advises never carrying your baby and a hot drink at the same time and using a lidded cup for hot liquids; the AAP adds never leaving coffee near a table or counter edge. Blind and curtain cords are a strangulation hazard — secure them to the wall or well out of reach, and keep the crib away from any cords. At the stove, keep your baby well back and turn pot handles inward; set your water heater no higher than about 49°C (120°F) to prevent tap-water scalds."

  - title: "Start the real babyproofing now, before they crawl"
    detail: "Independent movement is weeks away, so childproof ahead of it rather than after the first close call. Anchor top-heavy furniture and TVs, dressers and bookcases to the wall (tip-overs are a leading cause of serious injury); put hardware-mounted gates at the top of stairs — pressure-mounted gates aren't safe there; cover outlets; and add latches to cabinets holding cleaners, medicines, or anything hazardous, storing them up and locked in original containers. Skip baby walkers entirely — the AAP warns they raise the risk of falls, especially down stairs. Doing this now means you're not scrambling the day your baby figures out crawling."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "No routine visit or shots this month in Alberta"
    detail: "Alberta's routine immunization visits land at 2, 4, 6, and 12 months, so there's no vaccine at 7 months — the next shots come at the first birthday, and the next routine well-child check is around nine months. That doesn't mean you have to wait: you can book with your family doctor or public health any time a question or worry comes up, and a mid-year check is reasonable if you'd like your baby weighed or your list answered. One exception worth noting — the influenza vaccine is offered yearly from 6 months of age, so if flu season is starting, ask about it. Keep the Alberta health card handy for any visit."
    sources:
      - { label: "Alberta", url: "https://www.alberta.ca/immunization-routine-schedule" }
  - title: "Growth keeps climbing, and first teeth may arrive"
    detail: "Between visits your baby keeps gaining steadily on their own curve; you don't need a scale at home, but if you're ever unsure about weight gain or intake, that's a good reason to book a weigh-in. First teeth commonly break through around now — the CPS notes the first tooth usually appears about 6 months, but anywhere from 3 to 12 months is normal, and the two bottom middle teeth usually lead. As soon as a tooth is through, clean it once a day with a soft baby toothbrush, and plan a first dental visit within 6 months of that first tooth or by 12 months at the latest."

redFlags:
  - title: "Not sitting with support, or not bearing weight on the legs"
    detail: "Bring it up with your provider if by now your baby can't sit even with support, doesn't take any weight on their legs when held upright, seems very stiff or very floppy, or has poor head control — the AAP lists these among the developmental watch signs for this age. Reaching with only one hand consistently, or never reaching for things, is also worth mentioning. None of these is a diagnosis; they're prompts for a conversation, and ranges are wide (premature babies are assessed by adjusted age). Raising it early just means any support that helps starts sooner."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Developmental-Milestones-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "No babbling, no back-and-forth, or no response to their name"
    detail: "Flag it if your baby isn't babbling at all (the AAP notes absence of babbling by about 8 months is worth a check), doesn't turn or respond to their own name, doesn't make back-and-forth sounds or facial expressions with you, or doesn't seem to react to your tone or to sounds around them. Because hearing drives speech, a baby who doesn't respond to sound deserves a hearing check. Also mention any loss of a skill your baby previously had — babies gain skills, they don't lose them, so a regression always warrants a call. These are conversation-starters for your provider, not a verdict."

Health Link 811 is available any hour to help you decide, and you know your baby's baseline better than anyone — 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate reason to reach out. Anything pointing to a swallowed button battery, magnet, or small object, or a real choking event, is an emergency — call 911."

It also tracks with growing attachment — the same development behind stranger wariness this month. Reconnect for a few unhurried minutes before you dive into dinner or laundry, and try not to take the crankiness personally."

  - title: "Teething: real, but blamed for more than it causes"
    detail: "Sore, swollen gums, extra drool, and a baby who wants to chew everything are the honest signs of teething. Soothe it simply: rub the gums with a clean finger, or offer a firm rubber teething ring or a facecloth chilled in the freezer for about 30 minutes. Skip the numbing gels — the CPS advises against rubbing anesthetic gels on the gums — and skip amber teething necklaces, which are a strangulation and choking risk. A high fever, diarrhea, or a genuinely sick baby isn't 'just teething' and should be checked rather than waved off."

You don't need to push your baby to be held by anyone; let people say hi from a little distance and warm up on your baby's clock. It tends to build over the coming months and eases in the second year. Meanwhile, peekaboo and other 'gone and back' games quietly help your baby learn that people who leave come back."
