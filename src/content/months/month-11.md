title: "Month 11 — cruising, first words, and a baby on the move"
description: "Confident cruising, standing alone for a second or two, maybe a first wobbly step; first words with real meaning, pointing and waving; and a climber who needs the furniture bolted down. Babyproofing catches up to mobility this month."
milestones:
  - title: "Cruising with confidence — and standing alone for a second or two"
    detail: "By now most babies pull to stand easily and cruise — taking sideways steps while holding the couch, coffee table, or your hands for balance — which is exactly how they rehearse the balance and leg strength that walking needs. Some will let go and stand alone for a moment before plopping down, and a few are getting steady enough to stand unsupported for several seconds. The AAP describes babies this age pulling to a standing position 'every chance she gets' and cruising 'alongside furniture,' then trying 'tentative steps' once standing feels secure. Give her low, sturdy, stable things to cruise along (nothing on wheels, nothing that tips) and let her practise on bare feet, which grip better than socks and let her feel where she is."

  - title: "First independent steps — for some, not most, and that's fine"
    detail: "A handful of babies take their first solo steps in the eleventh month, but the normal range for walking is genuinely wide — roughly 9 to 15 months, with the average around a year — so a baby who is cruising happily but not yet walking is right on track, and one who walks early isn't ahead in any race. Walking usually arrives in a predictable order (pulling up, cruising, standing alone, then those first drunken-sailor steps), and once a baby lets go, most go from a few teetering steps to confident walking within days or a couple of weeks. Pushing or drilling it doesn't speed it up; the fastest route is lots of floor time and reasons to stand, like a favourite toy set just out of reach on the sofa. Expect far more sitting-down-with-a-thud than striding at this stage — that's the work, not a setback."

    detail: "Standing is no longer the only trick — many babies this month learn to stoop or squat to snatch a toy off the floor and then push back up to standing, and to lower themselves down from a stand instead of just toppling. That controlled bending and rising is real strength-and-balance work, and it's a good sign the leg and trunk muscles are getting ready for walking. You'll notice her deliberately squatting to inspect something, then rising with it clutched in a fist. Clear a safe patch of floor so all this up-and-down has somewhere to happen without a coffee-table corner in the way."

  - title: "A precise pincer grasp — and everything goes in and out of containers"
    detail: "The fine-motor headline is the pincer grasp: picking up small things neatly between thumb and pointer finger, a big step up from the whole-hand rake of a few months ago and the same skill that lets her feed herself peas and puffs. She'll also poke at things with one index finger and become obsessed with putting objects into a container and taking them out again, dropping, rolling, and dumping to figure out how the world behaves. The AAP notes babies this age deliberately drop and 'submerge' objects to learn cause and effect, and start realizing things have names and uses. Offer a cup or bowl and a few safe blocks — the in-out game will occupy her far longer than any flashy toy, and it's genuinely teaching her."

  - title: "First words with meaning — and a lot more understood than said"
    detail: "Around the first birthday some babies produce a first real word or two — often 'mama' or 'dada' aimed at the right parent, plus maybe 'bye-bye' or the name of a favourite thing — used consistently to mean that person or object. But comprehension races well ahead of speech: long before she talks, she understands simple requests ('go find the ball,' 'wave bye-bye'), so a baby with no clear words yet is not behind if she clearly follows what you say and babbles with the melody of real speech. The range here is very wide — some have two or three words by one year, others are still all expressive gibberish, and both are normal. Answer her sounds, name what she looks at, and read together; the input you pour in now is what her first words are built from."

  - title: "Gesturing, pointing, and the great social-game era"
    detail: "Even without words, your baby is communicating capably — pointing at what she wants, waving bye-bye, lifting her arms to be picked up, shaking her head 'no,' and copying your gestures hard because imitation is her favourite way to learn. This is peak peekaboo, pat-a-cake, and so-big season, and she now grasps object permanence — that you and her toys still exist when out of sight — which is why finding a hidden toy under a cloth delights her and why she'll hunt for it. Play the games back, narrate the pointing ('yes, that's the dog!'), and take turns; the give-and-take of these little rituals is early conversation. Clinginess and separation fuss are the flip side of the same milestone — she now knows you can leave, which is a sign of healthy attachment, not a step backward."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Feed the in-and-out obsession: containers, blocks, and dump-and-fill"
    detail: "The single most useful toy this month is a container and some things to put in it — a plastic bowl and a handful of blocks, a shape sorter, nesting cups, a muffin tin and some balls. Filling, dumping, stacking, and knocking down teaches cause and effect, spatial sense, and the pincer grasp all at once, and it holds a near-one-year-old's attention far better than a busy light-up gadget. Attention spans are stretching from a couple of minutes toward ten or fifteen with a single activity, so let her stay with something as long as she's absorbed. Narrate as she works — 'in… out… all gone!' — and you're feeding language while she plays."

  - title: "Read together — and let her point to the pictures"
    detail: "Looking at books becomes genuinely interactive now: she'll start recognizing familiar pictures and may point at them, which is the perfect cue for you to name what she's pointing at. Sturdy board books she can grab and turn, with big clear single images, beat long stories — expect her to want the same three pages on repeat, and read them anyway, because repetition is how the words stick. Ask 'where's the ball?' and give her time to look or point; that back-and-forth builds the link between objects and their names. A few minutes several times a day, on your lap, does more than one long sitting."

  - title: "Help her cruise toward walking (without pushing it)"
    detail: "The best walking practice is simply lots of supervised floor time with good reasons to stand and cruise: sturdy low furniture arranged so she can travel between handholds, a favourite toy placed just out of reach, and you offering two hands to walk toward. Push-along wagons or weighted toys she can lean on and shove are great once she's cruising, because she sets the pace and controls her own balance. Skip seated baby walkers entirely — Canadian and US authorities warn against them; they're a fall-and-tip-over hazard and don't help walking. Let her go barefoot indoors: bare feet grip better and let those little foot and ankle muscles develop and feel the floor, which is exactly what builds balance."

  - title: "Talk, imitate, and play the copycat games"
    detail: "She's a relentless imitator right now, so make yourself worth copying: talk through your day in short, clear sentences, pause for her to 'answer,' and turn everyday moments into call-and-response. Wave and say 'bye-bye,' blow kisses, clap, and she'll start copying — these gestures are real communication and often show up before spoken words. Songs with actions (pat-a-cake, itsy-bitsy spider), peekaboo, and naming body parts during a diaper change all pack in language and turn-taking. When she hands you a toy, take it, name it, and hand it back; that little loop is one of the earliest forms of conversation."

feeding:
  - title: "Three meals and a couple of snacks — mostly self-fed now"
    detail: "By the end of the first year most babies are eating roughly three meals plus one or two snacks a day, and with the new pincer grasp they can pick up small pieces and feed themselves. Offer soft finger foods cut small — foods that mash easily or melt in the mouth, in pea-to-fingertip-sized pieces — and expect mess, because self-feeding is a skill she practises by doing it (badly, at first). Let her drive: hand her the spoon to try even if most ends up on the floor, and follow her hunger and fullness cues rather than a target amount. Always have her sit in the high chair to eat, never eating while crawling, walking, or lying back, which is a choking risk."

  - title: "Breastmilk or formula is still the main milk — and cow's milk mostly waits for one"
    detail: "Breastmilk or formula still provides key nutrients and stays the main drink through the first year, though she'll naturally take less of it as solids fill more of her plate. Plain whole (homogenized, 3.25%) cow's milk isn't the everyday drink yet: US guidance says wait until 12 months, and Canadian guidance (CPS) allows introducing whole cow's milk between 9 and 12 months only if breastmilk is no longer available — never skim, 1%, or 2%, which don't have the fat a baby this age needs. Regular cow's milk given too early and in large amounts can crowd out iron and irritate the gut, so it shouldn't replace breastmilk or formula as the main milk before one. After the first birthday, cap cow's milk at about 500–750 mL (16–25 oz) a day so it doesn't blunt her appetite for iron-rich food."

  - title: "Keep working on the cup — and start easing off the bottle"
    detail: "This is a good stretch to give the open or straw cup real practice at meals, since drinking from a cup instead of a bottle is a skill that takes weeks of messy repetition to click. The AAP suggests offering a cup from around 6 months and aiming to have babies off the bottle sometime between 12 and 18 months, moving away from it gradually — bottles only at meals first, then dropping the nap and bedtime ones last. Weaning the bottle around a year is easier than fighting a strong-willed toddler over it later, and it protects new teeth from the constant milk-bathing that causes decay. If sippy cups are the bridge, use them only while she's learning and don't let her tote one around all day."

    detail: "Honey stays off the menu until after the first birthday because of the risk of infant botulism — that rule doesn't lift until 12 months. Keep cutting or avoiding the classic choking hazards: whole grapes and cherry tomatoes (quarter them), hot dog and sausage rounds, whole nuts, popcorn, hard raw carrot or apple chunks, and sticky globs of nut butter. Slice everything into thin, small pieces, and stay within arm's reach at every meal and snack. Snacks are useful little top-ups for a small stomach, but offer them sitting down and space them from meals so they don't wipe out her appetite."

sleep:
  - title: "Around 12–16 hours total, most of it at night, plus two naps"
    detail: "Babies this age need roughly 12 to 16 hours of sleep in a day, usually a long 9-to-12-hour night stretch plus two daytime naps (a morning and an afternoon one), each anywhere from about 30 minutes to two hours. Some start resisting the morning nap around now; if yours does, watch that total sleep doesn't slip too low, because an overtired baby actually sleeps worse. Nap lengths vary a lot from baby to baby and that's normal — a short napper who wakes happy and rested is getting what she needs. Keep days active and bright and evenings calm to protect the day-night rhythm."

  - title: "Separation anxiety can crash into the night"
    detail: "A baby who slept through for months may suddenly wake and cry at bedtime or overnight, clinging as you leave — this is separation anxiety, a normal developmental stage tied to her new grasp that you still exist when you walk out. The gentlest response is to check that nothing's wrong, then reassure quietly without making it a party: a calm word, a pat, and a quick exit, so she practises settling herself. Scooping her up for a cuddle, a feed, or a chat at every waking teaches her that waking earns that attention, which can lock in the pattern. A security object like a small blanket, a consistent wind-down, and a low night light can all take the edge off."

Aim to put her in the crib drowsy but still awake so she learns to fall asleep on her own, rather than only being able to drift off in your arms or with a bottle. Safe-sleep basics still hold: on her back to start, in her own crib with nothing loose in it. safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Anchor the furniture — the climbing has begun"
    detail: "A pulling-up, cruising baby is weeks away from climbing, and furniture tip-overs are a leading cause of serious injury at this age. Bolt dressers, bookcases, shelves, and any tall or top-heavy furniture to the wall with anti-tip brackets (metal 'L' brackets last; plastic can grow brittle and fail), and mount flat-screen TVs to the wall or strap them down — a modern TV weighs as much as a couple of bowling balls. Add drawer stops so she can't pull drawers out into a staircase, and don't perch tempting things (remotes, toys, your phone) up high, which just invites a climb. Anti-tip kits cost only a few dollars and take minutes; do it before she's tall enough to test the shelves."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/safety-prevention/at-home/Pages/Preventing-Furniture-and-TV-Tip-Overs.aspx" }
  - title: "Gate the stairs, guard the windows, ditch the walker"
    detail: "Put safety gates at the top and bottom of every staircase — the top gate should be a hardware-mounted one that screws to the wall, not a pressure gate that can give way. Install operable window guards on windows above the ground floor and never rely on a screen to hold a leaning baby, and keep cribs and climbable furniture away from windows. Skip seated baby walkers altogether; the AAP flatly says not to use one, because they send babies speeding into hazards and down stairs. Pad or remove sharp-cornered furniture from the spots where she cruises and falls most."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Safety-for-Your-Child-6-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Cords, blinds, and anything loopy around the neck"
    detail: "Cords from window blinds and curtains are a real strangulation hazard for babies and toddlers, who can get a loop around the neck in seconds — the safest fix is cordless coverings, and Canada now has the world's strictest cord regulations, but older blinds in your home won't be covered by them. Tie cords up out of reach with cleats or cut continuous loops, and move the crib, bed, and any climbable furniture away from windows and their cords. The same goes for anything else that can loop a neck: no necklaces, no soother clips left on in the crib, no long drawstrings on sleepwear. Do a walk-around at baby's eye level and remove the dangling temptations."

  - title: "Lock up the poisons, meds, and cleaners — up high AND latched"
    detail: "Keep all medicines, vitamins, cleaning products, laundry pods, cosmetics, and chemicals up, up, and away — completely out of sight and reach — and add safety latches to the cupboards she can open, because a climber will find the under-sink cabinet. Store things in their original containers, never in cups or bottles, and don't take medicine as 'candy' in front of her. In Alberta, program the Poison and Drug Information Service (PADIS) number, 1-800-332-1414, into your phone and stick it on the fridge; call them right away for any suspected poisoning, before she has symptoms. Keep an eye on button batteries and magnets too — both cause severe internal injury if swallowed and need emergency care."

  - title: "Water and hot things need a new level of vigilance"
    detail: "Never leave her alone in or near water for even a moment — a bath, a bucket, a wading pool, the toilet — because a baby can drown in a couple of inches, silently and fast. If you have a pool, fence it on all four sides now, before she's walking. Keep hot coffee and tea back from table and counter edges, turn pot handles inward on the stove, and check bathwater temperature, since a curious cruiser grabs and pulls at everything within reach. She has no sense of danger yet — close, constant supervision is still the single most important safety feature in your home."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "No routine shots this month — but book the one-year visit now"
    detail: "There are no routine immunizations scheduled at 11 months in Alberta: the schedule goes from the 6-month shots straight to the 12-month set, so this is a quiet month vaccine-wise and a perfect time to phone and book the one-year appointment before slots fill. At 12 months Alberta gives MMR-Var (measles, mumps, rubella, and varicella/chickenpox), the meningococcal conjugate (Men-C-C) vaccine, and a pneumococcal conjugate dose — several needles in one visit that open up protection against a new set of serious illnesses. Getting it on the calendar now means one less thing to chase at the busy first-birthday mark. Keep her Alberta health card handy for the visit."

  - title: "What the one-year checkup will cover — and why the growth curve may bend"
    detail: "The 12-month well-child visit typically includes weight, length, and head circumference plotted on the growth curve, a full physical, a development check, and sometimes a hemoglobin screen for iron, since iron needs are high in the second half of the first year. redFlags:
  - title: "Not bearing weight or getting mobile at all"
    detail: "Mention it at the next visit if, approaching a year, she can't bear weight on her legs when you hold her up, isn't pulling to stand, or has no way of getting herself around — no crawling, scooting, or cruising. Cruising-but-not-walking is completely normal at 11 months and not a flag; the concern is a baby who isn't finding any means of self-propulsion or won't put weight through her legs. Bring it up as a conversation, not a crisis — your doctor would rather hear it early. Remember premature babies are measured by their adjusted age, which can shift the timing by weeks or months."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Separation anxiety is peaking — and it's a good sign"
    detail: "The sudden clinging, the meltdown when you leave the room, the wariness of strangers — separation anxiety typically ramps up between about 10 and 18 months, and it's a milestone, not a regression. It means she now understands you still exist when you're gone (object permanence) and remembers that 'bye-bye' means you're leaving, but she can't yet grasp that you'll be back soon. Don't sneak out — slipping away while she's distracted backfires and makes her clingier; instead build a short, cheerful goodbye ritual and always follow through on returning. It passes, though it may take months, and handling it warmly now helps her weather bigger separations later."

  - title: "Bare feet beat shoes indoors — really"
    detail: "It's tempting to buy proper little shoes the moment she cruises, but barefoot is genuinely best for a baby learning to walk indoors: going shoeless lets the foot and ankle muscles develop, helps her feel the floor, and builds the balance that walking needs. Shoes are for protection outdoors — gravel, cold, other people's feet — not for teaching walking, and stiff hard-soled shoes can actually make learning harder. Socks or booties with non-skid soles are fine for chilly or slippery floors. When you do buy outdoor shoes (see the gear pick below), think soft, light, and flexible — as close to bare feet as possible."

    detail: "A new cruiser (and especially a new walker) will trip, thud onto her bottom, and bonk her head more times a day than you can count — it's how she learns, and misadventure won't deter her intrepid streak. Your job isn't to prevent every tumble but to make sure the falls are safe ones: soft landings, padded or removed sharp corners, gated stairs. How you react shapes hers, too — a calm 'oops, up you go' helps her shake off a minor spill, where gasps and panic cue tears even when it didn't really hurt.     why: "For most of this month the best footwear is none — barefoot indoors builds the balance and foot strength walking needs, and stiff shoes get in the way. Shoes are for protection outdoors once she's cruising or walking on rougher ground, so look for a true pre-walker: a soft, thin, genuinely flexible sole you can fold in half (so she can feel the ground and lift her feet), a wide foot-shaped toe box that doesn't squeeze her toes, lightweight breathable material like soft leather or cloth, and a secure velcro or elastic closure. Fit matters more than brand — leave about a finger's width (roughly 1–1.5 cm) between her longest toe and the shoe's end, measured standing, and re-check every 6–8 weeks because feet grow astonishingly fast at this age; it's better to have no shoes than shoes that are too tight. Skip anything with a stiff, thick, arch-supporting sole or a heel — those are built for older feet, not new walkers."
