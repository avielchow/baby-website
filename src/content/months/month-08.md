monthNumber: 8
title: "Month eight — on the move, so babyproof now"
description: "Eight months in, your baby is getting mobile — crawling or scooting, pulling up, babbling 'mamama,' and suddenly clingy about you. The headline job this month is racing that new mobility with real babyproofing."
milestones:
  - title: "Getting around — crawling, scooting, or commando-style"
    detail: "Somewhere in this stretch most babies find a way to move themselves across the floor, and the style genuinely doesn't matter: classic hands-and-knees crawling is usually mastered between about seven and ten months, but plenty of babies scoot on their bottoms, slither on their bellies (commando-crawling), roll, or crab sideways instead — and some skip crawling altogether and go straight to pulling up and cruising. What matters is that both sides of the body are working roughly equally and she's finding some way to explore. Give her lots of floor time and set a favourite toy just out of reach to invite the effort; more tummy and floor time is the single best thing you can do here."
    sources:

  - title: "Pulling to sit, and pulling up to stand"
    detail: "By now most babies sit steadily without support and free their hands to play, and off the floor they're starting to haul themselves upright — grabbing a crib rail, the couch, or your trouser leg and pulling to stand. Once they're up, many begin to cruise, shuffling along furniture while holding on. This is exactly why the crib mattress needs to be on its lowest setting and why anything she can pull up on has to be steady — a wobbly side table or an unanchored bookshelf becomes a handhold now. If she isn't sitting steadily on her own by nine months, mention it at the next visit."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-8-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "The pincer grasp is sharpening"
    detail: "Those chubby fingers are getting precise: over these months the clumsy full-hand rake refines into a true pincer grasp, picking things up neatly between the thumb and the first or second finger. It's the skill that lets her feed herself a single pea or a small piece of soft toast — and, less charmingly, spot and grab every crumb, bead, and bit of fluff on the floor. Offer safe, pea-to-chickpea-sized soft finger foods to practise on, and treat the new dexterity as your cue to sweep the floor for small objects, because whatever she pinches goes straight to her mouth."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Hand-and-Finger-Skills.aspx" }
  - title: "Separation anxiety and stranger wariness arrive"
    detail: "Right around now many babies become clingy in a brand-new way — bursting into tears when you leave the room and turning shy, wary, or upset when handed to someone unfamiliar, even a doting grandparent. This isn't a step backward or anything you caused; it's a genuine emotional milestone. She can finally tell the difference between you and everyone else, and doesn't yet grasp that you come back, so 'don't go' is the only tool she has. Separation anxiety usually peaks somewhere between about ten and eighteen months and then eases through the second year, and it's a sign of a healthy, secure attachment to you."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Emotional-and-Social-Development-8-12-Months.aspx" }

  - title: "Babbling for real — 'mamama' and 'dadada'"
    detail: "Language is taking off: the early vowel coos are turning into strings of repeated, recognizable syllables — 'ba-ba,' 'da-da,' 'ga-ga,' 'ma-ma' — with the rhythm and melody of real speech, and she's imitating many of the sounds and gestures she sees you make. Those first 'mama' and 'dada' sounds are usually stumbled upon by accident and don't yet mean you specifically; the meaning attaches a bit later, once she notices how thrilled you get. Answer everything she says with words of your own and leave a pause for her turn — this back-and-forth is how the naming of the world begins, and comprehension runs well ahead of talking."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Language-Development-8-to-12-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Object permanence is dawning — peekaboo finally makes sense"
    detail: "A big cognitive shift lands around eight months: she's starting to understand that things still exist when she can't see them. Hide a toy under a scarf now and she'll lift the scarf to find it, instead of acting as if it vanished — which is exactly why peekaboo suddenly delights her, and why she'll drop a spoon off the high chair fifty times to watch where it goes. This is real experimenting, not mischief. It also explains some of the new separation anxiety: she now knows you continue to exist somewhere when you leave, and she'd very much like you back."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Play peekaboo and hide-the-toy — over and over"
    detail: "Now that object permanence is switching on, hiding games are pure gold. Play peekaboo behind your hands, a blanket, or the side of the crib; hide a favourite toy partly under a cloth and let her uncover it, then fully under it as she gets the hang of it. Each round teaches the same reassuring lesson — things (and people) that disappear come back — which is the very idea she needs to make peace with your leaving the room. Take turns, keep it light and giggly, and let her be the one to do the hiding and finding as she's able."

  - title: "Narrate everything — she understands far more than she can say"
    detail: "Receptive language races ahead of talking, so the input matters enormously right now: name what she's looking at, describe what you're doing, read simple board books, and use real words in real sentences rather than only baby-talk. Point at and name objects across the room and watch her eyes go to them — when she looks toward a toy you've named, she's telling you she understood. Imitation is a two-way game too: make a sound or a wave, and give her a beat to copy it back."

  - title: "Cause-and-effect toys and things that move"
    detail: "With a better grip and a longer attention span (still only a couple of minutes on any one thing at eight months, stretching toward fifteen by her birthday), she loves toys that do something back: buttons, levers, dials, lids, and things that rattle, pop, or make noise when banged. Now that she's mobile, balls to chase, toys on wheels to push, and sturdy things to pull up on all get her moving. Don't overlook the kitchen-cupboard classics — nesting plastic containers, a wooden spoon, and measuring cups often beat anything from the toy aisle, and dropping-and-retrieving games are a whole afternoon's science experiment."

  - title: "Ease the clinginess instead of fighting it"
    detail: "Since separation anxiety is worse when she's tired, hungry, or unwell, time your exits for after a nap and a feed. When you do leave, keep goodbyes short and matter-of-fact rather than drawn-out — have whoever's staying start a distraction (a new toy, the mirror, a little game), say bye, and go; her tears usually settle within minutes once you're out of sight. Practise tiny separations at home: when she crawls off to a babyproofed room, don't chase her instantly — let her discover that a little distance is survivable and that you're still there."

feeding:
  - title: "Milk is still the main event"
    detail: "Even as meals get bigger, breastmilk or formula is still the backbone of her nutrition this month — roughly 24 oz (about 720 mL) a day, providing something like 400–500 of her daily calories. Breastfed babies nurse around four to six times a day; formula-fed babies take a similar total across the day. As solids ramp up she'll naturally drink a little less, and that's fine — think of food at this age as practice and complement, not replacement. Let her appetite lead; some days she eats enthusiastically and other days barely bothers, and both are normal."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Sample-One-Day-Menu-for-an-8-to-12-Month-Old.aspx" }
  - title: "Finger foods and self-feeding"
    detail: "This is prime time for letting her feed herself: her pincer grasp is arriving, so offer soft finger foods she can pick up and gum — think well-cooked veggie pieces, soft ripe fruit, small bits of scrambled egg, cheese, tender pasta, or toast fingers. Move past smooth purées to lumpier, mashed, minced, and soft-lump textures so she learns to move food around her mouth and chew. Cut everything to a safe size and softness (about pea-to-chickpea, smashable between your fingers), and expect glorious mess — it's how she learns. Always stay with her while she eats and keep her sitting upright, never propped or reclined."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year" }

  - title: "Keep the iron coming — and know the no-go foods"
    detail: "Iron stores from birth are running low by the second half of the first year, so keep offering iron-rich foods at most meals: puréed or finely minced meat, chicken, and fish; mashed beans, lentils, and tofu; eggs; and iron-fortified infant cereal. Two hard rules stay in force: no honey before twelve months (risk of infant botulism), and no choking-shaped foods — no whole grapes, nuts, popcorn, hard raw veg, or coins of hot dog or sausage. Halve or quarter round foods, cook hard vegetables soft, and skip anything small, hard, and round."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/feeding_your_baby_in_the_first_year" }
  - title: "Introduce an open cup or straw cup"
    detail: "Around eight or nine months is a good time to start offering water in a small open cup (held by you at first) or a straw cup with meals — it builds the mouth and jaw skills that plain sippy cups don't, and gets her ready to wean off the bottle by around a year. Expect far more to end up on her chin and bib than down her throat at first; that's the learning. Let her handle and inspect the cup, tip in just a few drops, and pause for her to swallow before offering more. Small sips of water with meals are all she needs — this is about the skill, not the volume."

sleep:
  - title: "Still about 12–16 hours, usually across two naps"
    detail: "Most babies this age sleep 12–16 hours in a full day, including a stretch of roughly 9–12 hours overnight, with the daytime sleep settling into two naps (individual naps run anywhere from half an hour to a couple of hours). Total sleep hasn't changed much from last month; what's shifting is that the pattern is consolidating. Keep the same rhythm of bright, active days and dark, quiet, boring nights that's been building her body clock all along."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep812m.html" }
  - title: "Night waking can flare up again — and it's usually developmental"
    detail: "Sleep problems are common in the second half of the first year, and a baby who'd been sleeping beautifully may suddenly start waking and crying at night. The usual culprits are the very milestones you're celebrating: separation anxiety (she wakes, notices you're not there, and protests) plus a brain busy practising crawling and pulling up. It's a phase, not a permanent backslide. Keep responses calm, brief, and boring — reassure her without turning night into playtime — and hold the bedtime routine steady."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep812m.html" }
  - title: "A predictable wind-down routine helps"
    detail: "A short, consistent bedtime sequence — a warm bath, a book or two, a quiet song, lights down — signals that sleep is coming and does a lot of the settling for you. Where you can, lay her down drowsy but still awake so she practises falling asleep on her own, which is what lets her resettle when she surfaces between sleep cycles overnight. Consistency is the active ingredient: the same order, the same cues, most nights."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep812m.html" }
  - title: "Safe-sleep rules still apply"
    detail: "None of the safe-sleep basics change now that she's mobile: back to sleep for every nap and every night, on a firm flat surface, in her own crib with nothing loose in it — no pillows, blankets, bumpers, or toys. With a baby who pulls up, this is also the moment to drop the crib mattress to its lowest position and remove anything she could use to climb or that could tangle. Room-sharing without bed-sharing is recommended for at least the first year."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Get down on the floor and see your home as she does"
    detail: "The single most useful babyproofing move is to get on your hands and knees and crawl through each room at her eye level — suddenly you spot the dangling cord, the tippy lamp, the coin under the couch, the outlet, the sharp table corner right at head height. To a newly mobile baby the whole house is a playground of things to crawl under, climb on, pull down, touch, and taste. Do this sweep now, before she's fully mobile, not after — and pair a safe environment with active supervision, because no gadget replaces a watching adult."

    detail: "Falls down stairs are a leading hazard for babies who are crawling and pulling up, so gate the stairs now. alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Plenty of babies never crawl — and that's completely fine"
    detail: "Crawling is not a required milestone and isn't part of formal developmental scoring: some babies bum-shuffle, some slither, some roll everywhere, and some skip floor-based locomotion entirely and go straight from sitting to pulling up to cruising and walking. There's no evidence that skipping crawling causes any problem down the line. What you're actually watching for is that she's finding some way to get around and using both sides of her body — not that she's doing it the textbook way."
