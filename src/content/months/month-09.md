title: "Month 9 — on the move, and checking in"
description: "Crawling, pulling up, and a refined little pincer grip meet a peak of separation anxiety — and the 9-month visit is a developmental check-in rather than a shot day in Alberta."
milestones:
  - title: "Crawling — but there's no one right way to do it"
    detail: "Most babies are crawling by now; the AAP puts the usual window for mastering it at about seven to ten months, often after a spell of rocking on hands and knees or even scooting backward first. Plenty of perfectly typical babies never crawl in the classic hands-and-knees style at all — they bum-shuffle, commando-slither on their tummies, or roll to where they want to go, and what matters is that they're using both sides of the body and finding a way to get around. However he does it, this is the month the floor becomes his highway, so the babyproofing below stops being optional. Ranges are wide, and preemies are measured from their adjusted age, not the calendar."

This frees both hands for playing while seated, which is why sitting and grabbing suddenly go together. The AAP flags it the other way round too: if she isn't sitting by herself by nine months, mention it to your doctor."

  - title: "Pulling up to stand, with cruising on the horizon"
    detail: "Many babies are now hauling themselves upright on the crib rail, the couch, or your trouser leg every chance they get, and the AAP describes them soon cruising sideways along the furniture, holding on for balance, as the next step toward walking. If yours isn't pulling up yet this month, that's well within normal — it's one of the more variable skills, and busy babies often perfect one thing (crawling, babbling) before tackling the next. A classic new-pull-up problem is getting stuck standing with no idea how to sit back down; help him fold gently to his bottom rather than letting him topple, and he'll learn the controlled sit within days to a couple of weeks."

  - title: "The pincer grasp is sharpening — and so is the choking risk"
    detail: "Somewhere around nine months babies start picking things up between the thumb and forefinger rather than raking them into a fist, which is why self-feeding peas and puffs suddenly becomes possible. That same delicate new grip will also find the dropped pill, the coin under the couch, and the bit of dog kibble long before you spot them — anything that fits through a toilet-paper tube can choke a baby this age. The upside is real: offer soft, pea-to-marble-sized finger foods and let him practise the pinch, which is genuine fine-motor training. The flip side is a standing rule that everything small lives up high from now on."

  - title: "Object permanence has clicked into place"
    detail: "By now your baby genuinely understands that things — and people — still exist when they're out of sight, which is a real leap in thinking. The AAP notes that where a younger baby forgot a toy the moment it was hidden, a baby this age will lift the cloth and search underneath, so sure the toy is still there that she keeps looking. Nemours frames the same milestone as looking for objects when they're dropped out of sight. This is exactly why peekaboo is now hilarious rather than baffling (you were never really gone), and it's also part of why separation from you suddenly feels like a big deal."

  - title: "Babbling with real syllables, and copying what you do"
    detail: "The early coos and gurgles have given way to strings of true consonant-vowel syllables — 'ba-ba-ba,' 'ma-ma-ma,' 'da-da-da,' 'ga-ga' — that vary in pitch and rhythm and sound uncannily like conversation. The AAP notes babies this age also begin imitating the gestures they watch you make, which is where the first waving and clapping tend to emerge, and may stumble on 'mama' or 'dada' almost by accident before learning to aim them at you on purpose. Comprehension is running ahead of speech: name a favourite toy across the room and watch whether she turns toward it — that glance is her telling you she understood. Answer every babble with real words and leave a pause for her 'reply'; that back-and-forth is how language is built."

  - title: "Shy with strangers, clingy with you — separation anxiety peaks"
    detail: "This is often the loudest month for separation anxiety: the Canadian Paediatric Society notes it usually peaks around eight or nine months (though it can crest again anytime up to eighteen months), and stranger wariness can come and go on and off through the first two years. Your once-sociable baby may now bury her face in your shoulder when Grandma reaches for her, wail when you leave the room, or track you anxiously around the house. It isn't a step backward or a sign she's been spoiled — it's the flip side of two good developments, object permanence (she knows you still exist somewhere else) plus a new grasp of who is and isn't familiar. Quick, matter-of-fact goodbyes, a consistent caregiver, and letting her warm to new people at her own pace all help; hamming up your own worry does not."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Peekaboo and hide-the-toy are now the best games in the house"
    detail: "Because object permanence has landed, games built on things disappearing and coming back are suddenly thrilling and genuinely instructive. Hide a toy under a cloth or an upside-down cup and let her uncover it; play peekaboo from behind your hands, a door, or a burp cloth; drop a ball into a box and tip it out again. Each round quietly reinforces that hidden things still exist — the same idea that, off the playmat, underlies her separation anxiety, so this play is reassurance as much as fun. Narrate it (\"Where's the ball? … There it is!\") to feed language at the same time."

  - title: "Let the tiny scientist experiment"
    detail: "The AAP describes babies this age as little scientists who learn how the world works by dropping, banging, throwing, shaking, and — of course — mouthing everything they can reach. Cause-and-effect toys pay off now: containers to fill and dump, stacking cups and rings, blocks to bang together, and simple busy-boards or pop-up toys with buttons and levers. The endless dropping-things-off-the-high-chair routine isn't defiance, it's an experiment in gravity and in getting you to fetch — a bit of both. Rotating a few toys keeps it fresh better than a full toy box dumped out at once."

  - title: "Talk, name, sing, and read — a language-rich day pays off"
    detail: "Comprehension is sprinting ahead of speech right now, so the everyday narration you do — naming body parts, describing what you're cooking, pointing out the dog, reading the same board book for the hundredth time — is doing real work. Nemours' guidance for this stage is simply to sing, talk, play, and read to your baby, because that back-and-forth is how babies learn language best. Leave gaps for her to babble back and treat her sounds as her turn in the conversation. Chunky board books she can grab, gum, and help turn are perfect; expect her to be more interested in flipping the pages than in the plot."

  - title: "Copycat games grow waving, clapping, and first gestures"
    detail: "Since babies this age imitate the gestures they watch you make, this is a great month to model the social ones on purpose: wave bye-bye every single time someone leaves, clap and say 'yay,' blow kisses, play pat-a-cake and 'so big.' Repetition and delight are the whole method — she copies what she sees over and over, and your obvious pleasure is the reward that keeps her doing it. These gestures are also early communication, a way for her to 'say' hello, more, and all-done before the words arrive. Copying your sounds counts too, so exaggerate the fun noises and let her volley them back."

  - title: "Give the new stander something safe to pull up on"
    detail: "A baby who's pulling up and getting ready to cruise needs sturdy, stable things to haul up on — a low, heavy coffee table or a weighted activity centre that won't tip, not a wobbly chair or a bookcase that could come down on her. Sitting on the floor a short crawl away and inviting her to come to you builds the strength for cruising and, eventually, walking. When she gets stranded standing and frustrated, calmly help her learn the controlled sit-down rather than always lifting her off. Keep her barefoot or in grippy non-slip socks for the best traction — smooth-soled shoes and slippery socks just cause skids."

feeding:
  - title: "Milk still leads, but three meals plus snacks is taking shape"
    detail: "Breast milk or formula is still the nutritional backbone this month, with solids catching up fast — Nemours' picture for this stage is a baby settling into regular meal and snack times alongside milk feeds. A common rhythm by now is roughly three small meals a day with a snack or two, wrapped around nursing or bottles, though your baby's appetite should call the shots meal to meal. Don't let milk or juice drown the appetite for solids; over the coming months solids shift from the 'gravy' to the main event, but that changeover isn't complete until well after the first birthday. If picky, food-of-the-week phases show up, that's classic and rarely a nutrition problem as long as she's growing."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed812m.html" }
  - title: "Finger foods and self-feeding — hand over the spoon"
    detail: "Now that the pincer grasp is arriving, Nemours notes babies around nine months can usually pick food up between finger and thumb and start feeding themselves, so this is the month to lean into finger foods and let her get gloriously messy. Offer soft, gum-mashable pieces cut pea- to marble-sized: ripe banana or avocado, well-cooked veg, soft pasta, flaked boneless fish, small bits of cheese, scrambled egg, squished blueberries. Give her a spoon of her own to wave around while you spoon-feed too — being part of the meal matters more than how much actually lands. Keep offering iron-rich foods (iron-fortified cereal, meat, beans, lentils), which stay important through the whole first year."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed812m.html" }
  - title: "Choking safety: seated, supervised, and the wrong foods off the menu"
    detail: "Self-feeding raises the choking stakes, so the rules tighten: feed only a baby who's seated upright in a high chair — never one crawling, cruising, or toddling around — and always stay with her while she eats. Nemours' foods-to-avoid list for this age is the standard one: hot dogs, whole grapes, raw hard vegetables, hard cheese, popcorn, nuts, and anything else that won't dissolve or can't be mashed with the gums. Learn the difference between gagging (noisy, red-faced, self-correcting — leave her to it) and true choking (silent, can't cough or cry — act at once); a baby-first-aid refresher this month is time well spent. No honey before the first birthday; as for cow's milk, Canadian guidance allows whole (3.25%) milk as a drink from 9–12 months once she's eating a variety of iron-rich foods — though breastmilk or formula remains the ideal main drink until one."

  - title: "Cups, juice, and cow's milk — where things stand at nine months"
    detail: "Keep letting her practise sipping from an open or straw cup with meals; it's a skill worth building now, and it takes the pressure off bottles later. Skip juice entirely before 12 months — Nemours is explicit that infants under a year shouldn't have it — since it just fills a small tummy with sugar and blunts the appetite for food and milk. Whole (3.25%) cow's milk as a drink can start between 9 and 12 months per Canadian guidance (there's no rush — breastmilk or formula still does the job), and most doctors are fine with whole-milk yogurt, cottage cheese, and hard cheese as foods before then. If you're still exploring how to start and expand solids safely, the Starting Solids guide covers first foods, allergens, textures, and choking in depth."

sleep:
  - title: "Roughly 12–16 hours a day, usually across two naps"
    detail: "Babies this age need about 12 to 16 hours of sleep in a full day, including a solid 9-to-12-hour stretch at night, and most still take two daytime naps that run anywhere from 30 minutes to two hours each. Total daytime sleep is slowly consolidating into those two predictable naps — often mid-morning and early afternoon — as the catnaps of earlier months fade. A consistent, calm bedtime routine (bath, book, feed, bed) and putting her down drowsy-but-awake keep giving her practice at falling asleep on her own. Every baby's exact numbers vary, so use these as a frame, not a target to hit."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep812m.html" }
  - title: "Night waking often flares — and separation anxiety is usually why"
    detail: "The second half of the first year is a classic time for sleep to wobble, and even a baby who used to sleep through may start calling out or crying in the night, then settling the moment a parent appears. Nemours ties much of this to separation anxiety — she wakes, realises you're not there, and wants proof you still exist. Newly minted standers add to it by pulling up in the crib at 2 a.m. and getting stuck, so you may be gently laying her back down in the dark. Offer brief, boring reassurance — a pat, a few quiet words — then let her resettle herself; long cuddle-or-feed sessions can accidentally teach her to wake for the attention."

  - title: "Safe-sleep basics don't change just because she's mobile"
    detail: "A baby who now sits, crawls, and pulls up still sleeps by the same rules: on her back, on a firm flat surface, in her own crib in your room, with nothing loose in the space — no pillows, blankets, bumpers, or toys. If she rolls or sits up herself in the crib after being placed on her back, you don't need to keep repositioning her, but you do keep the crib bare and lower the mattress once she can pull to stand so she can't tip out. Room-sharing without bed-sharing is still recommended, and bed-sharing still raises the risk of suffocation and SIDS. Dress her for the room temperature instead of adding covers."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Anchor anything that could tip — this is the big one now"
    detail: "A baby who pulls up will grab, lean on, and try to climb whatever's in reach, and furniture that tips is one of the most serious home hazards at this age — the AAP notes a child is treated in a US emergency department for a tip-over injury roughly every hour, and about half of the deadly ones happen in bedrooms. Anchor dressers, bookshelves, and shelving to the wall with metal anti-tip brackets (not brittle plastic ones), mount or strap flat-screen TVs, and never sit a TV on top of a dresser. Put the heaviest items in the lowest drawers and add drawer stops so a pulled-open drawer can't become a staircase. MyHealth Alberta's babyproofing checklist puts securing anything that could tip if grabbed or climbed at the top of the list."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/safety-prevention/at-home/Pages/Preventing-Furniture-and-TV-Tip-Overs.aspx" }

  - title: "Gate the stairs — top and bottom"
    detail: "Once she's crawling and cruising, stairs are an obvious fall risk, and MyHealth Alberta recommends putting safety gates at both the top and the bottom of stairways. At the top, use a hardware-mounted gate screwed into the wall — pressure-mounted gates can give way — and skip old accordion-style gates, which can trap a head or neck. Keep the gates latched every time, since it only takes one open one. Blocking off other danger zones the same way (a step down into a sunken room, the doorway to a bathroom) buys you breathing room."

  - title: "Pad sharp corners and lock the low doors and drawers"
    detail: "At standing and cruising height, coffee-table corners, hearth edges, and end tables are right at head level, so cover sharp corners with guards or soft pads. MyHealth Alberta also advises childproof locks or guards on drawers and on kitchen and bathroom cabinets, plus safety plugs or covers on all electrical outlets, because a crawler's whole job this month is to open, empty, and probe everything low. Prioritise the cabinets and drawers holding cleaners, medicines, sharp tools, and small objects — and better still, move those things up high, since a lock is a backup, not a guarantee.   - title: "Small objects up, cords tied back, water never unattended"
    detail: "That sharp new pincer grip finds tiny hazards fast, so keep small objects — coins, buttons, marbles, batteries, rubber bands, deflated balloons, small toy parts — well out of reach, and get down to floor level yourself to spot what she'll spot. Keep blind and drapery cords out of reach and cut any looped cords, which are a strangulation risk. Around water, never leave her alone in the bath even for a moment — babies can drown in seconds and in very little water — and keep the bathroom door closed and toilet lids down, since a curious baby can topple headfirst into a toilet or bucket. Constant supervision remains the real safeguard; assume she can do a bit more than you'd expect."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "The 9-month well-child visit — a developmental check-in"
    detail: "Most Alberta babies have a well-child visit around nine months. As at earlier checkups, the doctor measures weight, length, and head circumference and plots them on the growth chart, does a head-to-toe physical (eyes, heart, hips, and watching how she moves), and talks through eating, sleeping, and behaviour. The nine-month visit is also a standard point for a structured developmental screen — a short questionnaire about her sounds, movements, and social skills — designed to catch any delays early. Bring her Alberta health card and a written list of questions, and treat the milestone checklist as a conversation-starter, not a test to pass or fail."

  - title: "No routine shot this month in Alberta — the next round is 12 months"
    detail: "Unlike the 2-, 4-, and 6-month visits, there is no routine immunization scheduled at nine months on the Alberta immunization schedule — the next set of vaccines (including MMR-Var and the meningococcal and pneumococcal boosters) comes at 12 months. Some other places, including parts of the US, pair the nine-month checkup with vaccines, so don't be thrown if you read otherwise; here, the visit is squarely about growth and development. If your baby was due a fall or winter influenza vaccine and hasn't had it, this visit is a fine time to catch up, since flu vaccine is offered from six months on. Ask your provider to confirm your baby is up to date on the earlier doses."

redFlags:
  - title: "Movement flags to mention at the visit"
    detail: "Milestone ranges are wide and this is a checklist for conversation, not a scorecard — but a few motor signs are worth raising with the doctor around now: a baby who can't sit without support or get herself into a sitting position, or who doesn't pass things from one hand to the other. The AAP specifically says to let your pediatrician know if she isn't sitting by herself by nine months. None of these means something is wrong on its own; they're simply prompts to have a closer look, and the earlier a real delay is spotted, the more support helps. For a premature baby, judge all of this from the adjusted age."

  - title: "Your own mental health still counts as baby care"

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "A nursing or bottle 'strike' now usually isn't weaning"
    detail: "Around nine months plenty of babies get squirmy and distractible at the breast or bottle — pulling off to look at the cat, batting the bottle away, nursing for a minute then popping up to practise standing. It's almost always busyness, not readiness to wean: with a whole fascinating world to explore, sitting still for milk faces stiff competition. Try feeding in a dim, quiet room with the TV and your phone off, or when she's sleepy (first thing in the morning, after the bath), when she's less likely to bolt. Babies still do best on breast milk or formula until the first birthday, so this is worth riding out rather than reading as the end of milk."

  - title: "Still no teeth (or hair)? Both are completely normal"
    detail: "There are plenty of nine-month-olds with a big gummy grin and not a tooth in sight — the first tooth usually arrives around six months, but the normal range runs from about three months old to past the first birthday, and late teething is usually just hereditary. It doesn't hold up eating, either: first teeth are for biting, not chewing, and babies mash soft foods perfectly well with their gums. Thin hair is the same story — some babies stay nearly bald well into the second year, and it says nothing about how thick their hair will eventually be. Neither is a developmental concern on its own."

  - title: "Surprises in the diaper are usually just dinner"
    detail: "Now that she's eating a real variety of solids, her stools will change colour and texture to match — orange after carrots, reddish after beets, dark flecks after bananas or blueberries, and whole peas or corn kernels that reappear looking untouched. Because babies barely gum their food before swallowing and have short, still-maturing digestive tracts, a lot of what goes in comes out looking much the same. 'Sandy' or gritty stools are common too, often just oat cereal or pears passing through. Before you worry, think back to what she ate; genuine concerns (blood, hard pellets and straining, or watery diarrhea with dehydration) are a different picture and do warrant a call."
