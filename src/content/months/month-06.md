monthNumber: 6
title: "Six months — sitting up and the first spoonful"
description: "Half a birthday, and a milestone month on two fronts: baby is propping into a sit, rolling both ways, and grabbing everything in reach — and it's time to break out the high chair for that momentous first taste of solids."
milestones:
  - title: "Sitting — with support at first, then briefly on their own"
    detail: "This is the headline motor skill of the half-year mark. Somewhere in the 6-to-8-month window most babies, once you sit them upright, will hold the position without toppling forward onto their arms; a little before that they'll prop on their own hands in a wobbly 'tripod' sit for a few seconds. Give them floor time to practise — sit them among cushions so a tip-over is soft, and put a toy just out of reach to pull the trunk into work. Head control should be solid by now; the balance and back strength for a steady, hands-free sit are still weeks of practice away, so a baby who isn't sitting unsupported yet at exactly six months is squarely in the normal range."

  - title: "Rolling both ways — back to front and front to back"
    detail: "By the end of this stretch most babies can roll over in both directions, which is a bigger deal than it sounds: rolling is how they get their first taste of moving themselves across a space, and it changes the safety math on any raised surface for good. Many will also push up onto straight arms during tummy time and rock or pivot in a circle — the ingredients of crawling, still a few months off. Keep offering tummy time; it's the engine behind all of this."

  - title: "Hands that grab, rake, and pass things from hand to hand"
    detail: "The fine-motor leap this month is real. Babies reach out and grab a toy they want, use a whole-hand 'raking' or claw-like grip to scoop small things toward themselves, and around the sixth to eighth month learn to transfer an object from one hand to the other and turn it over to inspect it. And everything they catch goes straight to the mouth — mouthing is how a six-month-old explores texture and shape, not a sign of hunger or teething alone. This is exactly why choking-size objects become a live hazard now (see Safety below)."

  - title: "Babbling with real consonants — 'ba,' 'da,' 'ga'"
    detail: "Cooing is maturing into babble: strings of consonant-plus-vowel sounds like 'bababa' and 'dadada' that borrow the rhythm and melody of the language they hear all day. Around six or seven months babies start actively imitating the speech sounds you make and take turns 'talking' — you say something, they answer, you answer back. Feed the loop: when they land on 'bah,' echo it and offer words that start that way ('bottle,' 'ball,' 'bath'). Squeals, raspberries, and experimental shrieks are all part of the same vocal workout."

  - title: "Knows their people — and reads your tone"
    detail: "Socially this is a warm month. Babies now clearly recognize familiar faces and may study or size up a stranger, laugh out loud, love their own reflection in a mirror, and respond to the emotion in your voice — softening when you soothe them and crumpling if you speak sharply. Many turn toward a voice or their own name. This is the foundation of the attachment that, over the next few months, blooms into full-blown stranger wariness and separation anxiety, so enjoy the easy sociability while it lasts."

  - title: "Showing they're ready to eat"
    detail: "The feeding milestone of the month is readiness for solids, which is a cluster of skills rather than a birthday: sitting up with support and steady head-and-neck control, bringing hands and objects to the mouth, holding food in the mouth instead of pushing it straight back out with the tongue (the tongue-thrust reflex fading, usually between 4 and 6 months), and leaning in with open-mouthed interest when others eat. When most of these are in place — around six months — it's time. The full how-to is in the Feeding section and the Starting Solids deep dive."

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "Peekaboo, drop-and-fetch, and the joy of cause and effect"
    detail: "A six-month-old is starting to grasp that things exist even when hidden, which is why peekaboo suddenly gets belly laughs and why they'll drop a spoon off the high chair fifty times to watch it fall (and to watch you retrieve it — this is a game, not defiance). Lean into it: toys that do something back when acted on — rattles, roly-poly toys that right themselves, things that crinkle, pop up, or make noise — teach 'I did that,' which is the root of both learning and confidence. A few good toys beat a bin full; they can still only really attend to one thing at a time."

  - title: "Sitting opens up a whole new way to play"
    detail: "Once baby can prop into a sit, the world tilts upright and both hands come free to explore — a genuinely new vantage point after months on their back and belly. Prop them safely among cushions and set a ring of toys within reach so leaning and grabbing become their own balance practice. Big, light, easy-to-grip objects work best right now; heavy or fiddly toys just frustrate a new sitter."

  - title: "Talk, narrate, and take turns — then read the same board book again"
    detail: "The single most powerful thing you can do for language right now costs nothing: talk to them constantly and leave gaps for them to 'reply.' Narrate what you're doing, name what they're looking at, and when they babble, answer as if it were conversation — that turn-taking is exactly how the wiring for speech gets laid down. Books count double: chunky board books they can look at (and gum), read in a warm voice, are as much about the closeness and the sound of your voice as the pictures. Repetition is a feature, not a bug — they love the familiar."

feeding:
  - title: "Solids start around six months — as an addition, not a replacement"
    detail: "Breastmilk or formula stays the main event for the whole first year; first foods are about learning to eat and topping up nutrients (especially iron and zinc) that milk alone no longer fully covers after six months, not about calories replacing a feed. Health Canada and the CPS both put the start at around six months, once the readiness signs are there, and note that delaying much past six months actually raises the risk of iron deficiency. Expect chaos and comedy at first: most of that first spoonful ends up on the chin, the bib, and the tray, and that's the process working, not failing."

  - title: "Lead with iron — this is Health Canada's headline"
    detail: "The single most important thing about Canadian first-food advice is iron. A baby's in-utero iron stores are running out around six months, and iron deficiency in this window can do lasting harm to growth and development — so iron-rich foods should be among the very first offered, at least twice a day. Good sources: well-cooked meat, poultry, fish, cooked whole egg, tofu, and well-mashed legumes (beans, lentils), plus iron-fortified infant cereal (oat, wheat, barley, or rice) mixed to a soupy consistency with breastmilk or formula. Pairing iron-rich foods with vitamin-C-rich fruit or veg helps absorption. Whether you spoon-feed purées, offer soft finger foods (baby-led weaning), or mix both, the iron-first rule is the same."

  - title: "Offer the common allergens early — and keep offering them"
    detail: "The old advice to delay peanut, egg, and other allergens has flipped: introducing common allergenic foods around the time you start solids (roughly six months), and then keeping them in the regular rotation, is now thought to help prevent food allergies rather than cause them. Practical version: smooth peanut butter thinned with water or breastmilk (never a whole nut or a thick gob — that's a choking risk), well-cooked egg, dairy foods like yogurt and cheese. Introduce new foods one at a time with a couple of days between, so if a reaction shows up you know the culprit. If your baby has severe eczema or a known food allergy in the family, talk to your provider — they may advise starting some allergens even a bit earlier."

  - title: "No honey before one year — the one hard 'never'"
    detail: "Honey, and anything made with it, stays entirely off the menu until the first birthday. It can carry spores of the bacterium that causes infant botulism — harmless to adults but genuinely dangerous to a baby's immature gut, causing constipation, weak sucking, poor feeding, and lethargy. This applies to raw honey, cooked honey, and honey baked into foods alike. It's the clearest bright line in first-year feeding."

  - title: "Textures, gagging vs. choking, and safe sizes — see the deep dive"
    detail: "Start smooth and soupy and thicken and lump up the texture over the coming weeks; letting a baby experience lumps and soft finger foods before about nine months makes fussy texture-refusal later less likely. Gagging is loud, red-faced, and normal — it's a protective reflex pushing food forward, and it will happen a lot; choking is silent and needs immediate action. Keep foods soft enough to squish between your fingers, cut round foods lengthwise, and never leave a baby alone while eating. This section is deliberately a signpost: the full guidance on purées vs. baby-led weaning, choking-hazard foods, safe sizes, and an infant-choking response lives in the Starting Solids deep dive linked from this page."

  - title: "Milk amounts haven't dropped — yet"
    detail: "Even as solids arrive, milk intake barely changes this month. Breastfed babies still nurse on the order of five or six times a day; formula-fed babies take roughly 24–32 oz (about 720–960 ml) a day. Offer solids alongside milk, not instead of it — a common rhythm is a milk feed to take the edge off, then solids while they're cheerful, once or twice a day to start. If you're breastfeeding, keep giving the recommended 400 IU of vitamin D daily. Over the back half of the year, as eating skills grow, solids gradually take over more of the plate."

sleep:
  - title: "Around 12–16 hours a day, settling into two or three naps"
    detail: "Total sleep is roughly 12–16 hours across the day, most of it now loaded into the night, with the daytime consolidating into two or three naps that together add up to about 3–4 hours. Many six-month-olds sleep a long night stretch of nine hours or more, though brief wake-ups (that they may resettle from on their own) are completely normal — 'sleeping through' rarely means a truly unbroken night. If naps are still short and scattered, they'll knit together over the coming months."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "A consistent bedtime routine — and putting them down drowsy but awake"
    detail: "This is a good age to lock in a short, predictable wind-down: a bath, a feed, a book or a song, lights low, same order every night. The most useful habit you can build is laying baby in the crib drowsy but still awake, so they practise the skill of falling asleep on their own rather than only ever nodding off on the breast or in your arms — that's what lets them resettle themselves at 2 a.m. New skills like rolling and sitting, and the busy brain that comes with them, can briefly scramble sleep; consistency rides it out better than new crutches do."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "Safe-sleep rules don't relax just because they can roll"
    detail: "Keep placing baby on their back to sleep for every sleep, on a firm flat surface with a fitted sheet and nothing else in the space — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or toys — and room-sharing without bed-sharing is recommended for at least the first 6 months. Once a baby can roll both ways on their own, you no longer need to reposition them if they flip during the night, but you still always start them on the back. A pacifier at sleep time is fine, and keep the room cool and smoke-free."

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "Sitting + reaching = the pull-everything-over stage"
    detail: "A baby who can sit and reach can drag anything within arm's length toward themselves and into their mouth — tablecloths (and whatever's on them), hanging cords, hot drinks, phone chargers, houseplants. Do a get-down-on-the-floor sweep at their eye level: nothing hot, sharp, small, or dangly within reach of where they sit and play. Keep hot drinks well back from the edge of tables and never hold a hot drink and the baby at the same time."

    detail: "Everything goes in the mouth, so anything that could block the airway is a real risk from now on: coins, button batteries, small toy parts, older siblings' Lego, dried beans, grapes and other round foods left in reach, popped-off bits of anything. The rule of thumb — if it fits through a toilet-paper tube, it's too small — is worth applying to the whole floor. Button batteries and magnets deserve special vigilance because they cause severe internal injury fast; store them locked away."

    detail: "A wiggly six-month-old can arch, twist, and slide right out of a high chair or feeding seat, and falls from height are a leading cause of injury at this age. Use the full 5-point harness including the crotch strap every single time — the tray is not a restraint and won't stop a determined slide underneath it. Put the chair on the floor, not on a counter or table, and never leave a strapped-in baby unattended in the kitchen."

  - title: "Alberta's six-month vaccines — fewer than the US schedule"
    detail: "At the Alberta six-month visit the routine shot is the third dose of DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (the one combined needle covering diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Hib, and hepatitis B). redFlags:
  - title: "Mention at the six-month visit: not sitting, reaching, babbling, or responding"
    detail: "These are conversation-starters for the checkup, not alarms on their own, but flag them: a baby who can't hold their head steady or won't bear any weight to sit even with support, who doesn't reach for or grab objects, who makes no babbling sounds and doesn't take vocal turns, who doesn't respond to sounds around them or turn toward voices, who shows no affection for familiar caregivers, or who seems very stiff or very floppy. Loss of a skill a baby previously had is always worth a prompt call. Ranges are wide and premature babies are measured by their adjusted age — but your provider would always rather hear a worry early."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The first tooth may arrive — or may not, and both are fine"
    detail: "The first baby tooth usually pushes through around six months, most often one of the bottom front two, but the normal range is enormous — some babies cut a tooth as early as three months and others not until after their first birthday, and a toothless six-month-old is perfectly healthy. Teething can bring drooling, gum-gnawing, fussiness, and disrupted sleep, but it doesn't cause high fevers or diarrhea; if those show up, look for another cause. Once that first tooth is in, start cleaning it once a day (bedtime) with a soft baby brush, and plan a first dental visit within six months of it appearing or by age one."

  - title: "Teething relief that's safe — and the things to skip"

Skip the popular gadgets that aren't safe: amber teething necklaces are both a strangulation and a choking hazard and there's no good evidence they work, and topical teething gels and 'teething biscuits' are best avoided too.
