monthNumber: 5
title: "Month five — reaching, rolling, and grabbing the world"
description: "The fifth month is hands-on in every sense: rolling starts going both ways, everything within reach gets raked in and mouthed, the babble picks up real consonants and raspberries — and readiness for solids is quietly building toward the six-month line, not crossing it yet."
milestones:
  - title: "Rolling — and, for some, both directions"
    detail: "Many babies have tummy-to-back rolling down by now and are working on the harder back-to-tummy roll, which needs arching, twisting, and a bit more strength; the AAP frames the whole 4–7-month window as when babies 'learn to roll over in both directions,' with the two directions often arriving weeks apart. Nemours puts it the same way — 'your baby will probably learn to roll over in both directions' across this stretch. Treat it as a range, not a due date: some babies roll early and often, some skip straight to sitting and barely bother rolling, and both are normal. The practical consequence is bigger than the milestone — a baby who can roll can no longer be left alone on a bed, couch, or change table."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/move47m.html" }
  - title: "Sitting with support — the tripod is emerging"
    detail: "Held or propped, most babies this age can hold their trunk upright for a bit, and many are starting the classic 'tripod' sit — leaning forward on hands planted between the legs for balance. The AAP describes babies leaning forward and extending their arms to balance on the way to sitting steadily without support around six to eight months. Back and trunk muscles are visibly stronger: on the tummy they push up on straight arms and arch to lift the chest. Prop him in a corner of the couch or your lap and let him practise the wobble — the near-topples are how the balance gets built."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/move47m.html" }
  - title: "Bearing weight and bouncing on his legs"
    detail: "Hold him upright with his feet on your lap or the floor and he'll likely push down, take some of his own weight, and maybe bounce or 'dance' — Nemours notes babies this age 'learn to support all their weight when held in a standing position,' and the AAP describes the toe-curling, bouncing, mock-'walking' that happens when the feet touch a surface. This is leg-strengthening play, not early walking, and it doesn't mean he's ahead of schedule. It's also a normal thing to look for: a baby who takes no weight at all on his legs by around six months is worth a mention to the doctor."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/move47m.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Raking, reaching, mouthing, and passing hand to hand"
    detail: "Grasp is getting purposeful: babies begin using the fingers and thumb in what the AAP calls a 'mitten- or claw-like grip or raking motion' to scoop small things toward the palm, reach fairly accurately for what they want, and — usually toward the back half of this window — pass an object from one hand to the other. The true finger-and-thumb pincer grasp is still months off (around nine months), so expect whole-hand rakes for now. Everything he catches goes straight to the mouth, which is how he learns 'about touch, shape, and texture' — normal exploration, and the reason the floor has to be spotless of small objects."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn47m.html" }
  - title: "Babble with real consonants — and raspberries"
    detail: "The vowel coos of a couple of months ago are turning into true babble: the AAP notes that from about four months a baby 'will start to babble, using many of the rhythms and characteristics of' your language, stringing consonants and vowels into syllables. Expect blown raspberries, squeals, belly laughs, and a genuine back-and-forth — Nemours describes babies learning to 'take turns making sounds with you.' Answer every sound with one of your own and leave a gap for his reply; that turn-taking is early conversation. There's a wide normal range, but a baby not babbling or reacting to sound by around seven months is worth raising with the doctor."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Language-Development-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/c47m.html" }
  - title: "Turning to your voice — and maybe to his name"
    detail: "He turns his head toward the sound of your voice, reads the pitch and tone of it (the AAP notes babies respond to whether you sound playful or stern well before they know the words), and somewhere in this 4–7-month window many babies start to respond to their own name — Nemours lists it as an emerging skill, not a fixed five-month checkpoint. Use his name warmly and often, narrate what you're doing, and watch for the head-turn that says he's clocked it. Socially he's more engaged than ever, using 'smiles, squeals, laughs and other sounds to get your attention.'"
    sources:

developmentAndPlay:
  - title: "He learns with his hands and mouth — so give him things worth exploring"
    detail: "Reaching, raking, and mouthing aren't mess for its own sake — Nemours notes that 'by reaching out for things, babies learn about touch, shape, and texture,' and that they 'bring things to their mouths to learn about them.' Offer a small basket of safe objects with different weights, textures, and shapes — a wooden spoon, a soft cloth book, a crinkly toy, a silicone teether — and let him work through them one at a time. He can only really attend to one object at a time at this age, so a couple of good things beat an overflowing bin. Keep it a supervised floor session on a clean, safe surface."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn47m.html" }
  - title: "Cause and effect is suddenly fascinating"
    detail: "The idea that 'I did something and something happened' is a genuine thrill right now, so toys 'that move or make sounds when your baby plays with them' — as Nemours puts it — earn their keep. A rattle he shakes, a soft ball he bats, a toy that lights up when whacked all teach him he can act on the world. You are still his favourite cause-and-effect machine, though: pause your peekaboo, wait, and let his squeal be the button that makes you pop back. This back-and-forth is doing more for his brain than any single toy."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn47m.html" }
  - title: "Peekaboo, hide-the-toy, and the first hint of object permanence"
    detail: "The seeds of understanding that things exist when they're out of sight get planted now. Nemours suggests you 'hide a toy — but don't hide it very well — and encourage your baby to find it,' and play plenty of peekaboo; both are early object-permanence games he'll find genuinely surprising and delightful. Reading counts as play too: 'choose baby books with simple pictures and faces' and touch-and-feel books with textures. He won't follow a plot, but he's soaking up the sounds, the rhythm, and the closeness of your voice."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn47m.html" }
  - title: "Floor time is rolling practice — help him get both directions"
    detail: "Open floor space is where rolling, pivoting, and pre-crawling scooting get built, so trade some container time (bouncer, seat) for supervised time flat on a mat. To coax the roll, lay him on his back and hold an interesting toy or your face out to one side and slightly up so he twists to follow it — where the head turns, the body follows — and let him do as much of the roll himself as he can. Tummy time still matters here too: it's the strength work behind rolling and sitting. Short, frequent sessions beat one long unhappy one."

feeding:
  - title: "Still milk-only — solids aren't due yet"
    detail: "Breast milk or formula is the entire diet this month and meets all his nutritional needs; the AAP recommends 'only breast milk for approximately 6 months after birth,' and Nemours points to about six months for starting solids depending on readiness. There's no benefit to jumping the gun at five months, and the old idea that early cereal buys longer sleep doesn't hold up. So enjoy the simplicity for a few more weeks — the spoons, bibs, and glorious mess are coming soon enough, just not quite yet."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed47m.html" }
  - title: "How much milk right now"
    detail: "Formula-fed babies around this age typically take roughly 6–8 oz (180–240 ml) a bottle about five times a day, and the AAP's rough ceiling is no more than about 32 oz (960 ml) in 24 hours. Breastfed babies usually nurse somewhere around five to six times a day, more if that's their rhythm — you're watching the baby, not the clock. Steady growth along his curve, plenty of wet diapers, and a baby who's content after feeds tell you intake is fine; a feed he wants a little bigger or smaller is him self-regulating, exactly as intended."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/formula-feeding/Pages/Amount-and-Schedule-of-Formula-Feedings.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed47m.html" }
  - title: "Watch the readiness signs building — but don't start on them yet"
    detail: "The point of this month is to learn what 'ready for solids' looks like so you'll recognise it around six months. The signals to watch for: sitting up with support and holding his head steady and well; the tongue-thrust reflex fading so food isn't automatically pushed back out (the AAP says he can 'move food from a spoon into their throat'); real interest — watching your food, reaching for it, opening his mouth; and, as a rough marker, having roughly doubled his birth weight (about 13 lb / 5.9 kg). Seeing one or two of these at five months is normal and expected — it means the countdown is on, not that today is the day. When several line up around the half-year mark, that's your green light, and a quick word with your provider seals it."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/Starting-Solid-Foods.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed47m.html" }
  - title: "Why six months, and what changes then"
    detail: "Waiting until around six months lets his gut and swallowing mature and lowers the odds of early-feeding hiccups, while milk still covers everything nutritionally until then. After six months the calculus shifts: Nemours notes that beyond that point 'babies need the added nutrition — like iron and zinc — that solid foods provide,' since the iron a baby is born with starts running low. That's why iron-rich first foods matter later — but that's a six-month conversation. For now, milk has it covered."

sleep:
  - title: "Roughly 12–16 hours, consolidating into a shape"
    detail: "Nemours puts total sleep at about 12–16 hours a day now, 'which includes a longer stretch at night and at least two naps during the day,' with daytime sleep averaging around 3–4 hours. Naps are sorting themselves out — many babies settle toward two or three more predictable naps rather than the scattered newborn catnaps. By six months, Nemours notes, 'most babies are sleeping at night for 9 hours or longer, with brief awakenings,' and most no longer need a middle-of-the-night feed, though plenty — especially breastfed babies — still wake. Watch his mood more than the totals: a mostly cheerful baby is getting what he needs."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "A short, same-every-night bedtime routine pays off"
    detail: "This is a good age to lean into a simple, repeatable wind-down — Nemours recommends 'soothing activities that lead up to \"night-night\" time,' like a warm bath followed by a story or a song. The predictability itself is the sleep cue: the same few steps in the same order tell his body it's time to power down. Aim to put him down drowsy-but-awake when you can, so falling asleep in the crib becomes the normal way it happens rather than something that only works in your arms."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep47m.html" }
  - title: "Rolling in the crib can shake up sleep — and that's okay"
    detail: "Once he can roll, he'll do it in the crib, sometimes waking himself mid-manoeuvre or getting stuck on his tummy and protesting. You still always place him down on his back to start, but the current AAP guidance is that once a baby can roll both ways on his own you don't need to flip him back all night. Keep the sleep space bare — firm flat mattress, fitted sheet, and nothing else: no bumpers, pillows, blankets, or positioners. A new-skill sleep wobble (rolling, or teething, or a nap in transition) usually settles within a week or two."
    sources:

safetyOnTheMove:
  - title: "A baby who can roll is never safe on a raised surface"
    detail: "The single biggest safety shift this month is mobility: a baby who rolls (or is about to) can go from still to off-the-edge in a second, so the change table, adult bed, and couch are no longer places to step away from — keep a hand on him or change him on a mat on the floor. The AAP's guidance for this new-movement stage is to childproof ahead of the skill, not after the first fall. Assume every new ability arrives a little before you expect it, and set the floor up as his safest zone now."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-4-to-7-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Everything goes in the mouth — get serious about small objects"
    detail: "With raking hands and a mouth-first way of exploring, choking risk climbs fast. The AAP flags coins, marbles, button batteries, small balls, deflated balloons, pen caps, and small toy parts as the top hazards, and advises checking 'under furniture and between cushions' where these hide. A useful rule: if an object fits through a toilet-paper tube (roughly under 1¼ inches / 3 cm across), it's a choking hazard — keep it off the floor and out of reach. Do a floor sweep at his eye level, keep older siblings' small toys separated, and cut any food you offer later into pieces 'no larger than one-half inch.'"
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/health-issues/injuries-emergencies/Pages/Choking-Prevention.aspx" }
  - title: "Lower the crib mattress"
    detail: "As he gets stronger at pushing up and sitting, a crib mattress on a high setting becomes a fall risk — a baby who can lever himself up needs the mattress dropped. Move it to a lower setting now (and to the lowest before he can pull to stand in the coming months), following your crib's own instructions, so the distance from mattress to rail top keeps him safely inside. It's a five-minute job that's much easier to do a little early than after a scare."
    sources:

  - title: "In Canada, skip the wheeled walker entirely"
    detail: "A quick heads-up before the gear temptation kicks in: mobile baby walkers with wheels have been banned in Canada since 2004 — it's illegal to sell, import, or even give one away, including second-hand or with the wheels removed — because they let a baby reach hazards and stairs at speed. Health Canada still finds and recalls them from online sellers, so if someone offers you one, pass. A stationary activity centre (no wheels) is the legal, safer alternative when you want a hands-free spot for him — see the gear section below for how to use one well."

healthAndCheckups:
  - title: "No routine checkup this month in Alberta"
    detail: "Alberta's well-child schedule runs at roughly 3–5 days, by one month, then 2, 4, 6, 9, 12, 15, and 18 months — so there's no scheduled fifth-month visit; the calendar jumps from the four-month appointment straight to six months. That's normal and expected: five months is a settle-in stretch between checkups, not a gap in care. If anything is nagging you before the six-month visit, you don't have to wait — call Health Link 811 any time, or book with your provider."

redFlags:
  - title: "Movement that seems off — mention it at the visit"
    detail: "Ranges are wide and missing one marker rarely means a problem, but Nemours flags a few things worth raising with the doctor: by four months not holding his head steady when held, not holding a toy placed in his hand, and not pushing up during tummy time; and by around six months not rolling at all, not reaching for objects, and not being able to lean on his hands when propped to sit. Muscle tone that looks extreme in either direction — a baby who feels very stiff and tight, or very floppy and rag-doll-like — belongs on that list too. None of these is an emergency; they're conversation-starters for the six-month checkup."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Teething can start now — but it doesn't cause real fevers"
    detail: "Drooling by the bucket, gnawing on fists and toys, sore swollen gums, and extra fussiness can all mean a first tooth is on the way. MyHealth Alberta puts the first tooth at about six months on average, 'as early as 3 months or as late as 1 year,' with fussiness often starting a few days before it breaks through — so a five-month teether is right on time. To soothe: a clean cold (not frozen) teething ring or damp washcloth, or gently rubbing the gum with a clean finger for a minute or two; an age-appropriate dose of infant acetaminophen if he's really miserable. Skip numbing gels (babies swallow them) and amber teething necklaces (strangulation and choking risk). One caveat worth remembering: teething may nudge his temperature up slightly, but it does not cause a true fever — a real fever needs to be checked, not written off as teeth."
