weekNumber: 1
title: "The first days"
description: "The first week is about surviving the shock, keeping baby fed, and learning to read his cues. Almost nothing has a schedule yet."
milestones:
  - title: "Reflexes present"
    detail: "Rooting, sucking, grasp, Moro (startle), and stepping reflexes are all present at birth, and they're what the pediatrician checks at the first visit because they're a quick window into how the nervous system is wiring up. You can spot them yourself: stroke his cheek and he turns toward it with an open mouth (rooting), press a finger into his palm and he grips hard (grasp), and a sudden noise makes him fling his arms out and pull them back in (Moro). They're involuntary — he isn't 'startled awake because you did something wrong' — and most fade over the first few months as deliberate movement takes over."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Newborn-Reflexes.aspx"
  - title: "Vision at about 8–12 inches"
    detail: "He sees best at about 8–12 inches (20–30 cm) — almost exactly the distance from his eyes to your face when you're holding or feeding him, which is no accident. Beyond that everything is blurry, and he'll pick faces and high-contrast patterns over anything else, so your face is genuinely the best toy in the house this week. His eyes may also drift or cross now and then; that's normal at this age as the eye muscles learn to coordinate."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Babys-Vision-Development.aspx"
  - title: "Regains birth weight by ~day 10–14"
    detail: "Losing weight in the first days is expected, not a failure: healthy newborns typically drop 7–10% of their birth weight as they shed extra fluid and run on small colostrum volumes, then regain it within about two weeks. The pediatrician tracks this at the first visit, which is why you bring the discharge paperwork with the birth and discharge weights. As long as the curve turns around by the end of the first week and he's feeding and peeing well, the early dip is nothing to lose sleep over — you'll be doing enough of that anyway."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/grownewborn.html"
todos:
  - title: "First pediatrician visit at 3–5 days old"
    detail: "This visit is a weight check, a jaundice look-over, and a feeding review, plus follow-up on the hearing and blood-spot screens done in hospital. Book it before you leave the hospital if you can — day 3–5 is exactly when jaundice peaks and milk supply is establishing, so the timing matters. Bring the hospital paperwork with birth and discharge weights, and expect them to ask how the parents are doing too; that question is part of the checkup, not small talk."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/Your-Childs-Checkups/Pages/Your-Checkup-Checklist-Newborn-Visit-2-to-5-days-old.aspx"
  - title: "Register the birth"
    detail: "In Alberta this is done online through Vital Statistics, and the person who gave birth is the one who has to complete the form (a co-parent certifies alongside). Do it in the first two weeks — processing takes about 2–3 weeks, and the tracking number you get on submission is what lets you order the birth certificate afterward without extra hassle. The hospital usually gives you a packet pointing at the same process."
    sources:
      - label: "Alberta.ca"
        url: "https://www.alberta.ca/register-birth"
  - title: "Add baby to your provincial health card and to any private insurance"
    detail: "For AHCIP, fill out the Notice of Change (Addition) form and bring it to a participating registry office with the supporting documents — there's no charge. Do private insurance in the same sitting: most plans have a 30-day window from birth to add a newborn without medical underwriting, and it's an easy one to let slip in the newborn fog. Ten minutes now saves a genuinely annoying paperwork fight later."
    sources:
      - label: "Alberta.ca"

  - title: "Install and check the car seat before discharge"
    detail: "Rear-facing, tightly anchored (less than 2.5 cm of wiggle at the belt path), harness snug enough that you can't pinch a fold of strap at the shoulder, chest clip at armpit level. Skip the puffy suit under the harness — it compresses in a crash and leaves the straps loose; dress him in thin layers and tuck a blanket over the buckled harness instead. If you want a second set of eyes, AHS runs child car seat education clinics and the hospital may check the install before you leave."

feeding:
  - title: "Feed on demand, every 1–3 hours"
    detail: "8–12 feeds in 24 hours is typical for the first weeks, day and night — that's every 1–3 hours counted from the start of one feed to the start of the next, which is why it feels relentless. Feed on early hunger cues (rooting, hands to mouth, lip-smacking, squirming); crying is a late cue, and a frantic baby latches worse. There's no 10-minutes-per-side rule — let him finish the first breast at his own pace so he gets the fattier milk at the end. Cluster feeding in the evening, where he wants the breast almost continuously for a stretch, is normal behaviour and not a sign your supply is failing."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/How-Often-and-How-Much-Should-Your-Baby-Eat.aspx"
  - title: "A deep latch is the fix for most early feeding pain"
    detail: "Line him up nose-to-nipple, wait for a wide-open mouth, and bring him to the breast (not the breast to him) so he takes a big mouthful of areola with his chin pressed in and lips flanged out. A brief pinch as he latches can be normal; pain that lasts the whole feed means the latch is shallow — break the suction with a clean finger in the corner of his mouth and start again rather than toughing it out. Getting this right in week 1 is the single biggest predictor of breastfeeding going well, so if it keeps hurting, ask for a lactation consultant now, not at the six-week mark."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"

  - title: "Watch for at least 6 wet diapers per day by day 5"
    detail: "Diapers are the honest scoreboard for intake, since you can't see how many millilitres went in. A rough rule for the first days: about as many wet diapers as baby is days old (1 on day 1, 2 on day 2…), reaching 6 or more wet diapers per 24 hours by day 5 and staying there. Stools should shift from black, tarry meconium to green-brown and then to soft mustard-yellow by around day 5, usually 3–4 or more a day early on. Fewer wets than that, or stools stuck at meconium, means a same-day call to the pediatrician or a lactation consultant — early feeding problems fix fastest when caught early."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"
  - title: "Colostrum first, then milk 'comes in' around day 3–5"
    detail: "The first milk is colostrum — thick, yellowish, and produced in teaspoon-sized amounts, which is genuinely all he needs because a day-1 stomach holds roughly 5–7 ml (about the size of a cherry). Around day 3–5 the mature milk arrives, usually announced by noticeably fuller, heavier breasts and a baby who suddenly gulps and swallows audibly. Frequent feeding in these first days is what signals your body to make that transition, so all that nursing is doing double duty: feeding him now and building supply for later. If your milk hasn't come in within about 72 hours (day 3), talk to your doctor or a lactation consultant rather than waiting it out."
    sources:
      - label: "LLLI"
        url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/colostrum-general/"
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"
  - title: "Burp partway through and after each feed"
    detail: "Newborns swallow air with milk, and a trapped bubble can end a feed early or come back up as spit-up on your third shirt of the day. Pause to burp when switching breasts (or halfway through a bottle) and again at the end: over the shoulder, sitting on your lap with his chin supported in your hand, or face-down across your lap — firm pats or upward rubs on the back, with a cloth wherever his mouth points. If nothing comes up after a few minutes, that's fine too; not every feed produces a burp, and you don't need to keep pounding away at it."

sleep:
  - title: "14–17 hours of sleep spread across the day and night"
    detail: "Expect around 16–18 hours of sleep in every 24, but in 2–4 hour fragments with zero respect for the clock — newborns have no circadian rhythm yet, and it won't start organizing for several weeks. Day/night confusion (party at 3 a.m., comatose at noon) is standard-issue this week; you can gently nudge things by keeping days bright and interactive and nights dark, quiet, and boring, but don't expect results yet. The only workable survival strategy is sleeping in shifts or when he sleeps, not waiting for a 'real night' that isn't coming this week."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleepnewborn.html"
  - title: "Back to sleep, on a firm flat surface, nothing loose in the crib"
    detail: "Every sleep — naps included — goes on his back on a firm, flat mattress with just a fitted sheet. Nothing else in the sleep space: no pillows, bumpers, loose blankets, stuffed toys, or wedge/positioner products, until 12 months. This is the strongest-evidence safety guidance in all of infant care (it's what drove SIDS rates down dramatically), and it holds even if he seems to settle better on his tummy — that settling is exactly why tummy sleeping is riskier. Side sleeping doesn't count as a safe compromise either; back only."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies"
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.aap.org/en/patient-care/safe-sleep/"
  - title: "Room-share (not bed-share) for the first 6 months"
    detail: "A bassinet or crib in your room, close enough to reach, cuts SIDS risk substantially — the AAP puts it at up to half — while keeping night feeds short-distance. Bed-sharing is the part that adds risk (adult mattresses, duvets, and exhausted parents don't mix with newborns), so the safest version of the 3 a.m. feed is: feed in your bed if you like, then put him back in his own space before you drift off. If you think you might fall asleep feeding, the adult bed with no loose bedding nearby is still safer than a couch or armchair, which are the riskiest spots of all."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx"
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies"
  - title: "Swaddling can calm him — snug arms, loose hips, always on his back"
    detail: "A good swaddle recreates the snugness of the womb and damps the Moro reflex that keeps startling him awake. The mechanics: arms snug at his sides or across his chest, but the wrap loose enough at the bottom that his legs can bend up and out at the hips (tight legs stress the hip joints), and the fabric well clear of his face. Two hard rules: a swaddled baby sleeps only on his back, and swaddling stops at the first sign of attempted rolling — usually somewhere around 2 months, so you have time. Skip it if the room is warm and he seems hot; overheating is its own risk."
    sources:

        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/diapers-clothing/Pages/Swaddling-Is-it-Safe.aspx"

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever (rectal temp ≥ 38.0°C / 100.4°F)"
    detail: "Any fever in a baby under 3 months is a same-day medical assessment — no exceptions, no waiting to see if it comes down. Use a rectal thermometer for this age; it's the only reading accurate enough to make the call, and armpit or forehead readings that look borderline should be confirmed rectally. Don't give fever medicine first and see how he does: a newborn fever needs to be assessed as-is, because at this age it can be the only visible sign of a serious infection. When in doubt, HealthLink at 811 is a 24/7 Alberta nurse line."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking"
  - title: "Yellow skin or eyes worsening after day 5"
    detail: "Mild jaundice is very common and typically peaks around day 3–5 before fading — that's why the first checkup is timed for exactly this window. Call if the yellow is deepening after day 5, spreading down to the belly and legs, or paired with a sleepy baby who's feeding poorly; those are the signs bilirubin may be climbing instead of clearing. If it's needed, treatment is usually just phototherapy (a stint under blue lights), which works quickly and well — this is a very fixable problem when caught."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/jaundice_in_newborns"
  - title: "Won't wake to feed, or feeds < every 4 hours"
    detail: "Needing to wake a sleepy newborn for feeds is normal in the first few days — do it, even though waking a sleeping baby feels criminal. The red flag is a baby who's genuinely hard to rouse, too sleepy to finish feeds, or going longer than about 4 hours between feeds and dropping below 8 feeds in 24 hours. 'Such a good sleeper' in week 1 can actually be an underfed baby conserving energy, which is why this one is worth a call rather than a wait-and-see."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/feeding-nutrition/Pages/How-Often-and-How-Much-Should-Your-Baby-Eat.aspx"
  - title: "Fewer than 6 wet diapers by day 5"
    detail: "This is the clearest early sign of inadequate intake, and it pairs with the other soft signs: fewer than 3–4 stools a day, stools still black and tarry past day 4–5, or dark 'brick dust' staining in the diaper persisting past the first days. Call the pediatrician or a lactation consultant the same day — the usual fix is a feeding adjustment (latch, frequency, or a supplementation plan), and it works best started early. Track diapers on paper or an app for the first two weeks; sleep-deprived memory is not a measuring instrument."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/breastfeeding"
  - title: "Persistent vomiting (not spit-up), especially green or bloody"
    detail: "Spit-up is common and mostly a laundry problem: it dribbles or comes up with a burp, and the baby is unbothered. Vomiting is forceful — the feed comes back with real projection — and it's a different category. Green (bile-stained) or bloody vomit, or repeated forceful vomiting, needs urgent same-day assessment, because in a newborn it can signal a blockage that's very treatable but time-sensitive. One impressive spit-up after a big feed is not this; a pattern of forceful or discoloured vomiting is."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The first-week baby blues are extremely common"
    detail: "Up to 80% of new mothers get the baby blues — tearfulness, mood swings, and feeling overwhelmed, usually starting day 1–3 and fading within about two weeks. It's hormonal and sleep-deprivation driven, not a verdict on you as a parent, and the treatment is support, food, and any sleep you can get. If it persists past two weeks, deepens instead of lifting, or includes hopelessness, panic, inability to sleep even when baby sleeps, or intrusive thoughts, that's postpartum depression territory (roughly 1 in 8 new mothers) — call your provider, because it's common and it's treatable. Partners: you're the early-warning system here, since the person in it often can't see it."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/depression_in_pregnant_women_and_mothers"
  - title: "Skin-to-skin is genuinely regulating for both of you"
    detail: "A diapered baby on a bare chest, blanket over both of you — that's the whole technique, and it measurably stabilises his temperature, heart rate, breathing, and blood sugar while supporting feeding and bonding. It also settles the adult: skin-to-skin lowers parental stress hormones, and it works just as well on a partner's chest as the birthing parent's, which makes it the best possible job to hand the non-feeding parent. There's no dose limit and no schedule — do it as much as you both want, this week and for months to come."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/preemie/Pages/About-Skin-to-Skin-Care.aspx"
  - title: "Sponge baths only until the cord stump falls off"
    detail: "The umbilical stump needs to stay clean and dry, so skip tub baths and wipe him down with a warm, damp cloth instead — face and hands first, diaper area last. Fold the diaper below the stump so urine doesn't soak it, let it air when you can, and leave it alone otherwise; it dries up and falls off on its own in roughly 1–3 weeks. Two or three sponge baths a week is plenty at this age — newborns don't get dirty, and over-bathing just dries their skin. Call the doctor if the skin around the stump turns red, oozes pus, or smells bad."
    sources:
      - label: "AAP"
        url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/bathing-skin-care/Pages/Umbilical-Cord-Care.aspx"
