weekNumber: 6
title: "The six-week turn"
description: "Week six is the hinge — crying is cresting at its loudest right as the first real smiles arrive, a growth spurt has him feeding like a marathoner, and it's the birthing parent's postpartum-checkup week."
milestones:
  - title: "The first real social smile — for many babies, right about now"
    detail: "Somewhere in the 6-to-8-week window most babies produce their first genuine social smile — aimed at you, in response to your face or voice, using the whole face and not just the mouth. It's the reward the last month has been quietly saving up for. Ranges are wide and normal: some happy babies don't switch it on until 7 or 8 weeks, and once it starts, some are simply smilier than others. You can coax it out — catch an alert, content moment, bring your face to about 20–30 cm, smile big, then pause and give him a turn — but you can't force it, so if it hasn't landed this week it's genuinely not a concern yet."

  - title: "Cooing, gurgling, and the first back-and-forth 'conversations'"
    detail: "Those breathy vowel sounds — 'oooo', 'aahh' — are cooing, and around now you'll notice they're aimed at you: he makes a sound, and if you answer it and wait, he'll often make another. That turn-taking is the actual foundation of language, months before any real word. Feed it: repeat his sounds back, narrate what you're doing, read aloud, and leave gaps for his 'reply.' There's no wrong material and no such thing as too much — the grocery list in a warm, high-pitched sing-song counts as a language lesson."

  - title: "Steadier head, and eyes that track further"
    detail: "On his tummy he's beginning to push up onto his forearms and hold his head up longer, and held upright he can keep his head fairly steady for a moment before it bobs — still support it whenever you lift or carry him. His eyes are tracking better too: he'll follow a slowly moving toy or your face across more of his field of view, now often past the midline instead of losing it at the centre. Give him things to follow at about 20–30 cm and move them slowly; short, frequent tummy-time sessions are what build the neck and shoulder strength behind all of this."

  - title: "More awake, more social, and starting to know you"
    detail: "By six weeks the awake windows are longer and noticeably more sociable — he watches your face intently, calms toward your voice, and turns his head to find where a sound came from. He isn't just tolerating company anymore; he's starting to seek it, which is the first hint of the person emerging. Use the alert stretches for face-to-face play and talking, and take his cues: when he turns away, glazes over, or fusses, that's 'I've had enough for now,' not a rejection."

todos:
  - title: "Lock in the two-month immunization appointment"
    detail: "Alberta's first routine vaccines are due at 2 months and given at public/community health centres, not usually the family doctor's office: DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (one shot covering diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, polio, Hib, and hepatitis B), pneumococcal conjugate, and rotavirus (an oral vaccine, not a needle). If it isn't booked yet, call your local public health office or Health Link 811 this week and have his Alberta health card number ready. Getting it done on time also starts winding down the strict germ-bubble rules — the first shots are the milestone most families relax after."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Topic/Immunization/pages/routine-schedule.aspx" }
  - title: "The birthing parent's postpartum checkup is this week's headline"
    detail: "The complete postpartum visit typically happens around 4 to 6 weeks after delivery, so week six is often exactly it. Expect a check on physical recovery (including the incision if it was a C-section), a mood and depression screen — frequently the 10-question Edinburgh scale — a conversation about how feeding is going, and, yes, a real discussion of contraception, since pregnancy is possible again even before your period returns and even while breastfeeding. Bring your own list; this appointment is for the parent, and 'I'm not okay' is exactly what it's for. If mood has already been low for more than a couple of weeks, call now rather than waiting for the date."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=abl1277" }
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=tn9653" }
  - title: "Get ready for the two-month well-child visit"
    detail: "The baby's 2-month checkup is close, and it's a big one: weight, length, and head circumference plotted on the growth curve, a look at development and behaviour, a maternal-depression screen for the parent, feeding and safety questions — and the first round of vaccines is usually given at the same visit. Jot your questions down beforehand (they evaporate the second you're in the room), and count nothing too small to ask: spit-up, crying, rashes, sleep, and your own coping all belong on the list."
    sources:

feeding:
  - title: "The classic six-week growth spurt — marathon feeding, not low supply"
    detail: "Right on cue, many babies hit a growth spurt around six weeks: a day or two (sometimes up to three) of a hungrier, fussier baby who wants to nurse almost constantly, often clustering the feeds into the evening. This is the mechanism working exactly as designed, not a failure — frequent nursing is the signal that tells your body to make more milk, and supply climbs to meet the new demand within a couple of days. It is not a sign your milk is 'drying up' or 'not enough.' Ride it out by following his cues and feeding on demand; formula-fed babies may simply take a little more per bottle for a few days."
    sources:
      - { label: "LLLI", url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/growth/" }
      - { label: "LLLI", url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/frequency-feeding-frequently-asked-questions-faqs/" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Between spurts, feeds may start to spread out a little"
    detail: "Once the growth spurt passes, breastfed babies still typically feed 8–12 times in 24 hours, though the intervals may stretch toward every 3–4 hours, and formula-fed babies usually take 3–4 oz (90–120 ml) every 3–4 hours. Keep watching the baby rather than the clock: rooting, hand-to-mouth, and stirring mean feed soon; crying is the late cue, not the first. Wet diapers are still the report card — six or more soaked ones a day says intake is fine. If it's working, change nothing."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
      - { label: "LLLI", url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/frequency-feeding-frequently-asked-questions-faqs/" }
  - title: "Breastfeeding is 'well established' now — which changes a few things"
    detail: "By around six weeks, for most pairs, breastfeeding is established: comfortable latch, reliable supply, steady weight gain. That's the milestone that makes a couple of other things reasonable — introducing an occasional bottle of expressed milk if you want feeding flexibility, and offering a pacifier at sleep times. If feeding still hurts, or you dread it, or you're worried about weight, that's a fixable problem and it's easier to fix now than later: ask for a lactation-consultant referral, call Health Link 811 any hour, or reach a La Leche League leader for free support."

sleep:
  - title: "Sleep may consolidate slightly — one longer stretch appears"
    detail: "Babies this age still sleep about 14–17 hours across 24, but around now the pieces may start to gather: some offer one longer run at night after a late feeding, with 2–3 naps in the day. Keep the expectations honest — at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means a 5–6 hour stretch, and even that is hit-or-miss for weeks yet. His sleep cycle is slowly drifting closer to yours; the rhythm builds over the coming months, not overnight this week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Keep days bright and loud, nights dark and boring"
    detail: "The fastest way to help that longer night stretch settle in is to sharpen the contrast between day and night: daytime feeds and diaper changes bright, chatty, and social; night ones dim, quiet, and dull, with minimal eye contact and no playtime. Plenty of six-week-olds still have day and night partly muddled, which is normal and self-corrects. You're not training him so much as giving his emerging body clock a clear signal about which one is which."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep rules do not change"
    detail: "Back for every sleep — naps included — on a firm, flat surface, with nothing loose in the space: no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners, and dress him for the room instead of adding covers. Keep his crib or bassinet in your room but not in your bed; room-sharing for at least the first 6 months is linked to a substantially lower SIDS risk, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. If he falls asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies" }
redFlags:
  - title: "Any fever is still an emergency at this age"
    detail: "A rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher in a baby under 3 months means immediate medical care — this rule does not relax for months yet, first vaccines or not. A rectal reading is the accurate method at this age, so it's worth owning a digital thermometer and knowing the technique before you need it at 2 a.m. Don't give fever medicine and wait to see how it goes; a young baby with a fever needs to be seen."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Postpartum depression or anxiety — in either parent"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, relentless anxiety or panic, or trouble bonding that lasts longer is postpartum depression or anxiety — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. Six weeks is precisely when it tends to surface and precisely when the postpartum checkup screens for it, so expect the mood questions and answer them honestly — being asked is the system working, not an accusation. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811. Any thought of harming yourself or the baby means call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."
    sources:

  - title: "Crying that's pushing you past your limit"
    detail: "This is the loudest week for many babies, and it's the week to have the plan ready before the rough evening, not during it. If the crying is taking you to the edge, put the baby down on his back somewhere safe like the crib, close the door, and step away for a few minutes to breathe — a crying baby alone in a safe crib is fine; an exhausted adult at the breaking point is the real danger. Never, ever shake, hit, or throw a baby: shaking can cause lifelong brain injury or death in seconds. Then tag someone in — your partner, a friend, or Health Link 811."
    sources:

  - title: "Not feeding or growing, forceful vomiting, or 'not himself'"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful vomiting (different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal), diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual or hard to rouse. By six weeks you know his baseline better than anyone, and 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate clinical observation that Health Link 811 will take seriously at any hour. Bloody stools mean seek care right away."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "This is the peak of the crying curve — you're at the top, not the start"
    detail: "Intense, hard-to-soothe crying typically runs from about 3 to 12 weeks and is loudest for many babies right around now, then eases and is usually over by 3 to 4 months — this is the 'Period of PURPLE Crying' (Peak pattern, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, Evening clustering). A baby who cries a lot this week is usually a completely normal baby sitting at the top of a normal curve; it isn't caused by anything you're doing, and some of it can't be soothed away by anyone. Knowing the shape is protective — the far side of this hill is real, and it's close."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }
  - title: "Parent tip: guard one protected sleep block, on purpose"
    detail: "If there are two of you, assign each parent one guarded 3–4 hour sleep block a day — earplugs in, door shut, fully off duty, not listening with one ear. At the crying peak, sleep is the cheapest and most effective mental-health intervention you have, and relentless broken sleep is one of the things that feeds postpartum depression and anxiety in both parents. Treat the block like a work shift: it isn't selfish, it's maintenance on the only two people running this operation."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=tn9653" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/depression_in_pregnant_women_and_mothers" }
  - title: "A baby-safe mirror is this month's favourite new toy"
    detail: "Now that he tracks faces and holds his head up a little, a soft-framed, baby-safe mirror propped during tummy time is oddly captivating — he has no idea it's him, but that face smiling back is endlessly interesting and coaxes a few extra minutes of head-lifting practice. The same goes for high-contrast patterns, rattles pressed into his palm, and a play gym overhead. Vary the position of the mirror — in front, then to each side — to invite him to turn and track."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn13m.html" }
  - title: "The germ bubble has an end date now"
    detail: "The strict hand-washing and no-sick-visitors rules matter most in these early weeks, while his immune system is immature and he hasn't had his first shots. Most families loosen up gradually after the 2-month vaccines — one more reason to keep that appointment — and outdoor outings were never the risky part in the first place. If a relative grumbles about the rules, blame this website; that's partly what it's for."
    sources:
