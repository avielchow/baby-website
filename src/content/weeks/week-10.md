weekNumber: 10
title: "Ten weeks — chuckles, chatter, and reaching hands"
description: "Coos are turning into back-and-forth 'conversations,' a first chuckle may sneak out, hands are starting to reach and grab — and the drool has arrived, which almost certainly isn't teething yet."
milestones:
  - title: "A first chuckle may be around the corner"
    detail: "Somewhere in here many babies add a new sound to the repertoire: a chuckle — not yet a full belly laugh — usually coaxed out when you do something delightfully silly and repeat it. Nemours puts the chuckle right around the four-month mark, so at ten weeks a baby who's already doing it is early and a baby who isn't is completely on time; the wide range is the point, not a stopwatch. You invite it the same way you invited the first smiles: find an alert, happy stretch, get in close, and be a bit ridiculous — a funny noise, a gentle raspberry, the same peekaboo beat over and over. The reward, when it lands, is one of the best sounds you'll ever hear."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-4mos.html" }
  - title: "Cooing, vocal play, and real back-and-forth 'conversations'"
    detail: "The breathy vowels are getting richer — long strings of ooh and aah, gurgles, squeals, and experiments with pitch — and crucially he's starting to make sounds back in response to being spoken to, turning his head toward your voice and even making noises to get and keep your attention. This is the beginning of turn-taking: coo at him, then pause and give him a real beat to answer, and you're running a genuine conversation with the timing of speech long before the words show up. Answer his sounds by repeating them back and adding a simple word, and keep up the singsong, high-pitched 'parentese' — the exaggerated melody genuinely helps him tune into language. He's also reading your face now, so let it be expressive."

  - title: "Reaching, swiping, and holding on"
    detail: "Hands are becoming tools. Around now babies use their arms to swing at a dangling toy, will hold a rattle or toy briefly when you place it in the palm, and reliably bring both hands together and up to the mouth at midline. The aim is still wildly imprecise — he'll bat and miss far more than he connects — but every swing is practice for the hand-eye coordination that leads to a deliberate grab in the coming weeks. Give it something to work on: hang a light, high-contrast toy within arm's reach over the play mat, and tuck an easy-to-hold rattle into his hand so he feels the cause-and-effect of his own movement. Expect nearly everything he grabs to head straight for the mouth — that's how he investigates the world right now."

  - title: "Stronger push-ups, a steadier head, and smoother tracking"
    detail: "On his tummy he's graduating from lifting the head to pushing up on his forearms and elbows, lifting the chest, and holding the position long enough to look around — the core and neck work that builds toward rolling. Off the tummy, most babies this age can hold the head fairly steady when you hold them upright, and the wobble that made you cradle every lift is fading (support it still). His eyes are working more smoothly too: he'll follow a slowly moving face or toy across his field of view and study his own hands with new fascination. Move slowly side to side to give his eyes something to track, and keep offering that upright 'look at the world' view he now loves."

todos:
  - title: "Make tummy time a daily, several-times-a-day habit"
    detail: "This is the single most useful thing you can do for his motor development right now, and it only works if it's frequent. The AAP's guidance is to build gradually — short two-to-five-minute sessions a few times a day, working toward a total of roughly 15–30 minutes across the day by around this age — always supervised and always on his back for sleep, never the tummy. Keep it from becoming a fight by getting down on the floor face-to-face, using a mirror or a high-contrast toy just ahead of him, or starting chest-to-chest while you lie back if he protests the floor. A little fussing is normal; it's hard work, and the strength he's banking now is what rolling, sitting, and crawling are built on."
    sources:

  - title: "Talk, narrate, and get on the floor face-to-face"
    detail: "The best developmental toy in the house is your face and your voice, and the way to use them is to narrate — say what's happening as it happens ('I'm warming your bottle,' 'here comes the blue sock'), name what he looks at, and answer every coo as if it were a real remark. Get down on the floor at his level for daily face-to-face play; the AAP encourages exactly this back-and-forth talking to grow language, and it costs nothing. A few minutes of unhurried, screen-free, one-on-one attention several times a day does more than any app or flashcard. When he 'serves' with a sound or a look, 'return' it with words and a smile — that loop is how the brain wiring for language and connection gets built."

  - title: "Get ahead of the drool"
    detail: "The drool ramping up now is normal (more on why below), but wet skin left sitting against the chin, neck folds, and chest can cause a red, chapped drool rash. Keep a few soft bibs in rotation to catch it and change them when they're soaked, pat — don't rub — the chin dry through the day, and a thin smear of a plain barrier ointment on the chin at night protects the skin. This is a laundry-and-bibs chore, not a medical problem, and it comes and goes for months. If a rash looks angry, weepy, or isn't settling with basic care, it's worth a mention to your provider or Health Link 811."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeds keep spreading out — watch the baby, not the clock"
    detail: "Intake is still generous but the schedule keeps relaxing: breastfed babies commonly nurse around 8–10 times in 24 hours, and formula-fed babies are often taking roughly 4–6 oz (120–180 ml) per feed, six or so times a day, with the gaps stretching compared to the newborn weeks. Numbers are averages, not targets — a baby who wants a bit more or less at a given feed is self-regulating, exactly what you want. Read the early hunger cues (stirring, rooting, hands to the mouth) rather than waiting for the late one, crying, since a frantic baby is harder to feed. You don't need to count ounces to know it's working — steady weight gain on the growth curve, roughly six or more wet diapers a day, and a baby who settles after feeds are the report card."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/formula-feeding/Pages/Amount-and-Schedule-of-Formula-Feedings.aspx" }

  - title: "All that drool doesn't change the menu"
    detail: "It's still all breast milk or formula — the extra saliva and the constant hands-to-mouth chewing are not signs he's ready for solids or a bottle of anything else. Solids aren't recommended until around six months, and there's no more nutritionally complete food for him right now than milk. If mealtimes have gotten mistier at the breast or bottle because he's distracted by his own hands, a calm, low-stimulation spot to feed usually helps. If feeding still hurts, feels off, or you're worried about supply or weight, a lactation consultant or Health Link 811 can help — earlier is easier."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
sleep:
  - title: "A longer night stretch may be showing up"
    detail: "Babies this age still sleep roughly 14–17 hours across a full day, usually split into three or four daytime naps plus the longer night portion, but many start offering one longer run at night around now. Keep your expectations calibrated: at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means only a 5–6 hour stretch, and plenty of ten-week-olds — especially breastfed ones — still wake once or twice to feed, which is normal and often still needed. Keep days bright, social, and full of daylight and nights dark, quiet, and boring; the day-night rhythm is built over the coming months, not forced this week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep rules haven't changed — even as he gets stronger"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, naps included, on a firm, flat surface, in his own crib or bassinet in your room, with nothing loose in the space — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners — and dress him for the room instead of adding covers. Room-sharing without bed-sharing for at least the first six months is associated with a substantially lower risk of SIDS. His new strength doesn't change any of this: if he drools all over a wedge or falls asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can, and never leave a teether or anything else in the sleep space."
    sources:

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever still means being seen — he's under three months"
    detail: "At ten weeks he's still under three months old, so the strict newborn fever rule applies with no exceptions for drool or 'teething': a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher needs medical attention right away, not a wait-and-see. Do not chalk a fever up to teething — the first teeth are still months off for most babies, and true teething doesn't cause a real fever anyway. Take the temperature rectally for accuracy at this age, and call Health Link 811 or seek care immediately if he's hot, and always for a fever with a baby who seems genuinely unwell."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=stt11260&lang=en-ca" }
  - title: "Not himself: poor feeding, forceful vomiting, or hard to wake"
    detail: "Call for feeding refusal or worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful (projectile) vomiting — different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal — diarrhea, far fewer wet diapers or other signs of dehydration, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual and hard to rouse. By now you know his baseline better than anyone, so 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate reason to call, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Any trouble breathing means seek care immediately."

  - title: "Development worth raising at the next visit"
    detail: "Milestones have wide ranges, so one missing skill this week isn't an alarm — but a cluster is worth a conversation. Flag it if he doesn't watch things as they move or doesn't follow you with his eyes, doesn't react to loud sounds or your voice, isn't bringing his hands to his mouth, can't hold his head steady at all, or seems unusually stiff or floppy. The four-month checkup is the natural place to raise any of these, and the milestone checklist is meant to start that conversation, not to be a test he passes or fails; if something worries you sooner, call and ask."

  - title: "Parent mental health — still worth checking in on"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, or trouble bonding that lasts longer is postpartum depression or anxiety territory — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. It can also surface later in the first year, not just in the newborn haze, so ten weeks in is not too late to notice it. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811 if a low mood is lingering, and treat any thoughts of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency — call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The big drool almost certainly isn't teething"
    detail: "Around now the bibs come out, and it's easy to assume a tooth is on the way — but drooling becomes especially common between about 3 and 6 months as part of the normal, mouth-centered phase of development, when salivary glands ramp up and babies simply haven't learned to swallow the extra saliva yet. Real teething usually comes later: the AAP and Nemours put the first tooth typically between 4 and 7 months, and Alberta's own guidance says most babies get their first tooth around 6 to 10 months. So the drool, the bubble-blowing, and the constant chewing on hands and toys at ten weeks are him exploring with his mouth and managing saliva, not cutting teeth — which is also why a teether right now is really a graspable, gummable soother, not a tooth remedy. Saliva isn't just mess, either: it moistens the mouth, helps him swallow, and protects the gums and future teeth."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/teething-tooth-care/Pages/Drooling-and-Your-Baby.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/teething.html" }
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=stt11260&lang=en-ca" }
  - title: "Everything goes in the mouth — and that's how he learns"
    detail: "Hands, fists, your finger, the corner of a bib, whatever he can grab — it all heads for the mouth right now, because at this stage his mouth is his most sensitive and best-developed tool for investigating the world. This is normal, healthy exploration, not a bad habit to break, and it pairs perfectly with his new reaching skill. Give it safe targets: clean, one-piece objects too big to swallow and with no cords, small parts, or liquid-filled sections, and keep genuinely small or breakable things out of reach now that his aim is improving. Supervise the mouthing, wash the toys, and let him explore."
    sources:

  - title: "You are the best toy — and that takes the pressure off"
    detail: "It's easy to feel you should be buying the developmental toy of the month, but the research-backed truth is simpler: the thing that grows his brain most is unhurried, face-to-face, back-and-forth time with you — talking, singing, responding, playing on the floor. That's genuinely good news for a tired parent, because it means the most valuable thing you can offer is also free and requires no setup. On the days you have nothing left, narrating the dishes while he watches from the mat still counts. Protecting a little of that one-on-one time — and your own rest and sanity so you can show up for it — is the whole game right now."
