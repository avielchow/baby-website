weekNumber: 4
title: "Nearly one month"
description: "Week four is steadier but louder — crying is climbing toward its peak, feeding has (mostly) found a groove, and it's time to book the two-month vaccines."
milestones:
  - title: "Brief head lifts in tummy time"
    detail: "Most babies this age can turn the head from side to side while on the tummy and hold it up for a moment, though it still flops without support — keep a hand behind it when you pick him up. Aim for 2–3 short, supervised sessions a day of about 3–5 minutes each, always awake, working toward roughly 15–30 minutes a day by around 7 weeks. Getting down on the floor face-to-face turns it into a game instead of a workout, and those neck and shoulder muscles are the foundation for rolling and sitting later. If he protests, shorter and more often beats one long miserable session."
    sources:

      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/back-to-sleep-tummy-to-play.aspx" }
  - title: "More alert, and locked onto your face"
    detail: "Focus is still sharpest at 8–12 inches (20–30 cm) — almost exactly feeding distance — and he prefers faces over any other pattern. By the end of the first month most babies are noticeably more alert and curious than they were at two weeks, spending more of the day awake and studying whoever is holding them. Use those windows: bring your face to close range, exaggerate your expressions, and move slowly side to side to invite his eyes to follow. Eyes that occasionally cross or drift are still completely normal at this age."

  - title: "Hearing is fully mature"
    detail: "Hearing is fully developed by one month: he recognizes and reacts to loud sounds — startling at a sudden one is normal, and quietly reassuring — and often calms or turns toward familiar voices, yours above all. Keep narrating your day, reading aloud, and answering his little sounds with sounds of your own; that back-and-forth feels silly but is exactly how language input works at this age. There's no wrong material — the grocery list counts."

  - title: "First real smiles and coos are close"
    detail: "For most babies the genuine social smile arrives around 2 months — often somewhere in the 6-to-8-week window — with early coos and gurgles emerging over the same stretch. The fleeting sleep-smiles you're seeing now are reflexes: the warm-up act. You can set the stage by catching an alert moment, bringing your face close, smiling big, and then pausing to give him a turn. When the first real one lands — aimed at you, unmistakable — it retroactively pays for a lot of 3 a.m. feeds."

todos:
  - title: "Book the two-month immunization appointment now"
    detail: "Alberta's first routine vaccines — DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB, pneumococcal conjugate, and rotavirus — are given at 2 months through public/community health centres, not usually your family doctor's office. Slots around the 2-month mark can book up, so call your local public health office this week, or Health Link 811 if you're not sure which clinic serves your area; have his Alberta health card number handy when you call. Getting it in the calendar now also starts the countdown on the germ-bubble chapter — most of the strict visitor rules relax after this round of shots."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Topic/Immunization/pages/routine-schedule.aspx" }
  - title: "One-month well-child visit, if your provider scheduled one"
    detail: "Expect weight, length, and head circumference plotted on the growth curve, a review of feeding and sleep, and time for your running list of questions. Write the questions down beforehand — they reliably evaporate the moment you're in the exam room — and don't rate anything too small to ask; feeding, spit-up, crying, and rashes are exactly what this visit is for. It's also a fair place to raise how the parents are sleeping and coping, not just the baby."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/Your-Childs-Checkups/Pages/Your-Checkup-Checklist-1-Month-Old.aspx" }
  - title: "Book the 6-week postpartum checkup for the birthing parent"
    detail: "The complete postpartum visit typically happens around 4 to 6 weeks after delivery: physical recovery (including the incision if you had a C-section), a check on mood, how feeding is going, and a conversation about contraception — yes, that's a real topic already. If it isn't on the calendar yet, book it this week. And if mood has already been low for more than a couple of weeks, call now rather than waiting for the appointment — there's no rule that you have to hold out until week six."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=abl1277" }
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=tn9653" }
  - title: "Get outside — and keep the visitor rules"
    detail: "A daily walk is genuinely good for both of you, and open air is far easier on a young immune system than crowded indoor spaces — outdoors was never the risky part. The rules that still earn their keep: anyone holding the baby washes hands first, anyone with sniffles stays home, and strangers can admire but not touch. Where you can, skip enclosed crowds — malls, planes, packed waiting rooms — for a few more weeks, until his immune system matures and the first vaccines are on board."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeding should be feeling established by now"
    detail: "Breastfed babies still typically feed 10–12 times in 24 hours — roughly every 2 hours, counted from the start of one feed to the start of the next — while formula-fed babies usually take 3–4 oz (90–120 ml) every 3–4 hours. Watch the baby rather than the clock: rooting, hand-sucking, and stirring mean feed soon, and crying is the late sign, not the first one. Diapers remain the report card — six or more wet ones a day says intake is fine. If it's working, don't change anything."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
      - { label: "LLLI", url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/frequency-feeding-frequently-asked-questions-faqs/" }
  - title: "A growth spurt is likely around 6 weeks"
    detail: "Expect a day or two of a hungrier, fussier baby who seems to want to feed almost hourly — growth spurts commonly land around six weeks and again near three months, though plenty of babies pick their own week. The extra demand is the mechanism, not a malfunction: more frequent feeding is exactly what tells your supply to increase, and it settles back down within a couple of days. Formula-fed babies may simply take a little more per bottle for a few days. It is not a sign your supply is failing."
    sources:
      - { label: "LLLI", url: "https://llli.org/breastfeeding-info/growth/" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "If feeding still hurts or feels shaky, get help now"
    detail: "Persistent pain, cracked nipples, worries about weight gain, or a sense of dread before feeds are fixable problems — and the fix is easier at four weeks than at eight. Ask your clinic or public health nurse for a lactation consultant referral, call Health Link 811 any hour of the day, or reach out to a La Leche League leader for free breastfeeding support. 'It's technically working but I hate it' fully qualifies as a reason to call."
    sources:

sleep:
  - title: "Maybe one slightly longer stretch — still no schedule"
    detail: "Babies this age sleep about 14–17 hours across a 24-hour day, and some start offering one longer stretch at night around now — though at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means a 5–6 hour run, and even that is months away for many. Plenty of four-week-olds still have day and night partly reversed, which is completely normal. Keep days bright and social and nights dark, quiet, and boring; the rhythm builds over the coming months, not this week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep rules haven't changed"
    detail: "Back for every sleep — naps included — on a firm, flat surface, with nothing loose in the sleep space: no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners, and dress him for the room temperature instead of adding covers. Keep his crib or bassinet in your room without sharing your bed: room-sharing for at least the first 6 months is associated with cutting SIDS risk by as much as half, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. If he falls asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies" }
  - title: "A pacifier at sleep time is on the table now"
    detail: "Once breastfeeding is well established — comfortable, good supply, latching well, gaining weight, which for many pairs is right around now — offering a pacifier at naps and bedtime is associated with a lower risk of SIDS. Offer it as he settles, don't force it, and there's no need to pop it back in once he's asleep and it falls out. Never tie it on a cord or string around his neck or to the crib, and skip dipping it in anything sweet. If he spits it out or flatly refuses, that's fine too — it's an option, not an assignment."
    sources:

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever is still an emergency at this age"
    detail: "Any fever in a baby under 3 months — a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher — means immediate medical care, exactly as in week one; this rule doesn't relax for a while yet. A rectal reading is the accurate method at this age, so it's worth having a digital thermometer on hand and knowing the technique before you need it at 2 a.m. Don't give fever medicine and wait to see how it goes — a young baby with a fever needs to be seen."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Not feeding or growing normally, vomiting, diarrhea, or acting unusual"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful vomiting (different from easy, dribbly spit-up, which is still normal), diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual or hard to wake. By four weeks you know his baseline better than anyone — 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate clinical observation, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Bloody stools mean seek care right away."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Crying you can't cope with"
    detail: "If the crying is pushing you to the edge, put the baby down on his back somewhere safe like the crib, close the door, and step away for a few minutes to calm yourself — a crying baby alone in a crib is safe; an exhausted adult past the breaking point is the real risk. Never shake, hit, or throw a baby, ever: shaking can cause lifelong brain injury or death in seconds. Then call someone — your partner, a friend, or Health Link 811. Needing a break is normal at exactly the age crying peaks; this is the week to have the tag-out plan ready before the rough evening, not during it."
    sources:
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }

  - title: "Baby blues that haven't lifted, or feeling hopeless — in either parent"
    detail: "The blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, or trouble bonding that lasts longer is postpartum depression territory — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811, and expect mood questions at the postpartum checkup (often the 10-question Edinburgh scale) — being asked isn't an accusation, it's the system working. Thoughts of harming yourself or the baby mean call 911, or the 988 suicide crisis line, now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Crying is heading for its peak — it's a curve, not a verdict"
    detail: "Intense crying typically falls between 3 and 12 weeks of age, peaking for many babies around the 2-month mark and settling by 3–4 months — this is the 'Period of PURPLE Crying,' where PURPLE stands for Peak pattern, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, and Evening clustering. A baby who cries a lot right now is usually a completely normal baby at the loud end of a normal curve — it isn't caused by anything you're doing, and some of it can't be soothed away by anyone. Knowing the shape of the curve is protective: you're closer to the peak than to the start, and the far side is real."
    sources:
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
  - title: "Alert windows are lengthening — baby massage is a lovely way to fill one"
    detail: "Gentle infant massage — slow, light strokes over legs, arms, chest, and tummy — is a calm way to spend one of those growing alert windows, and a bonding channel that works equally well for either parent. Pick a moment between feeds when he's neither hungry nor just topped up, keep the room warm, use far less pressure than feels necessary, and stop while it's still going well — a few minutes is plenty at this age. If he stiffens or fusses, that's 'not now,' not 'never' — try again another day."
    sources:

  - title: "Parent tip: formally trade one protected sleep block"
    detail: "If there are two of you, assign each parent one guarded 3–4 hour sleep block per day — earplugs in, door closed, completely off duty, no listening with one ear. Around the crying peak, sleep is the cheapest and most effective mental-health intervention available, and relentless broken sleep is one of the things that feeds postpartum depression and anxiety in both parents. Treat the block like a shift at a job: it isn't selfish, it's maintenance on the only two people running this operation."

  - title: "The germ bubble is temporary"
    detail: "The hand-washing and no-sick-visitors rules matter most in these early weeks, while his immune system is immature and he hasn't had his first vaccines. Most families loosen up gradually after the 2-month shots — one more reason to book them now — and outings outdoors were never the problem in the first place. If a relative grumbles about the rules, feel free to blame this website; that's partly what it's for."
