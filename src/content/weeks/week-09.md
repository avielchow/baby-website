weekNumber: 9
title: "Just past two months — the fog lifts and the fun starts"
description: "The post-vaccine grumpiness fades within a day or two, the smiles turn reliable and chatty, and hands become the most fascinating toy in the room — all while crying keeps sliding down the far side of its peak."
milestones:
  - title: "Smiles get reliable — and the cooing turns into conversation"
    detail: "The social smile that arrived around two months is settling into something you can count on: aim your face and your voice at him in a calm, alert moment and he'll usually beam back, using his whole face, not just his mouth. The coos are getting richer too — breathy vowels and throaty gurgles he'll now aim at you, at a toy, or at his own hands, treating his voice as a game. The single most useful thing you can do is take turns: make a sound back at every coo he offers, then pause and leave a gap for his reply. A first chuckle or squeal can surface any time now, though a first chuckle more typically lands around four months, with full belly laughs often a bit later — a wide, normal range either way."

  - title: "Hands are discovered — batting, mouthing, and the first real grasp"
    detail: "Around now hands stop being random background objects and become his favourite toy and his first tools. His fists are unclenching, so he'll open and shut them to entertain himself, bring them to his mouth (after a lot of near-misses), swipe at a toy dangling just out of reach, and — where a newborn only reflex-gripped whatever touched his palm — he may now deliberately hold and shake a light rattle you place in his hand. The batting is clumsy and rarely lands, which is exactly the normal stage before coordinated reaching. Feed the new skill: dangle something interesting over his chest, wrap his fingers around a lightweight rattle and gently shake his hand so it makes a sound, and keep the pieces big and one-piece."

  - title: "Stronger head control and higher push-ups on the tummy"
    detail: "On his belly he can lift his head up, and many babies this age are starting to push up on the forearms to get a better look around — the muscle work that eventually leads to rolling, sitting, and crawling. Off the tummy, the flaily newborn jerks are smoothing into more controlled arm and leg movements. Head control is real progress but still a work in progress, so keep supporting his head and neck whenever you lift or carry him. Short, frequent, supervised tummy sessions are what build this, and getting down face-to-face turns the work into a game."

  - title: "Watching and following you across the room"
    detail: "Vision has stretched well past feeding distance: he'll watch you move around the room, hold his gaze on a toy for several seconds, and follow a slowly moving face or rattle with his eyes as it travels from one side to the other. Your face is still the single most interesting pattern in his world. Use it — move slowly side to side to invite his eyes to track you, and give him time to study you up close. Eyes that occasionally cross or drift are still normal at this age; it's the muscles fine-tuning, not a problem."

todos:
  - title: "Keep tummy time on the daily menu"
    detail: "This is the week to make short tummy sessions a habit if they aren't already — a few times a day, a couple of minutes each, working up as his neck gets stronger. If he protests, start on your own chest while you lie back, lie down face-to-face with him, prop a rolled towel under his chest, or add a floor mirror; and if he's genuinely miserable, give him a break and try again later rather than forcing it. This is the muscle work behind head control, rolling, and everything that follows — and because babies who spend all their awake time on their backs can develop a flat spot, varied floor play matters for the shape of his head too. Always supervised, and always back-to-sleep once he's done."

  - title: "Comfort the last of the post-vaccine grumpiness away"
    detail: "By now the two-month shots are a week or so behind you, and any reaction should be gone or nearly gone: the low-grade fever, extra fussiness, sleepiness, poorer appetite, and the red or sore spot where the needle went in almost always settle within a day or two. If a tender injection site lingers, a cool wet cloth over it and gently moving that arm or leg helps, and for discomfort or fever there's weight-based infant acetaminophen — check the right dose with your pharmacist or provider rather than guessing, and never give aspirin. One lingering housekeeping note: the rotavirus vaccine (the oral one) can shed in his stool for up to about 10 days, so keep washing hands well at diaper changes."

  - title: "Start thinking about weaning the swaddle"
    detail: "Rolling typically shows up somewhere around 3–4 months, and the firm safety rule is to stop swaddling at the first signs he's trying to roll — a swaddled baby who ends up face-down can't push up or roll back. You don't have to wait for the roll to plan ahead: many families move to an arms-out sleep sack over these weeks, either cold-turkey or by freeing one arm at a time for a few nights. It's worth having the next-stage sack on hand now so the switch isn't a scramble the night he first rolls. See this week's gear note for how to make the transition smooth and safe."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeds keep spreading out a little"
    detail: "Intake is still generous but the clock keeps stretching. Breastfed babies are nursing roughly every two hours, or about 10–12 times in 24 hours; formula-fed babies around this age typically take about 4–5 oz (120–150 ml) every 3–4 hours, with feeds noticeably more spaced out than the round-the-clock newborn weeks. Bigger babies tend to drink more per feed, smaller babies often feed a bit more often — both are fine. These are averages, not targets: a baby who wants a little more or less at a given feed is self-regulating, which is exactly what you want."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Watch the baby, not the clock — and let diapers be the report card"
    detail: "Rooting and sucking on his hands are the early hunger cues; crying is the late one, and a frantic baby is harder to latch or feed than a merely-hungry one, so it pays to catch the earlier signals. When he slows, stops, or turns away, he's telling you he's done — don't push a set number of ounces. You don't need to count minutes or millilitres to know it's working: roughly six or more wet diapers a day, regular soiling, steady weight gain, and a baby who settles after feeds all tell you intake is fine. If feeding still hurts or feels off, a lactation consultant or Health Link 811 can help."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
sleep:
  - title: "A longer night stretch may be consolidating"
    detail: "Babies this age still sleep roughly 14–17 hours across a full day, usually as 2–3 daytime naps plus a longer run at night after a late feeding. Many start offering that longer night stretch around now — but at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means only about a 5–6 hour block, and plenty of babies aren't there yet, which is completely normal. Keep days bright, social, and full of daylight and nights dark, quiet, and boring; the day-night rhythm is built over the coming months, not forced this week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep still applies — and the swaddle is on its way out"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, naps included, on a firm, flat surface with a tightly fitted sheet, in his own crib or bassinet in your room, with nothing loose in the space — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, positioners, or toys — and dress him for the room rather than adding covers. Room-sharing without bed-sharing for at least the first 6 months lowers SIDS risk, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. The one thing changing soon is the swaddle: if you still swaddle, stop at the first signs he's trying to roll and move to a properly-sized arms-out sleep sack. A longer night stretch doesn't change any of this — if he drifts off in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever: the post-shot window has passed — treat a new one seriously"
    detail: "A mild fever in the first day or two after vaccines is expected, but that window is behind you now. At this age — still under 3 months — a fever is not something to medicate and wait on: babies younger than 3 months with a fever should be seen urgently, and the standard rule is that a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher means being checked right away. Any fever now that isn't a fresh vaccine reaction gets treated like that newborn rule. Call Health Link 811 or your provider rather than reaching first for fever medicine, and don't give ibuprofen to a baby this young without medical advice."

  - title: "Belly warning signs after the rotavirus vaccine"
    detail: "The rotavirus vaccine carries a very low risk of intussusception — a blockage where one part of the bowel slides into another — usually within about a week of a dose, so it can still be on the radar this week. Seek care right away for severe stomach swelling or pain, vomiting that won't stop or keeps returning, blood in his stool, or a high fever. Serious reactions to any vaccine are rare, but seek emergency care for any signs of a severe allergic reaction such as trouble breathing or swelling of the face."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://www.albertahealthservices.ca/assets/info/hp/cdc/if-hp-cdc-ipsm-infant-child-aftercare-104495.pdf" }
  - title: "Not feeding or growing, forceful vomiting, or just not himself"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful (projectile) vomiting — different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal — diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual and hard to wake. By now you know his baseline better than anyone, so 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate reason to call, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Bloody stools or any trouble breathing mean seek care immediately."

  - title: "Parent mental health — still worth checking in on"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, feeling unable to care for the baby, constant anxiety or panic, or trouble bonding that lingers past that is postpartum depression or anxiety territory — it affects about 1 in 8 new mothers, as many as 1 in 10 fathers can develop it too, and with treatment most people recover. If low mood or anxiety is hanging on a couple of months in, that's the signal to talk to your doctor or public health office, or call Health Link 811 — don't wait for a scheduled visit. Treat thoughts of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency: call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Crying should be well down the far side of its peak"
    detail: "Intense crying peaks for many babies somewhere in the 6-week-to-2-month range and then eases over the following weeks, usually settling by 3–4 months — the 'Period of PURPLE Crying,' where the crying is at its Peak, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like in the face, Long-lasting, and clusters in the Evening. If the last few weeks were loud, the odds are strongly that you're past the worst and heading down. A baby who cries a lot is usually a normal baby at the loud end of a normal range — it isn't caused by anything you're doing. And if the crying ever pushes you to your limit, it is always safe to put him down on his back in the crib and step away to collect yourself for a few minutes. Never, ever shake a baby."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }
  - title: "His hands are the best toy in the house"
    detail: "You don't need much to entertain a baby this age — his own hands are riveting, and a lightweight rattle, a high-contrast toy held about a foot away, or a baby-safe soft mirror will fill his lengthening alert windows nicely. Wrap his fingers around a rattle and shake his hand so it sounds; that cause-and-effect ('I moved, it made noise') is genuinely fascinating to him. All that hand-to-mouth gnawing and drool is exploration, not teething — real teething is usually still a couple of months off — and one or two good toys beat a binful, since he can only attend to one thing at a time."

  - title: "You're allowed to enjoy this part"
    detail: "The first two months are mostly survival; right about now the ledger tips, as reliable smiles and coos start paying you back for the sleepless nights. Let yourself enjoy the show — the nurturing that builds his brain is the ordinary stuff you already do: talking, singing, holding, reading, answering his sounds. If the enjoyment isn't coming, though, that's worth noticing rather than pushing past; a persistent flatness or dread is exactly the kind of thing the mental-health check above is for, and reaching out early makes it easier to fix."
