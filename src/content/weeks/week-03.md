weekNumber: 3
title: "Hungrier and louder"
description: "Week three's story is appetite and crying: a growth spurt makes him ravenous, evening fussing ramps up, and both are completely on schedule. Meanwhile the alert, watching-you moments are quietly getting longer."
milestones:
  - title: "Alert windows are stretching"
    detail: "Most babies are calmly awake a little more each week now, and by the end of the first month they're noticeably more alert and responsive — the AAP's one-month picture includes brief head-lifting, watching faces, and startling at sounds. Those quiet-alert windows, often right after a feed, are the golden minutes: that's when to do tummy time, hold him at face height, and talk to him. Keep it low-key — at this age 'play' just means your face, your voice, and slow movement. When he's had enough he'll tell you by looking away, squirming, or fussing; that's a cue to wind down, not a rejection."

  - title: "Watching faces at close range"
    detail: "Babies this age prefer human faces over any other pattern, and they see best at 20–30 cm (8–12 inches) — almost exactly where your face is during a feed, which is not a coincidence. When he's quiet and alert, hold your face in that zone and drift slowly side to side; many babies this age will briefly track you. After faces, high-contrast patterns and movement are his favourites, so a simple black-and-white card works as well as any fancy toy. Eyes that occasionally cross or drift when he's focusing up close are still completely normal in these early months."

  - title: "Crying is climbing — that's the curve, not a problem"
    detail: "Healthy babies cry more each week from about 2 weeks on, typically peaking somewhere around 6–8 weeks (the CPS frames the whole peak window as 3 to 12 weeks) before easing off by 3–4 months. Researchers call this the Period of PURPLE Crying: the letters stand for Peak pattern, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, Evening — in other words, crying that comes out of nowhere, resists everything, and clusters at night can still be textbook-normal development. Knowing the shape of the curve is genuinely protective: it turns 'what are we doing wrong?' into 'this is the part of the graph we're on.' You're not causing it, and you can't always fix it — your job on the worst evenings is comfort, not cure."

todos:
  - title: "Build tummy time into the daily rhythm"
    detail: "Aim for 2–3 short sessions a day, 3–5 minutes each, always awake and always supervised — hooking it to something that already happens (after a diaper change, after a nap) is how it actually sticks. You're working up to 15–30 minutes a day by around 7 weeks, and the payoff is real: neck, shoulder, and core strength for rolling and sitting later, plus less time on the back of the skull, which prevents flat spots. If he protests on the floor, cheat: chest-to-chest with you reclined counts, so does tummy-down across your lap, and getting your own face down at his level gives him something worth lifting his head for. Short and frequent beats long and miserable — end each session before the meltdown, not after."

  - title: "Make a crying plan before the rough evening, not during it"
    detail: "Agree on a tag-out signal now, while everyone is calm: when one of you is at the end of the rope, hand the baby over, no questions, no scorekeeping. If you're alone and at your limit, putting him down on his back in the crib and stepping into another room for a few minutes is safe and smart — a crying baby in a safe crib is fine; a parent past their breaking point is the actual risk. Check on him every 10–15 minutes or so, breathe, and call someone if you need backup. This is exactly the plan the CPS and the PURPLE program tell parents to make in advance, because 7 p.m. with a screaming baby is the worst possible time to improvise one."

  - title: "Switch to sleeping in shifts"
    detail: "One protected 3–4 hour block each beats a whole night of broken dozing for both of you, because it's the uninterrupted stretch that actually restores you. One parent takes the early shift, the other the late one, and the off-duty parent sleeps somewhere they can't hear every squeak — earplugs and a closed door are tools, not betrayals. Taking turns is also standard advice for surviving the crying weeks specifically: a rested parent has far more patience at the evening peak. Protecting each other's sleep is one of the best things you can do for postpartum mental health, which makes it baby care, not self-indulgence."

feeding:
  - title: "Expect a growth-spurt feeding frenzy around now"
    detail: "Many babies hit a growth spurt in these early weeks and suddenly want to eat almost hourly for a day or two — La Leche League's plain framing is that near-constant nursing during a spurt is normal, not a supply problem. Follow the hunger cues (rooting, hand-to-mouth, stirring and smacking sounds; crying is the late cue) and let him set the pace. The mechanism is worth knowing because it's reassuring: the extra time at the breast is exactly the signal that tells your supply to step up, so the frenzy is the fix, not evidence of a shortage. It typically settles back down within a couple of days, once supply catches up to the new order volume."

  - title: "Evening cluster feeding is normal, and possibly strategic"
    detail: "Feeds bunched close together in the evening — sometimes with barely a gap between them — are common at this age and don't mean your milk is running short; it may be his way of tanking up before a longer sleep stretch. The overall picture is still 8–12 feeds per 24 hours, so a clustered evening usually just means fewer feeds somewhere else in the day. The practical move is to plan for it rather than fight it: park yourself somewhere comfortable around the usual witching hour with water, snacks, and something to watch, and let the cluster happen. Judge how feeding is going by the whole day and the diapers, never by any single stretch."

  - title: "Diapers are still the report card"
    detail: "Six or more heavy wet diapers a day is the number that says he's getting enough, along with soft, seedy yellow stools. Frequent stools are typical right now — often several a day, sometimes one with nearly every feed. After the first month it's normal for breastfed babies' stools to spread way out, even to one every 2 to 7 days, as long as they stay soft when they come; that's a schedule change, not constipation. Counting diapers beats every other form of worry because it's objective — if the count is there day after day, the milk is going in, whatever the clock says about feed lengths."

sleep:
  - title: "Still no schedule — and that's on schedule"
    detail: "Sleep still comes in short 2–4 hour stretches wrapped around feeds, day and night alike, adding up to roughly 14–17 hours across 24 — newborns simply haven't developed a circadian rhythm yet. You can start gently teaching the difference now: days are bright, social, and normally noisy; nights are dark, boring, and businesslike, with feeds and changes done in dim light at low volume. Don't expect visible results for weeks — the point is to lay track, not to see the train arrive. Any 'schedule' this week is his, and following it is the strategy."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleepnewborn.html"
  - title: "Safe sleep rules apply to every sleep, including naps"
    detail: "On his back, on a firm flat mattress, with nothing loose in the crib — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, stuffed toys, or positioners. That setup applies to naps and to whoever is supervising: grandparents and other helpers need the same briefing, because 'just this once on the tummy' is where risk hides. Instead of covers, dress him for the room temperature — a sleep sack or one extra layer than you'd wear is the usual rule of thumb, since overheating is itself a SIDS risk factor. Back-sleeping for every sleep is also exactly why daytime tummy time matters: he gets his tummy-down practice while awake and watched."

      - label: "CPS"

  - title: "Room-share, don't bed-share"
    detail: "His crib or bassinet in your room for the first 6 months, when SIDS risk is highest — room-sharing on its own is protective, and it makes night feeds shorter trips anyway. Bed-sharing is the line not to cross: an adult bed's soft mattress, pillows, and duvet raise the risk of SIDS and suffocation, however tempting the 3 a.m. math looks. The realistic danger is accidental: if you're feeding in bed and might drift off, clear the pillows and covers away first, and move him back to his own sleep surface the moment you're done. Feeding in an armchair or on a couch when exhausted is actually riskier than the bed, because of the crevices a baby can slip into."

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever (rectal temp ≥ 38.0°C / 100.4°F)"
    detail: "Unchanged from week one: any fever in a baby under 3 months means medical care right away, not a wait-and-see. Rectal is the accurate method at this age — ear thermometers aren't reliable for young infants, and forehead strips aren't either. Don't give fever medicine and reassess; a true fever this young needs to be looked at even if he seems okay in himself. Same immediate-call rule for bloody stools."

      - label: "CPS"

  - title: "Crying plus anything else that seems off"
    detail: "Long crying spells alone are normal now — that's the curve. What changes the picture is crying plus something else: vomiting, diarrhea, feeding or growing poorly, fewer wet diapers, unusual sleepiness or floppiness, or a baby who just isn't behaving like himself. A cry that sounds genuinely different — high-pitched, weak, or pained rather than the usual complaint — is also worth a call. HealthLink 811 is there around the clock in Alberta, staffed by nurses, and 'is this normal?' is exactly what they're for."

  - title: "Umbilical area: redness, discharge, or pain"
    detail: "The stump usually dries and falls off by about 3 weeks — right about now — so this is its last appearance on the checklist (mention it to the doctor if it's still attached well past that). Keep the area clean and dry and fold the diaper below it; there's no need for alcohol swabs. The call-the-doctor signs are red skin spreading around the base, foul-smelling yellowish discharge, or crying when you touch the area. A few drops of blood as it detaches are normal, like a scab coming off."

  - title: "Forceful vomiting is not spit-up"
    detail: "Spit-up dribbles out easily, often with a burp, and is still completely normal — volume-wise it always looks like more than it is. Vomiting is different: it shoots out with force, and if that's happening repeatedly or after every feed, call the pediatrician — around this age doctors want to rule out pyloric stenosis, a treatable stomach-outlet problem that classically shows up at 2 weeks to 2 months with escalating projectile vomiting. Also call if vomit is green or bloody, or if he's vomiting and producing fewer wet diapers. One dramatic spit-up in an otherwise happy, feeding, peeing baby is not an emergency."

  - title: "If you ever feel close to shaking him — put him down and step out"
    detail: "Never shake, hit, or throw a baby; shaking can cause lifelong brain injury or death, and the peak-crying weeks are exactly when the risk to babies is highest — which is why the PURPLE program exists. The escape hatch is always available: baby on his back in the crib, you in another room for a few minutes with the door closed, then call your partner, a friend, or 811. Feeling pushed to the edge by relentless crying doesn't make you a bad parent — it makes you a human one who used the plan. Asking for help is the strong move, not the weak one."

  - title: "Parent check: sadness or anxiety that isn't lifting"
    detail: "By three weeks the baby blues should be fading; if the fog is thickening instead, that's information, not failure. Postpartum depression affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers (it hits fathers too), and the signs to act on are hopelessness, panic, constant worry, trouble caring for the baby or yourself, or persistent deep sadness. It is very treatable, and earlier help means a shorter road — call your doctor or 811 now rather than waiting it out. If there are any thoughts of harming yourself or the baby, that's an emergency: seek help immediately."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "'Colic' has an actual definition — the rule of threes"
    detail: "Three or more hours of crying a day, three or more days a week, in an otherwise healthy, well-fed, growing baby — that's the classic definition, and it describes as many as 1 in 5 babies. It usually starts between 2 and 5 weeks, is worst in the late afternoon and early evening, and resolves on its own by 3 to 4 months. Nothing you did caused it, and colicky babies grow and develop exactly like everyone else. The label's real value is permission: once the doctor confirms he's healthy, you can stop hunting for the fixable cause and focus on getting the household through the peak weeks."

  - title: "The soothing toolkit is worth drilling"
    detail: "Motion (rocking, walks, car rides), sucking (breast or pacifier), swaddling, skin-to-skin, a warm bath, white noise, and turning the lights and stimulation down all have a real track record — the AAP's fussy-baby list is essentially this, tried one at a time. Give each technique a few minutes before switching; rapid-fire cycling reads as more stimulation to an overloaded baby. Video genuinely beats text here — pediatrician Robert Hamilton's famous demonstration of 'The Hold' (fold the arms across the chest, support the chin, grip the bottom, rock at a 45° angle) is worth two minutes of your time. And the honest fine print: nothing works every time, on any baby, so a failed technique is not a failed parent."

  - title: "Accept every specific offer of help"
    detail: "The best mental-health move this week is delegating everything that isn't feeding the baby — meals, laundry, groceries, holding him while you shower or nap. When someone says 'let me know if you need anything,' convert it on the spot into something concrete with a time attached: 'Tuesday, could you bring dinner?' People mean it, and specific asks get done. Support and rest aren't just comfort — they're among the strongest protective factors against postpartum depression, which makes a protected block of sleep per parent per day health care, not a luxury."
