title: "Two months — the first smile, the first shots"
description: "The two-month mark is the first real milestone: genuine social smiles, the first well-child visit and round of vaccines, and — right on cue — crying starting down the far side of its peak."
milestones:
  - title: "The real social smile arrives"
    detail: "Somewhere around now most babies produce the genuine social smile — aimed at you, using the whole face and not just the mouth, in response to your voice and grin rather than a passing bit of gas. The CDC's two-month checklist lists smiling at people, looking at your face, and calming down when spoken to or picked up as the social milestones by this age. You can invite it: catch an alert, unhurried moment, bring your face to about 20–30 cm, smile big, then pause and give him a turn to answer. Remember the checklist describes what most babies (about 75%) do by two months, not a deadline — a few genuinely happy babies don't switch on the smiles until 7 or 8 weeks."

  - title: "Cooing, gurgling, and turning toward sounds"
    detail: "The CDC lists making sounds other than crying — the breathy vowel coos and throaty gurgles — and reacting to loud sounds as the two-month language milestones. Many babies also turn the head toward a voice, yours most of all, and treat their own coos as a game to play with themselves and with you. Answer every sound he makes with a sound of your own and leave a pause for his reply; that turn-taking feels silly but is exactly how early language input works. There's a wide normal range here — some babies coo before a month, others closer to three."

  - title: "Head up and pushing up in tummy time"
    detail: "On his tummy, most two-month-olds can lift the head up, and many are starting to push up on the forearms; off the tummy, the flaily newborn jerks are smoothing into more controlled arm and leg movements. The CDC lists holding the head up on the tummy, moving both arms and both legs, and opening the hands briefly as the movement milestones by now. Keep offering short supervised tummy sessions a few times a day — getting down face-to-face turns it into a game, and this is the muscle work that leads to rolling and sitting. Head control is still a work in progress, so support it when you lift him."
    sources:
      - { label: "CDC", url: "https://www.cdc.gov/act-early/milestones/2-months.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-Birth-to-Three-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "Watching, following, and knowing you at a distance"
    detail: "Vision has stretched well past feeding distance: babies this age watch you as you move across the room, follow a slowly moving face or rattle with their eyes, and start to recognize familiar people from a few feet away. The CDC groups watching you move and looking at a toy under cognitive milestones, alongside the social one of calming when you pick him up. Use it — move slowly side to side to invite his eyes to track you, and let him study your face, still his favourite pattern in the world. Eyes that occasionally cross or drift are still normal at two months."

todos:
  - title: "The two-month well-child visit"
    detail: "This is the first big checkup: weight, length, and head circumference plotted on the growth curve, a head-to-toe physical exam, a review of feeding, sleep, and development, and the first round of vaccines. It's also a screening point for the parents — the visit routinely includes questions about how the birthing parent is doing, including mood, anxiety, and how feeding is going, so an honest answer here is the system working, not an imposition. Bring the baby's Alberta health card, and treat the appointment as a two-way conversation, not an inspection."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/Your-Childs-Checkups/Pages/Your-Checkup-Checklist-2-Months-Old.aspx" }

  - title: "The first immunizations — and how to make the shots easier"
    detail: "Alberta's two-month routine is three vaccines: DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (one combined needle covering diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Hib, and hepatitis B), the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PNEU-C15), and rotavirus — which is a liquid given by mouth, not a needle. That's protection against eight diseases in one visit, and the immune system handles the few added antigens easily. To ease it: you can breastfeed or hold him skin-to-close during and right after the injections, and gentle holding plus a calm voice settles most babies within a minute or two. These are given at your public health / community health centre, not usually the family doctor's office."

  - title: "Know what's normal after the shots — and how to help him feel better"
    detail: "Most reactions are mild and fade within a couple of days: a low-grade fever, extra fussiness or tiredness, poorer appetite or sleep, and redness, swelling, or a sore spot where the needle went in. To help, cuddle him, lay a cool wet cloth over a sore injection site and gently move that arm or leg, and for a fever dress him in fewer layers and offer extra feeds. There is medicine for fever or pain — infant acetaminophen — but the dose is by weight, so check the right amount with your provider or pharmacist (or the package) rather than guessing, and never give aspirin. The rotavirus vaccine virus can appear in his stool for up to 10 days, so wash hands well at diaper changes."
    sources:

  - title: "Bring your list of questions"
    detail: "The two-month visit is the natural place to raise anything that's been nagging you — a feeding or spit-up worry, a rash, whether the crying seems like more than normal, a movement or a sound he isn't making yet, or your own mood. Write the questions down beforehand, because they reliably evaporate the moment you're in the exam room, and don't rank anything as too small to ask. The milestone checklist is meant to be a conversation-starter at exactly this kind of visit, not a scorecard you pass or fail."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeds are spreading out a little"
    detail: "Intake is still generous but the clock is stretching. Breastfed babies typically nurse about 10–12 times in 24 hours, and formula-fed babies usually take around 4–5 oz (120–150 ml) every 3–4 hours by now, with feeds starting to space out compared to the newborn weeks. A rough ceiling for formula: about 2½ oz (75 ml) per pound of body weight per day, and no more than roughly 32 oz (960 ml) in 24 hours. These are averages, not targets — a baby who wants a bit more or less at a given feed is self-regulating, which is exactly what you want."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/formula-feeding/Pages/Amount-and-Schedule-of-Formula-Feedings.aspx" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Watch the baby, not the clock — and let diapers be the report card"
    detail: "Rooting, hand-sucking, and stirring are the early hunger cues; crying is the late one, and a frantic, crying baby is harder to latch or feed than a merely-hungry one, so it's worth catching the earlier signals. You don't need to count minutes or ounces to know it's working: roughly six or more wet diapers a day, steady weight gain on the growth curve, and a baby who settles after feeds tells you intake is fine. If feeding still hurts, feels shaky, or you dread it, a lactation consultant or Health Link 811 can help — this is fixable, and easier to fix sooner."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
sleep:
  - title: "A longer night stretch may be showing up"
    detail: "Babies this age still sleep roughly 14–17 hours across a full day, but many start offering one longer run at night around now — though at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means only a 5–6 hour stretch, and plenty of two-month-olds aren't there yet. Some still have day and night partly flipped, which is normal. Keep days bright, social, and full of daylight and nights dark, quiet, and boring; the day-night rhythm is built over the coming months, not forced this week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep rules haven't changed"
    detail: "Back for every sleep — naps included — on a firm, flat surface, in his own crib or bassinet in your room, with nothing loose in the space: no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners, and dress him for the room instead of adding covers. Room-sharing without bed-sharing for at least the first 6 months is associated with cutting SIDS risk substantially, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. A longer night stretch doesn't change any of this — if he falls asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies" }
redFlags:
  - title: "Fever: expected briefly after shots, still urgent otherwise"
    detail: "A mild fever in the first day or two after the vaccines is a common, expected reaction, and you can treat his discomfort with fewer layers, extra fluids, and the correct weight-based dose of infant acetaminophen. But a fever that climbs high, lasts beyond about 48 hours, or comes with a baby who seems genuinely unwell still warrants a call to Health Link 811 — and any fever that isn't clearly tied to the recent shots gets treated like the newborn rule: a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher in a baby under 3 months means being seen right away, no medicate-and-wait."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://www.albertahealthservices.ca/assets/info/hp/cdc/if-hp-cdc-ipsm-infant-child-aftercare-104495.pdf" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Signs of a serious reaction after the rotavirus vaccine"
    detail: "The rotavirus vaccine carries a very low risk of intussusception — a blockage where one part of the bowel slides into another — usually within about 7 days of the first dose. Seek medical care right away if he has severe stomach swelling or pain, vomiting that won't stop or keeps coming back, blood in his stool, or a high fever. More generally, serious reactions to any vaccine are rare, but call Health Link 811 to report anything serious or unusual, and seek emergency care for any signs of a severe allergic reaction such as trouble breathing or swelling of the face."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://www.albertahealthservices.ca/assets/info/hp/cdc/if-hp-cdc-ipsm-infant-child-aftercare-104495.pdf" }
  - title: "Not feeding or growing, forceful vomiting, or acting unusual"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful (projectile) vomiting — different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal — diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual and hard to wake. By two months you know his baseline better than anyone, so 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate reason to call, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Bloody stools or any trouble breathing mean seek care immediately."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Parent mental health — the checkup screens for it too"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, or trouble bonding that lasts longer is postpartum depression or anxiety territory — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. The two-month visit is deliberately a screening point: expect to be asked how you're doing, and answer honestly. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811 if the low mood is lingering, and treat thoughts of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency — call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."
    sources:

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Crying should be starting down the far side of its peak"
    detail: "Intense crying typically peaks for many babies right around the two-month mark and then eases over the following weeks, settling by 3–4 months — this is the 'Period of PURPLE Crying,' where PURPLE stands for Peak pattern, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, and Evening clustering. If these last few weeks have been loud, you are more likely past the worst than heading into it, and the far side of the curve is real. A baby who cries a lot is usually a normal baby at the loud end of a normal range — it isn't caused by anything you're doing, and if the crying ever pushes you to the edge, it is always safe to put him down on his back in the crib and step away to collect yourself. Never, ever shake a baby."
    sources:
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
  - title: "Hands are becoming interesting — bring on the rattles"
    detail: "Around now hands stop being random background objects and start becoming tools: he'll open and close them, bring them to his mouth, bat at things dangling nearby, and briefly grip a rattle if you tuck it into his palm. Wrap his fingers around a lightweight rattle and gently shake his hand so it makes a sound — that cause-and-effect ('I moved, it rattled') is genuinely fascinating to him. A baby-safe soft mirror and a high-contrast toy held about a foot away are easy ways to fill his lengthening alert windows."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/development-2mos.html" }
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-Birth-to-Three-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "The germ bubble can start to loosen after this round"
    detail: "The strict hand-washing and no-sick-visitors rules matter most in the first weeks, while the immune system is immature and the first vaccines aren't yet on board. Protection from the shots builds over roughly the next couple of weeks, and most families relax the tightest rules gradually after the two-month round — outings outdoors were never the risky part anyway. The sensible habits worth keeping: hands washed before holding him, anyone with sniffles stays home, and admirers can look but not touch."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/newborn-out.html" }
