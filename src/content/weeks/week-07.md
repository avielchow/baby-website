weekNumber: 7
title: "Smiles you can count on"
description: "Week seven is the payoff week — social smiles turn reliable, cooing becomes little back-and-forth conversations, the worst of the crying starts easing off its peak, and the two-month shots are now just days away."
milestones:
  - title: "The social smile is getting reliable — and it's aimed at you"
    detail: "By the end of the second month the real social smile is usually here: not the fleeting sleep-and-gas grins of the newborn weeks, but a whole-face smile he gives on purpose when you talk to him or smile first. Catch an alert, calm moment, bring your face to about 8–12 inches, smile big, and wait — babies learn fast that their smile reliably gets a smile back, and that discovery is half of why they keep doing it. If he looks past you rather than dead-on at first, that's normal; direct eye contact can feel like a lot, and he'll come back to your eyes. This is the milestone that quietly repays a month of 3 a.m. feeds."

  - title: "Cooing turns into a two-way conversation"
    detail: "Around now he's making sounds other than crying — breathy vowels, 'ooo' and 'aah' coos, throaty gurgles — often practised as much for his own pleasure as for yours. The single best thing you can do is treat it like real conversation: when he coos, coo back, then pause and leave a gap for him to 'reply.' That deliberate wait is the whole game; babies whose parents talk *with* them rather than *at* them build language sooner, and the turn-taking rhythm is what you're teaching. Narrate your day, mimic his sounds, and answer every little 'ah' — there's no wrong material, and the grocery list counts."

  - title: "Eyes follow smoothly past the middle — and hands start to swing"
    detail: "His eyes now track a moving object or your face smoothly from one side, across the midline, to the other — hold a toy or your face about a foot away and move it slowly side to side to invite the follow. He'll also settle a gaze on something interesting for a few seconds at a time. Batting and swiping at dangling things is just beginning and will sharpen over the next couple of months, so hang a toy or your hand just out of reach and let him take a swat at it — the misses are the practice. Occasional eye-crossing is still normal at this age."

  - title: "Stronger neck: he holds his head up in tummy time now"
    detail: "The reward for all those short tummy-time sessions is showing up — most babies this age can hold the head up while on the tummy, pushing up and looking around instead of face-planting, and are working toward holding it steady when you hold them upright over the next few weeks. It still isn't fully steady, so keep a hand behind the head when you lift or carry him. Those neck and shoulder muscles are the foundation for rolling and sitting later, and the only way to build them is time off the back while awake. Getting down on the floor face-to-face turns the work into a game."

todos:
  - title: "Keep tummy time going — aim for 15–30 minutes a day, in short bursts"
    detail: "The AAP recommends tummy-to-play a few times a day, starting from a few minutes and working up — by around two months many babies tolerate a daily total of roughly 15–30 minutes, spread across short, awake, supervised sessions rather than one long one. Vary the position so it isn't a chore: tummy-to-tummy on your reclined chest, a lap-soothe across your knees, or on the floor with a rolled towel under his chest and a mirror or your face for him to lift toward. If he protests, shorter and more often beats a single miserable stretch, and ending while it's still going well keeps him willing to try tomorrow. Remember the pairing: back to sleep, tummy to play."

  - title: "Final prep for the two-month checkup and first vaccines — they're next week"
    detail: "The two-month visit is the big one: measurements plotted on the growth curve, a physical exam, a development-and-feeding review, and the first round of routine shots. In Alberta at two months that's DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (one combined needle covering diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, polio, Hib, and hepatitis B), the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine, and rotavirus given by mouth — protection against seven or eight diseases in one visit. Write your questions down now while you think of them (feeding, sleep, that rash, tummy time, your own mood — nothing is too small), and bring his Alberta health card. It's normal for him to be fussy, warm, or extra sleepy for a day or two after; ask the nurse about infant acetaminophen dosing before you leave."

  - title: "Outings are ramping up — keep the few rules that still matter"
    detail: "As you get out more, a daily walk in the fresh air is genuinely good for both of you, and open outdoor space was never the risky part. The rules still worth keeping for one more week, until the vaccines are on board: anyone holding him washes hands first, anyone with sniffles stays home, and strangers can admire but not touch. Where you can, skip enclosed crowds — malls, planes, packed waiting rooms — a little longer. After the two-month shots, most families start letting the visitor rules relax."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeding is well established — and starting to spread out a little"
    detail: "Breastfed babies still typically feed about every two hours, roughly 10–12 times in 24 hours, while at around two months formula-fed babies take about 4–5 oz (120–150 ml) every 3–4 hours. Feeds may begin to space out a touch now, but keep watching the baby rather than the clock: rooting, hand-to-mouth, and stirring mean feed soon, and crying is the late cue, not the first. Diapers stay the report card — six to eight wet ones a day says intake is fine, and fewer than five or six is the signal to check in. If it's working, don't fix it."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Learn his hunger cues — catch them before the crying"
    detail: "Feeding goes far more smoothly when you read the early signals instead of waiting for the wail: stirring and rooting, turning the head to search, bringing hands to the mouth and sucking on fists. Crying is the last cue on the ladder, and a frantic, over-hungry baby is harder to latch and settle for both of you. You know his baseline better than anyone now — the fist-to-mouth move is often his first clear 'I'm ready to eat.'"
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "If a six-week growth spurt is still settling, that's normal"
    detail: "If he's suddenly hungrier and fussier and seems to want to feed almost hourly, a growth spurt — commonly around six weeks — is the likely culprit, and it usually passes within a day or two. The extra demand is the mechanism, not a malfunction: more frequent nursing is exactly what tells your supply to increase. Formula-fed babies may simply take a bit more per bottle for a few days. It is not a sign your supply is failing."

sleep:
  - title: "Maybe one longer stretch at night — but still no real schedule"
    detail: "Babies this age sleep about 14–17 hours across a 24-hour day, and some start offering one longer run at night around now as their sleep cycle inches closer to yours — though at this age 'sleeping through the night' officially means a 5–6 hour stretch, usually after a late feed. Plenty of seven-week-olds still wake often, and that's completely normal. Keep days bright, social, and active and nights dark, quiet, and boring; the rhythm builds over the coming months, not in one week."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe-sleep rules do not change — even as sleep improves"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, naps included, on a firm, flat surface, with nothing loose in the sleep space: no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners, and dress him for the room temperature instead of adding covers. Keep his crib or bassinet in your room but out of your bed — room-sharing without bed-sharing for at least the first six months is linked to a lower risk of SIDS, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. If he falls asleep in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."
    sources:

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever is still an emergency at this age"
    detail: "Any fever in a baby under three months — a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher — means immediate medical care, and this rule doesn't relax until after three months. A rectal reading is the accurate method at this age, so it's worth knowing the technique before you need it at 2 a.m. Don't give fever medicine and wait to see how things go; a young baby with a fever needs to be seen. (This is separate from being a little warm or fussy for a day after the two-month shots — but if you're unsure, call.)"
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Not feeding or growing, forceful vomiting, or 'just not himself'"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful vomiting (different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal), diarrhea, or a baby who is much sleepier than usual or hard to wake. By seven weeks you know his baseline better than anyone, and 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate clinical observation — Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Bloody stools mean seek care right away."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }

  - title: "Crying that's pushing you past your limit"
    detail: "Even as the crying starts easing off its peak, some evenings will still be brutal. If it's pushing you to the edge, put the baby down on his back somewhere safe like the crib, close the door, and step away for a few minutes to collect yourself — a crying baby alone in a safe crib is fine; an exhausted adult past the breaking point is the real danger. Never shake, hit, or throw a baby, ever: shaking can cause lifelong brain injury or death in seconds. Then call someone — your partner, a friend, or Health Link 811. Needing a break is normal; have the tag-out plan ready before the hard evening, not during it."

  - title: "Parent check-in: mood that hasn't lifted, in either of you"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, or trouble bonding that lasts longer is postpartum depression territory — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811, and expect mood questions at appointments (often the 10-question Edinburgh scale) — being asked isn't an accusation, it's the system working. Your two-month checkup is a natural place to raise it out loud. Thoughts of harming yourself or the baby mean call 911, or the 988 suicide crisis line, now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The crying is starting to come down off its peak"
    detail: "Intense crying typically runs between about 3 and 12 weeks, peaks for many babies around the two-month mark, and is usually over by three to four months — the 'Period of PURPLE Crying' (Peak, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, Evening clustering). If you're around week seven, you're at or just past the summit and the far side is real. A baby who still cries a lot right now is usually a normal baby at the loud end of a normal curve — it isn't caused by anything you're doing, and some of it can't be soothed away by anyone. Knowing the shape of the curve is itself protective."
    sources:
      - { label: "PURPLE", url: "https://dontshake.org/purple-crying" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/colic_and_crying" }
  - title: "The 'pause' is the secret to those first conversations"
    detail: "The easiest way to grow his coos and smiles is almost embarrassingly simple: say something, then stop and wait. Leave a real gap — a beat or two longer than feels natural — for him to answer with a sound or an expression, then respond to whatever he offers as if it were a brilliant point. That serve-and-return rhythm, repeated a hundred small times a day, is how turn-taking and language get wired in. You can do it while changing a diaper or making dinner; the content matters far less than the back-and-forth."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/learn13m.html" }
      - { label: "CDC", url: "https://www.cdc.gov/act-early/milestones/2-months.html" }
  - title: "Parent tip: protect one guarded sleep block each"
    detail: "If there are two of you, assign each parent one protected 3–4 hour sleep block a day — earplugs in, door closed, fully off duty, not listening with one ear. Sleep is the cheapest and most effective mental-health intervention available right now, and relentless broken sleep is one of the things that feeds postpartum depression and anxiety in both parents. Treat the block like a shift at a job: it isn't selfish, it's maintenance on the only two people running this operation."
    sources:
      - { label: "AHS", url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/pages/conditions.aspx?hwid=tn9653" }

  - title: "The germ bubble is about to loosen"
    detail: "The hand-washing and no-sick-visitors rules matter most in these first weeks, while his immune system is immature and he hasn't had his first vaccines. Most families ease up gradually after the two-month shots — one more reason next week's appointment feels like a milestone — and outdoor outings were never the problem in the first place. If a relative grumbles about the rules this last week, feel free to blame this website; that's partly what it's for."
