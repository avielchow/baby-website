weekNumber: 2
title: "Two weeks in"
description: "Week two is about getting back to birth weight, saying goodbye to the cord stump, and feeding around the clock — while the crying quietly starts to ramp up."
milestones:
  - title: "Back to birth weight by ~day 10–14"
    detail: "Most babies dip 7–10% below birth weight in the first days, then climb back to (or above) it by around two weeks — after that, expect roughly 30 g (1 oz) a day of gain for the first few months. The two-week weight check confirms the curve has turned; until then, watch feeds and diapers rather than hunting for a home scale. Regaining birth weight is the single best sign that feeding is working, which is why everyone keeps asking about it."

  - title: "Cord stump falls off"
    detail: "Usually between 1 and 3 weeks, most often right around now. It dries from yellow-green to brown to black before detaching, and a few drops of blood-tinged fluid at the navel when it lets go is completely normal. The small raw spot underneath heals over within a week or so — keep it clean and dry, and resist the urge to pull on a stump that's hanging by a thread."
    sources:
      - label: "AHS"
        url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/aftercareinformation/pages/conditions.aspx?HwId=abk2216"
  - title: "Jaundice on its way out"
    detail: "Typical newborn jaundice shows up between days 1 and 4 and fades on its own over one to two weeks as the liver gets up to speed. By now the yellow should be retreating — it clears from the face downward — not spreading to the arms and legs. Frequent feeding genuinely helps, since bilirubin leaves the body in poop."

      - label: "CPS"

  - title: "First growth spurt"
    detail: "Many babies hit a rapid-growth stretch around 7–10 days (and again around 3 weeks and 6 weeks): suddenly hungrier, fussier, and glued to the breast or bottle. It typically lasts 2–3 days, then eases as milk supply catches up. Nothing to fix — feed on cue, cancel what you can, and ride it out."

todos:
  - title: "Vitamin D drops: 400 IU every day if breastfeeding"
    detail: "Canadian Paediatric Society guidance for all breastfed and partially breastfed babies, starting from birth — breast milk alone doesn't carry enough vitamin D, and at Alberta's latitude babies make next to none from sunlight. Easiest routine: one dropper dose into the cheek (or onto the nipple just before latching) at the same feed every day, so it runs on autopilot. Formula-fed full-term babies usually don't need drops because formula is already fortified. That one daily drop is the entire prevention program for rickets — cheap insurance for growing bones."

  - title: "Keep the cord stump clean and dry until it's gone"
    detail: "Fold the top of the diaper down below the stump so it stays dry and open to air, and stick to sponge baths until it falls off and the navel has healed. If pee or poop gets on it, wash with plain water and pat dry — no alcohol swabs or antiseptics needed; dry and left alone is the whole job. Let it detach on its own schedule, even when it's dangling by a thread."

Bring your actual questions — the 3 a.m. ones you swore you'd remember. HealthLink 811 covers everything in between, 24/7."

Knocking out all three in one sitting while he naps is a genuinely satisfying 20 minutes."

feeding:
  - title: "Still 8–12 feeds per 24 hours"
    detail: "Breastfed babies eat about every 2–3 hours, formula-fed about every 3–4 — counted from the start of one feed to the start of the next, so a 40-minute feed plus a 90-minute nap means it's nearly time again. The trick is catching early hunger cues: stirring, rooting, lip-smacking, hands to mouth. Crying is a late cue, and a frantic baby latches worse than a calm one, so feeding at the first signs makes everyone's night easier. Once he's back above birth weight and gaining steadily, you can generally stop waking him and let him set the night schedule himself."

  - title: "Growth-spurt days: feed on cue, not on schedule"
    detail: "During a spurt he may want to eat almost constantly for a day or two, especially through the evening — one feed blurring into the next. That cluster feeding is the system working as designed: more time at the breast tells the supply to scale up, and things usually settle within 2–3 days once production catches up. It's demand-setting, not evidence there isn't enough milk, so the fix is more feeding rather than topping up — park yourself on the couch with water, snacks, and something to watch."

  - title: "Diapers are still the dashboard"
    detail: "From about day 5 on, expect at least 6 heavy wet diapers a day plus regular stools — breastfed babies often produce 3 or more mustard-yellow, seedy poops a day at this age. If the diaper count is there and the weight is climbing, intake is fine, whatever the clock or your worry brain says. It's a far better gauge than how long feeds take or how full the breasts feel."

sleep:
  - title: "Roughly 14–18 hours a day, still in 2–4 hour pieces"
    detail: "Newborns sleep a lot but never for long: 2–4 hours at a stretch, around the clock, adding up to 14–18 hours a day — and some log even more. Their sleep cycles are short and mostly light sleep, which is why he startles awake the moment you lower him into the bassinet. Waking to feed this often is biology doing its job: a two-week-old's stomach simply can't hold a whole night's worth of milk."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleepnewborn.html"
  - title: "Day/night confusion is normal and not fixable yet"
    detail: "There's no circadian rhythm at two weeks — it develops gradually over the coming weeks, and no schedule can rush it. You can still lay the groundwork: daylight, chatter, and normal household noise around daytime feeds; lights low, voice boring, and diaper changes only if needed at night. He won't learn anything yet, but the contrast gives his body clock the raw material to set itself."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleepnewborn.html"
  - title: "Safe sleep, every sleep — including the 4 a.m. desperation nap"
    detail: "On his back for every sleep, on a firm flat surface, in a crib, cradle, or bassinet with nothing loose — no blankets, bumpers, pillows, or toys. Room-sharing (his bassinet beside your bed) for the first 6 months lowers the risk of SIDS; bed-sharing raises it, especially with exhausted adults, soft bedding, or a couch involved. The rules don't bend at 4 a.m. — if you're about to fall asleep holding him, put him down in the bassinet and take the protest crying over the couch-doze."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/safe_sleep_for_babies"
redFlags:
  - title: "Fever (rectal temp ≥ 38.0°C / 100.4°F)"
    detail: "Still the week-one rule, worth repeating all month: any fever in a baby under 3 months means a same-day medical assessment — call the doctor, head in, or call 811 if you're unsure where to go. Rectal temperature is the accurate method at this age; forehead and ear readings can wait until he's older. Don't give fever medicine and wait it out — a newborn fever needs eyes on the baby first."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking"
  - title: "Jaundice getting worse instead of better"
    detail: "By two weeks the yellow should be fading, not advancing. Call the doctor the same day if it's spreading to the arms and legs, if he's unusually sleepy and hard to wake for feeds, if he's feeding poorly or losing weight, or if the jaundice is still obvious past two weeks. Most causes are very manageable — but the doctor will want a bilirubin check to rule out the rare ones."
    sources:
      - label: "CPS"
        url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/pregnancy-and-babies/jaundice_in_newborns"
  - title: "Angry-looking navel after the cord comes off"
    detail: "A little goo and even a few spots of blood are normal while the navel heals. What isn't: spreading redness, swelling, or warmth around the base, red streaks on the belly, pus, a foul smell, fever, or crying when the area is touched. Those point to infection, which is rare but moves fast in newborns — call the doctor or 811 the same day."
    sources:
      - label: "AHS"
        url: "https://myhealth.alberta.ca/Health/aftercareinformation/pages/conditions.aspx?HwId=abk2216"
  - title: "Still losing weight, or not trending back toward birth weight"
    detail: "By two weeks the curve should be pointing up, and weight loss beyond about 10% of birth weight at any point deserves attention. If the weigh-in shows he's flat or still dropping, that's a same-day feeding assessment with the doctor or a lactation consultant — not a wait-and-see. It's almost always a fixable feeding-mechanics issue, and the earlier it's caught, the easier the fix."
    sources:
      - label: "Nemours"
        url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/grownewborn.html"
  - title: "Baby blues that aren't lifting"
    detail: "Baby blues peak around day 4 and should be easing by the end of this week — that fade is the defining feature. Low mood, anxiety, or tearfulness that persists past two weeks, feeling hopeless or numb toward the baby, or any thought that life isn't worth living is postpartum depression territory: it affects up to 15 in 100 mothers (partners can get it too), it's very treatable, and it deserves a call to your provider or 811 today. This check is for both of you — the non-birthing parent should be watching, and asking."
    sources:

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The crying is about to increase — on purpose, sort of"
    detail: "Normal newborn crying starts climbing around two weeks and peaks somewhere between 3 and 12 weeks (most often around 6–8) before fading by 3–4 months — researchers call it the Period of PURPLE Crying, and 'resists soothing' is literally one of the letters. Build a soothing sequence: run the basics (hungry? diaper? too hot or cold?), then try skin-to-skin, gentle motion, sucking, and steady shushing. If nothing works and you're fraying, put him down safely in the crib, step out, and take ten minutes — a crying baby in a safe crib is fine, and never, ever shake a baby."
    sources:

  - title: "Skin weirdness is standard-issue"
    detail: "Peeling, flaking skin in the first week or two is the newborn default and needs nothing — not even lotion. Baby acne (small red or white-headed bumps on the face) often shows up in the first few weeks, courtesy of leftover pregnancy hormones; don't scrub, squeeze, or treat it, because it clears on its own. Plain water and patience handle nearly all newborn skin."

  - title: "Parent survival tip: protect one real sleep stretch"
    detail: "Blues or not, sleep deprivation makes everything feel worse — and one consolidated block of sleep restores more than the same hours in fragments. Hand baby off between feeds (a pumped bottle, formula, or bringing him in only to nurse) so the birthing parent gets one unbroken 3–4 hour stretch, and aim for one proper meal and one adult conversation a day. That's not indulgence — it's the maintenance schedule that keeps the whole operation running."
