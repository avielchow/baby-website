title: "Almost three months — a little person emerges"
description: "The gaps between feeds and the stretches of sleep are starting to feel less random, hands have become the best toy in the house, and the coos are turning into back-and-forth conversations — with no shots due until four months to interrupt the fun."
milestones:
  - title: "Smiles saved for you — and the first flicker of anticipation"
    detail: "The reflexive newborn smiles are long gone; by now most babies smile on their own to get your attention and light up for the familiar faces they know best. Watch for something new layered on top: the beginnings of anticipation. A baby who quiets and gets busy-legged when he sees you scoop up the bottle, or settles the moment you start the pre-feed routine, is starting to connect a cue with what comes next — early evidence that his world is becoming predictable to him. Meet it head-on: narrate what you're about to do, catch his eye before you start, and let the routine itself become part of the comfort."

  - title: "Cooing 'conversations,' squeals — and maybe a first laugh"
    detail: "The breathy 'oooo' and 'aahh' coos are becoming a two-way game: he makes a sound, and if you answer and pause, he'll often answer back, turning toward your voice to do it. Many babies this age add delighted squeals to the repertoire, and some produce a first real chuckle when you try to make them laugh — though a full belly laugh is genuinely a few weeks off for plenty of happy babies, so its absence is not a worry. Feed the loop: reply to every coo with a real reply, leave a gap for his turn, and name what he's looking at. That silly-feeling turn-taking is exactly the back-and-forth that builds language."

Mouthing is how he investigates the world right now, not necessarily a sign of teething, so offer clean, baby-safe things to grab and keep small or detachable parts well out of reach."

    detail: "Head control is arriving: held upright, many babies now hold the head fairly steady rather than bobbling, and on the tummy the push-up is progressing from a brief head-lift toward propping up on the forearms with the chest off the floor. This is the strength that eventually leads to rolling, so it's worth protecting. Keep offering short tummy sessions through the day, get down to his eye level to give him a reason to look up, and keep supporting the head whenever you lift or carry him, since 'steadier' isn't 'fully steady' yet."

  - title: "Keep tummy time in the daily rhythm"
    detail: "The forearm push-up only comes from practice, so keep tummy time a regular, several-times-a-day habit rather than a once-in-a-while event — short and frequent beats long and miserable. Aim for a few minutes at a stretch when he's awake and content (not right after a big feed), and build up as his tolerance grows. If he protests, drop down flat so you're face to face, prop a high-contrast toy or a mirror just ahead of him, or start with tummy-to-tummy on your chest and progress to the floor. Every minute of head-lifting and pushing up is the groundwork for rolling, sitting, and crawling."

  - title: "Lean into a few gentle anchors — not a rigid schedule"
    detail: "Now that a loose rhythm is emerging, a few consistent anchors help it firm up without forcing a clock onto a baby who isn't ready for one. The easiest and highest-payoff is a short, predictable bedtime sequence — say a bath, a feed, a book, then down — repeated in the same order so the steps themselves start to signal 'sleep is coming.' A simple wake-up ritual and a regular tummy-time-then-play window help too. Keep it flexible and led by his cues; the goal is gentle predictability that makes your day plannable, not a timetable you feel you're failing."

feeding:
  - title: "Feeds are settling into a looser, more predictable pattern"
    detail: "The round-the-clock newborn grazing is easing into something you can start to anticipate. Breastfed babies still nurse often — roughly every couple of hours, about 10–12 times in 24 hours — while formula-fed babies around this age typically take about 4–5 oz (120–150 ml) every 3–4 hours, heading toward 6–8 oz across 4–5 feeds as they approach the 3–6 month window. These are averages, not targets: a baby who wants a little more or less at a given feed is self-regulating, which is exactly right. Keep following his cues rather than a strict clock, even as the clock gets more predictable on its own."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
  - title: "Cues first, and let the diapers be the report card"
    detail: "Rooting, hand-sucking, and stirring are the early hunger signs; crying is the late one, and it's easier to feed a merely-hungry baby than a frantic one, so catch the earlier signals. You don't need to tally ounces or minutes to know it's working — roughly six or more wet diapers a day, steady weight gain along his own growth curve, and a baby who's alert and content between feeds all say intake is fine. Fewer than five or six wet diapers in a day, or a baby who seems persistently unsatisfied, is worth a call to your provider or Health Link 811."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
sleep:
  - title: "A more predictable pattern — and maybe a longer night stretch"
    detail: "Total sleep is still around 14–17 hours a day, but the shape of it is firming up: many babies this age fall into 2–3 daytime naps plus a longer run at night after a late feed. Remember that at this stage 'sleeping through the night' officially means only a 5–6 hour stretch, breastfed babies often wake sooner, and sleep cycles only start reorganizing toward a more adult pattern around four months — so a longer stretch is a welcome bonus, not a milestone you're behind on if it hasn't shown up. Keep days bright and social and nights dark, quiet, and boring; that contrast is what builds the day-night rhythm over the coming months."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "Safe sleep rules haven't changed"
    detail: "Back for every sleep, naps included, on a firm, flat surface, in his own crib or bassinet in your room, with nothing loose in the space — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners — and dress him for the room rather than adding covers. Room-sharing without bed-sharing for at least the first 6 months is linked to substantially lower SIDS risk, while bed-sharing raises the risk of SIDS and suffocation. A more grown-up sleep pattern doesn't change any of this: if he drifts off in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/sleep/Pages/A-Parents-Guide-to-Safe-Sleep.aspx" }

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever in a baby this young is still a same-day matter"
    detail: "With no shots due this week, there's no expected post-vaccine fever to explain one away. Any fever gets treated by the under-3-months rule: a rectal temperature of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher means being seen right away — no medicate-and-wait — because a young baby's fever can be the first and only sign of something serious. Health Link 811 will help you decide how and where to be seen at any hour, and anything that looks like real distress goes straight to emergency."
    sources:
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Not feeding or growing, forceful vomiting, or 'not himself'"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful (projectile) vomiting — different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal — diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual and hard to rouse. By almost three months you know his baseline better than anyone, so 'he's just not himself' is a legitimate reason to call, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Any trouble breathing, blue or dusky colour, or blood in the stool means seek care immediately."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/feed13m.html" }
      - { label: "CPS", url: "https://caringforkids.cps.ca/handouts/health-conditions-and-treatments/fever_and_temperature_taking" }
  - title: "Development is a conversation, not a scorecard — but flag these"
    detail: "Milestones come with wide, forgiving ranges, and premature babies are measured by their adjusted age, so a baby who's a bit behind on one thing is usually just a baby. Still, a few patterns are worth raising with your doctor rather than waiting: if he doesn't respond to loud sounds at all, doesn't watch things as they move or follow with his eyes, never smiles at people, seems unusually stiff or notably floppy, or loses a skill he clearly had before. None of these is an emergency, but they're exactly the kind of thing your doctor wants brought forward early — the checklist is meant to start that conversation."

  - title: "Parent mental health — still worth a check, even now"
    detail: "The baby blues fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, or trouble bonding that lingers for weeks is postpartum depression or anxiety, which can begin or deepen well after the newborn stage. It affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. Reach out to your family doctor or Health Link 811 if a low or anxious mood has hung around more than about two weeks, and treat any thought of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency — call 911 or the 988 suicide crisis line now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "Everything goes in the mouth now — and that's the point"
    detail: "Around now the mouth becomes his main research tool: fingers, fists, your shoulder, and any toy he can steer there all get gummed and slurped with great seriousness. Extra drool and hands-in-mouth are not a reliable sign that teeth are coming — most babies don't cut a first tooth until a few months from now — it's simply how he explores texture and shape. Keep the things within his reach clean and genuinely baby-safe with no small or breakable parts, and let him at it; this is early learning, not a habit to break."
    sources:
      - { label: "AAP", url: "https://www.healthychildren.org/English/ages-stages/baby/Pages/Movement-Birth-to-Three-Months.aspx" }
  - title: "A developmental leap — and a sleep wobble — may be on the horizon"
    detail: "If you've been enjoying longer stretches, you may hear other parents warn about the '4-month sleep regression.' Here's the reassuring version: as the brain matures over the coming weeks, sleep reorganizes into more adult-like cycles, and some babies who were sleeping longer briefly start waking more while everything rewires — often alongside a burst of new skills and alertness. Not every baby has a rough patch, and the ones who do come out the other side. If it arrives, it isn't a step backward or anything you broke; hold your safe-sleep basics and your gentle bedtime anchor steady, and it passes."
    sources:

  - title: "The fog really does start to lift — take the walk"
    detail: "Somewhere in this stretch a lot of parents notice the newborn haze thinning: the smiles land, the coos answer back, and the days feel a shade more predictable, which makes the whole thing more fun and less relentless. Protect that momentum — bank sleep when you can, say yes to the offered help, and get outside for a daily walk, which is good for both of you and never was the risky part of leaving the house. Feeling flat instead of lighter as the weeks pass is worth mentioning to your doctor; a calm, supported parent is genuinely part of the baby's care."
