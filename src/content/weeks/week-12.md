title: "Three months — the end of the fourth trimester"
description: "The first-quarter milestone: a steady head, hands that finally do things, coos that turn into back-and-forth 'conversations,' and — right on schedule — crying now well down the far side of its peak. The next shots land at four months."
milestones:
  - title: "A steady head, and pushing up to see the world"
    detail: "By around three months most babies hold the head steady without support when you hold them upright, and on the tummy they push up on the forearms — some even briefly onto the hands — to lift the head and chest and look around. The old flaily newborn jerks have smoothed into more controlled arm and leg movements, the legs are straightening out and kicking with purpose, and a few babies are already rocking toward their first roll (usually a 3–4 month event, no rush). Keep offering short tummy sessions through the day: this is the neck, shoulder, and core work that head control, rolling, and sitting are all built on. Ranges are wide — 'around three months' describes what most babies are doing, not a line your baby has to have crossed by a certain day."

  - title: "Hands become tools — opening, mouthing, batting, and grabbing"
    detail: "The fists that stayed clenched all newborn are opening up: around now he'll carefully open and shut his hands, bring them together, and bring them to his mouth — and, as far as a three-month-old is concerned, his own hands are the most fascinating toy ever invented, worth staring at for long stretches. He'll start swiping and swatting at things dangling just out of reach, and if you tuck a light rattle into his palm he may hold it and even shake it for a moment before it tumbles free. Give that new skill something to do: dangle a high-contrast toy about a foot above his chest so he can take aim, and let him work at reaching, missing, and connecting. Grabbing on purpose and reliably holding on come over the next month or two, so brief catches now are exactly on track."

  - title: "Watching, following, and knowing you across the room"
    detail: "Vision has stretched far past feeding distance: his visual range now reaches several yards, he follows a moving face or toy smoothly with his eyes, and he watches faces closely — recognizing familiar people, and lighting up for you, from across the room. Use it by moving slowly side to side to invite his eyes to track you, and let him study your face, still the single most interesting pattern in his world. The random eye-wandering or occasional crossing of the newborn weeks should be fading by two to three months; eyes that still consistently cross or drift are worth mentioning at a checkup. He'll answer a familiar face with a hesitant smile, then coos and 'body talk,' while a stranger may get only a long, curious stare — that's early social sorting, not shyness to worry about."

  - title: "Coos, squeals, chuckles, and the first real 'conversations'"
    detail: "This is the month the sound show gets good: strings of vowel coos ('oooo,' 'aahh'), squeals, gurgles, the first chuckles (not quite a belly laugh yet), and — the magic part — sounds made straight back at you when you talk, plus a head that turns toward your voice. He may start imitating some of your sounds and expressions, discovering that just moving his lips can start a 'conversation.' So have them: when he coos, answer with a sound or a word, then leave a pause for his reply, and go back and forth. This turn-taking feels silly but is exactly the input early language runs on — and there's a wide normal range, with some babies chatty before two months and others closer to four."

todos:
  - title: "Build tummy time toward about an hour a day — in little spurts"
    detail: "Now that pushing up is paying off, the goal shifts from tolerating tummy time to accumulating it: aim to work up toward roughly an hour a day by three months, banked in short, frequent sessions rather than one long stretch — a few minutes after each nap and diaper change adds up fast. Make it worth his while: get down face-to-face so he's lifting to look at you, put a mirror or a high-contrast toy just ahead to reach for, or try tummy-to-tummy on your chest and the football/tummy-down carry on fussier days. Always supervised and always on the floor, never on a couch or bed he could roll off, and back to the crib for actual sleep. If he melts down, stop and try again later — many short wins beat one miserable marathon."

  - title: "Put the four-month shots on the calendar"
    detail: "There's no routine immunization visit at three months, so this is a heads-up week: Alberta's next round lands at four months, and it's four vaccines — DTaP-IPV-Hib-HB (the combined diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Hib, and hepatitis B needle again), the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine, meningococcal conjugate (Men-C-C), and rotavirus (the one given by mouth, not a needle). Book it if you haven't, and remember the two-month soothing playbook still works — feeding or holding him skin-to-close during and right after the injections, and a calm voice, settles most babies within a minute or two. These are given at your public health / community health centre, so check whether your clinic needs the appointment booked ahead."

  - title: "Narrate, read, and sing — you're his language input"
    detail: "He learns language mostly from you, so flood him with it: name what you're doing and what he's looking at, read simple board books and nursery rhymes while he studies the pictures, and sing — the tune matters more than the voice. Follow his lead with 'serve and return' — when he coos, gurgles, or looks at something, answer it with words, a smile, or the same sound, then wait for his turn — because that back-and-forth, not passive screen sound, is what wires early language. There's no such thing as too young to be read to, and a few minutes woven through the day beats a scheduled 'lesson.'"

feeding:
  - title: "Feeds are fewer, faster, and more efficient"
    detail: "Intake is still all liquid and still generous, but it's consolidating. By three months a breastfed baby often nurses around 8 times in 24 hours, while bottle-fed babies tend to eat a little less often — roughly every 4 hours — and may take up to about 6–7 oz (177–207 ml) per feed, some more and some less. A useful ceiling for formula still holds: about 2½ oz (75 ml) per pound of body weight per day, up to roughly 32 oz (960 ml) in 24 hours. He's also become a more efficient nurser, so a feed that now takes ten minutes instead of thirty isn't a supply problem — it's skill; the 'a feed must last X minutes' rule was never real."

  - title: "Trust the baby, the diapers, and the growth curve — not the clock"
    detail: "You still don't need to count ounces or minutes to know it's working: roughly six or more wet diapers a day, a baby who settles and seems satisfied after feeds, and steady tracking along his own line on the growth curve are the report card. Growth naturally slows a touch from the newborn rocket-ship pace this month, and that's expected — what matters is a steady curve, not a dramatic one. Keep reading hunger cues (rooting, hand-sucking, stirring) rather than waiting for the late cry, and if feeding still hurts or worries you, a lactation consultant or Health Link 811 can help — it's easier to fix sooner than later."

sleep:
  - title: "Sleep is getting more patterned — and a longer night stretch may be showing up"
    detail: "Babies this age still sleep a lot — roughly 14–17 hours across a full day, most of it in a few daytime naps plus a longer haul at night — but the timing is starting to organize itself into something you can loosely predict. Many three-month-olds offer one longer run overnight now, though 'sleeping through the night' at this age officially means only a 5–6 hour stretch, and plenty of babies aren't there yet — that's normal, not behind. Keep building the day-night rhythm the slow way: days bright, social, and full of daylight; nights dark, quiet, boring, and low-key for feeds and changes."
    sources:
      - { label: "Nemours", url: "https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/sleep13m.html" }
  - title: "A heads-up about the four-month bump — and the rules that don't change"
    detail: "Fair warning, framed reassuringly: babies don't settle into mature, adult-like sleep cycles until around four months, and as that rewiring happens some babies briefly wake more, fight naps, or lose a stretch they'd 'figured out' — the popular name is the four-month sleep regression, and it's a sign of development, not backsliding, and it passes. Nothing you did causes it and no single trick cures it; ride it out with the same consistent, boring nights. What never changes: back for every sleep, naps included, on a firm flat surface, in his own crib or bassinet in your room, with nothing loose in it — no blankets, pillows, bumpers, or positioners — and if he konks out in a swing, carrier, or car seat, move him to the flat crib when you reasonably can."

redFlags:
  - title: "Fever in a baby this young is still an urgent rule, not a wait-and-see"
    detail: "With no vaccines this month, there's no 'expected' post-shot fever to explain a temperature away — so a rectal reading of 38.0°C (100.4°F) or higher still means a baby this young needs to be seen right away, not medicated and watched at home. Take the temperature rectally for accuracy at this age, and don't give infant acetaminophen and then sit on it — call. For anything short of that, Health Link 811 (24/7) will help you sort urgent from watchful, and trust 'he's just not himself' as a real reason to call."

  - title: "Development you'd want a doctor to look at"
    detail: "The milestone lists are wide-range and meant as a conversation-starter, not a scorecard — but a few things are worth raising, and there's no three-month checkup to catch them, so flag them yourself. Mention it if by around three to four months he doesn't watch things as they move or follow with his eyes, doesn't smile at people, can't hold his head steady when held upright, doesn't make sounds or coo back, doesn't bring his hands to his mouth, or seems to have lost a skill he clearly had. Any of these is a reason to call your doctor — not a diagnosis, just a look — and premature babies are measured by their adjusted age, so subtract the early weeks before you compare."

  - title: "Feeding refusal, forceful vomiting, or a baby who's 'off'"
    detail: "Call the doctor for feeding refusal, worries about weight gain, repeated or forceful (projectile) vomiting — different from the easy, dribbly spit-up that's still normal at this age — diarrhea, or a baby who's much sleepier than usual and hard to rouse. By three months you know his baseline better than anyone, which is exactly why a gut feeling that something's wrong is worth a call, and Health Link 811 will take it seriously at any hour. Bloody stools or any trouble breathing mean seek care immediately."

  - title: "Parent mental health — three months in is a real checkpoint"
    detail: "The 'baby blues' fade within a couple of weeks of birth; sadness, hopelessness, losing pleasure in things, constant anxiety, rage, or trouble bonding that's still hanging on at three months is postpartum depression or anxiety territory — it affects roughly 1 in 8 new mothers, partners can develop it too, and it is very treatable. Three months in, with the fog of the newborn weeks lifting, is exactly when a lot of parents realize the low mood isn't lifting with it — that's information, not failure. Call your family doctor or Health Link 811 if it's lingering, and treat any thoughts of harming yourself or the baby as an emergency: call 911 or the 988 Suicide Crisis Helpline now."

alsoWorthKnowing:
  - title: "The crying is well down the far side of its peak now"
    detail: "If the worst weeks felt endless, here's the payoff: intense infant crying typically peaks around the two-month mark and then eases over the following weeks, settling down by three to four months — the arc the 'Period of PURPLE Crying' describes (Peak, Unexpected, Resists soothing, Pain-like face, Long-lasting, Evening clustering). At three months most families are clearly past the summit, with the late-afternoon and evening fussing winding down. It was never caused by anything you did or didn't do, and the calmer stretch you're moving into is real. One thing that never expires: if crying ever pushes you to the edge, it is always safe to put him down on his back in the crib and step away to breathe — never, ever shake a baby."

It's worth pausing to notice how far both of you have come — the person who brought this baby home twelve weeks ago was doing something genuinely hard, and got here. Take the photo, write the thing down, tell someone. The days were long; the quarter was fast."

  - title: "He may 'talk' to the ceiling fan, the mirror, and his own hands"
    detail: "Around now babies start holding long, animated one-sided chats with fascinating inanimate objects — the ceiling fan, a mirror, a patterned curtain, and above all their own hands — cooing and squealing at them, sometimes blowing bubbles and raspberries. He doesn't yet know the baby in the mirror is him, but he finds that other cutie delightful and may even smile at him. It looks eccentric and is completely normal: it's a brain practicing attention, cause-and-effect, and sound-making on whatever holds still long enough. A baby-safe mirror and simple high-contrast toys are the cheapest entertainment you'll ever buy."
