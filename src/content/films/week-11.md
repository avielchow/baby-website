## The story

Week 2 refused to show a clock, because clocks had stopped meaning anything — time was a patch of sunlight crawling up a nursery wall. Week 11 does the opposite on purpose: it prints the time on screen, plainly, all day long. **6:47. 9:15. 1:30. 7:05.** Because the astonishing thing about this week is that those numbers mean something now. There is a wake-up time. There is a nap that happens because she recognizes the steps that lead to it. There is — whisper it — an evening. The film is one real day, dawn to couch, and its only special effect is that the schedule exists.

**Open** at 6:47 on the miracle nobody warned them about: she wakes up *pleasant*. Chatting to the ceiling, hands finding each other, in no hurry. A face appears over the rail and she lights up — recognition, on schedule. Breakfast happens in morning light in the same chair that used to only know 3 a.m. The getting-dressed negotiation follows, sleeves versus fists, narrated like a sports broadcast.

**Middle** is the day doing its rounds. The 9:15 put-down is the thesis shot: the sack zips and her body goes loose, because she knows what comes next — anticipation, the newest thing she owns. The 11 o'clock play window gives the film its widest breath: a coo rally with long, patient gaps, then batting practice in slow motion — hands to the midline, the swing, the astonished self-review. The 1:30 walk takes the film outside under real sky. And at 4:40 the film keeps its honesty clause: one brief, unglamorous shot of the daily rough patch, because an ordinary day is a documentary, not a brochure.

**Close** with the long descent into evening: dinner made with a supervisor riding in the carrier (on the prep side of the kitchen, always), the bath and its full-body kicks, and then the liturgy — book, song, sack, down, the same order every single night, jump-cut inside one unmoving frame like turning pages. The hand on her chest for three breaths. The monitor's little grayscale glow on the coffee table. And then the destination: 8:12, two glasses, a couch, a conversation in normal voices — an hour that belongs to two adults again, which no earlier film could have contained because it did not exist. Black. **6:47** — *and then we did it all again.*

## Mood & look

Settled and diurnal. Where the early films split into day-world and night-world, this one is a single continuous arc that changes colour the way a day actually does: blue-gold at dawn, white and a little overbright through the middle, stripy at 4:40, honey from the bath onward, lamp-and-monitor-glow at the end. The WB strategy carries that arc on set — 5600K locked for every daylight chapter so dawn reads blue and noon reads white, 4300K locked from the kitchen onward so the tungsten evening goes gold — and the grade's job is to protect the differences, not erase them. Skin brightness steps through the day on the scopes like a sundial. Even the grain follows the clock: cleanest at noon, thickest at 7:31.

The chapter cards are the signature and they are deliberately boring: small off-white numerals, lower-left, cut in and out with no animation, like entries in a field notebook. The restraint matters — the numbers are only miraculous because of how ordinary they look. Framing language: locked-off patience for the routines (the routines are the stars and they perform unprompted), the 85mm for recognition and the lay-down, the 50mm as workhorse, the 28mm for geography — the play mat, the liturgy proscenium, the widening pull that ends the film. The RS3 goes outside at 1:30 and comes back for the last shot; the Pocket 3 handles the kitchen; slow motion appears exactly once, for a swing and a hit.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The one-day ideal.** This film wants to be shot in a single real day — the light arc, the wardrobe, the energy curve all match for free, and the day you shoot is genuinely the day you show. Pick a day with no appointments, charge everything the night before, and pre-rig the two standing setups before bed: the crib framing for shot 1 (tripod placed, focus set, well clear of the crib) and a card/battery check across all three cameras. Then shoot the day *as it happens* — the camera goes to the schedule, never the reverse. Most setups are locked-off and take two minutes to plant; the whole day costs maybe forty minutes of actual operating.

**The fallback batching plan.** If the day fights back — and with a baby, some days do — split the film across two or three days **by chapter, matched to the clock**: morning chapters (shots 1–5) on day one, midday (6–8) on day two, evening (9–15) on whichever evenings cooperate, each shot filmed within half an hour of its stated time so the light is honest. The one hard pairing rule: shots 12 and 13 are the same ritual from two positions and must be shot on *different* nights — one camera, one operator, and the bedtime rite is never interrupted to reposition. Keep her outfit consistent within each chapter (nobody will clock a morning-vs-afternoon change; the day plausibly includes one anyway, likely at 7:40).

**WB discipline, stated once and taped to the camera:** 5600K locked for shots 1–9 (all daylight chapters — dawn's blue and 4:40's gold are the point), 4300K locked for shots 10–15 (the mixed and tungsten evening). Never auto, never split within a chapter. ISO strategy rides the same arc: second base 3200 for dawn and everything after the bath, 800 through the bright middle, ceiling 6400 (12800 only for shot 13's dimmed lamp). Outdoors at 1:30 is the one exposure problem indoors never poses — variable ND if you have it, f/5.6–f/8 or 1/100 if you don't.

**Standing safety rules, every setup, no exceptions:** lights low, weighted, and away from her — never above or near the crib, the changing table, or anywhere they could tip toward her; nothing hot in reach; no flash, ever; no camera or gimbal path passing over the baby — the crib shots are shot *beside* the rail, through the slats; staged sleep always back-down in a bare crib, sleep sack only, which is both the rule and the composition. Kitchen chapter: the carrier parent works the prep side only — never the stove front, never over hot oil, splatter, or steam, never carrying anything hot — and the stove belongs to the other parent, pot handles turned in. Bathroom chapter: no powered lights near water, camera planted at the doorway out of the splash zone, and both of the parent's hands on the baby every second while the camera runs unattended. And the oldest rule still governs: her schedule wins every argument. It's just that this week — for the first time — the schedule is also the film.
