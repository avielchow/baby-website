## The story

The fourth trimester is the name for what these twelve films have been
about without saying it: the three months a newborn spends finishing the
womb's work out in the open — and the three months her parents spend being
born as parents alongside her. This film ends that era the only way this
series could: by going back to its own frames and standing in them again.

**Open — the third push (shot 1).** Film 1 opened on an empty nursery and
closed on the same push with a sleeping newborn in it. The finale walks
that push a third time, and the room has stopped holding its breath
entirely: play mat, crib, board books, a person on the floor narrating to
the olive tree. Two seconds of the original empty room flash in — the
audience's memory confirmed, the device declared — and the title lands.

**Middle — the frames, revisited (shots 2–13).** The film moves through
the series' signature frames with tape-mark discipline: the front door,
where a sleeping car seat has become an upright inspector (film 1); the
feeding chair's famous framing, sixth pass, now with a fist gripping a
shirt collar (film 2); the 3 a.m. frame, lamp dark, chair empty, because
she sleeps — the happiest empty frame in twelve weeks (film 2); the
hallway follow shot at a scandalously reasonable hour (films 1, 2, and 5's
long evening, forgiven); the hand-in-palm scale shot split-screened
against itself (films 1 and 9); the tummy-time frame where one impossible
second has become a held push-up with time left over for a toy (film 3).
Between the echoes, the film plants what only month three could grow: a
head that holds itself, hands with actual jobs, a smile launched across a
whole room (films 6 and 7, graduated), the laugh — confirmed, repeatable,
caught again in 60p (film 10, paid off twice). And the monthly portrait
from film 4 is made for the third time in the same spot, so months one,
two, and three can stand shoulder to shoulder and say everything without
a caption.

**Close — the family, the card, the door (shots 14–15).** One wide frame,
everybody in it — parents, baby, whatever cats consent — held longer than
any shot in the series, until the family forgets the camera and becomes
itself. Black. A small lowercase card: *end of the fourth trimester.*
Then the last frame the series will ever add: the entryway, a high chair
still in its box, the stroller waiting by the front door — the same door
everything came through in film 1 — and a slow drift that settles on it
and holds. The newborn era ends facing the door it began at, with the
future leaning against the wall, waiting its turn.

## Mood & look

Valedictory, but dry-eyed until it isn't. The finale's discipline is
sameness: same lenses, same S-curve, same warm-amber/teal split, same
grain, same milky blacks — because the entire emotional technology of this
film is that the *filmmaking* refuses to change while everything inside
the frames does. Resist every instinct to make the finale "bigger." No new
look, no montage speed, no swelling drone. The THEN footage is regraded
from its original sources through this project's one tree, so a
twelve-week-old clip and yesterday's clip wear identical skin, and the
only visible difference across a match cut is her.

The pairs carry the structure: cut on identical framing, hold both sides
longer than feels efficient, and spend the two split-screens where
measurement beats montage — her hand, her push-up. Days are window-soft,
the one night frame is honestly near-black, the door and its glass bloom
warm as they always have. The final family wide is allowed one indulgence:
a slightly deeper vignette, because that frame is the photograph this
whole series was secretly for.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The match kit — build it before shooting anything.** This film is made
in prep. Assemble: (1) frame-grabs of every frame being re-created —
film 1 shots 1, 3, 7, 14; film 2 shots 3, 11, 14; film 3 shot 7; film 4's
portrait framing — exported to the phone at full quality; (2) the original
guides open in a tab, because every re-creation shot above restates its
original's settings and the originals are the tiebreaker; (3) painter's
tape, and the week-2 taping method (L-brackets around the tripod feet,
witness marks across pan and tilt). Re-marking a lost position: stand
roughly on the diagram's spot, hold the frame-grab beside the a7CII
monitor, and walk the tripod until door jambs, rails, and furniture edges
sit in the same screen positions — then tape everything and photograph the
marks. Ten patient minutes per frame, done once, before she's in the room.

**Batch by room and light, as always.**
- *Nursery, mid-morning:* shots 1 and 9 share the room and hour — mark
  both, then shoot 1 early in the week and 9 during a real nap. Shot 7's
  blanket-by-the-window session joins the same light.
- *Living room, mid-morning through noon:* 3, 8, 11, 12 ride her alert
  windows — two shots per window, never more, across several days.
- *The chair:* shot 4 at a real daytime feed; shot 5 is sixty seconds of
  some honest 3 a.m. — set the frame at bedtime, roll on the way past.
- *Hallway, 7 p.m.:* shot 6 during the actual bedtime walk.
- *Portrait morning:* shot 13, on or near her three-month day, same hour
  as months one and two.
- *Golden hour, one good evening:* shot 14 — the one shot to protect the
  calendar for. Everyone home, cats fed late so dinner-bribery lands.
- *Any time, no baby:* shots 2's re-marks and 15 — the entryway shot can
  be made during any nap.

**Three-month safety, updated and non-negotiable.** She rolls now, both
ways, sometimes the moment you look away — so: never unattended on ANY
raised surface, not the bed, not the beanbag, not the changing pad, not
"for one second"; floor is the default set. Every framed scene with her
on furniture has a spotter within arm's reach, in frame or just out of
it. She grabs now, too: camera straps, cables, and anything small stay
outside her reach radius, which is longer than you think. The standing
rules hold as ever — no lights above or near her where they could tip,
nothing hot close, no flash at her, camera and gimbal never passing over
her, takes short around feeding and sleeping. Safe sleep at three months:
down on her back in a bare crib, swaddle retired now that she rolls,
arms free. And the oldest rule in the series, one last time: the shot
list is a menu, not a contract. She has been the director for twelve
weeks. The crew is just grateful for the work.
