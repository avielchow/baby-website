## The story

**Before — loud, close, dark (Shots 1–7).** The film opens at the top of the
crying curve and doesn't flinch: an 85mm sliver of a parent pacing the
evening living room, a red face over a shoulder, hard lamp shadows, cuts
that come too fast to rest on. The six-week growth spurt compresses into
three jump-cut frames from the feeding chair — latch, kneading hand, burp
cloth, again. In the middle of the din, a quieter thread: Dad building the
smile trap — a camera staged at the changing table, framed, exposed, focus
taped, powered, one button from rolling — because this is the week the
first real smile tends to arrive, and this family intends to be pointing a
camera at it. The peak crests (the wall of evening, two spent faces on the
couch), the trap collects its false alarms — gas grins, sleep-smiles,
mouths that almost commit — and then one soft morning frame: his eyes
locked on a face at exactly the distance smiles are born, deciding.

**The turn (Shot 8).** Four frames of black. Total silence. Then the trap's
own frame — the one we watched being built — and in it, in slow motion,
his whole face answers a parent's for the first time. The film pivots on
this single shot: it is the exact middle, the longest hold, and the moment
the grade, the pace, the sound floor, and the house itself all change.

**After — open, bright, slow (Shots 9–15).** The reverse angle is laughter;
the sheers come open and stay open. Mom leaves for her own appointment —
the postpartum checkup, the film's quiet role-reversal, shot from behind
Dad and the baby as she exits into the bright doorway — and the camera
stays home for Dad's wide, sunlit shift. She comes back and the smile is
aimed at her, live, with sound. Then the second half stretches out: the
full-production smile session (officially also the guilt-free fallback
slot if the trap missed smile number one), a long slow slide across the
same rooms the dark half happened in, and finally the answer to the
opening shot — the same evening hold, twice the light, and a wobbly head
lifting over a shoulder to smile straight down the lens. Fade to black.
His name, the date, and a second line the series has been waiting six
weeks to write: *first smile.*

## Mood & look

Two films in one house, hinged at the middle. The first half borrows the
series' warmth and turns it dense: single-source lamps, hard sides,
negative fill, faces at 45 IRE, a vignette holding the frame tight, cuts
at the pace of a fraying evening. It should feel close and airless — not
horror, but honestly heavy, the way the loudest week actually sits on a
household. The second half is the same family look with every window
thrown open: lifted blacks lifted further, shadows opened, clean warm
highlights, the vignette relaxing its grip, holds that let a room breathe.
Crucially, the halves are siblings, not strangers — same S-curve backbone,
same halation, same grain — built as two Resolve group grades so the
difference is engineered and consistent, never shot-by-shot mood swings.
The proof of concept is Shots 1 and 15: the same room at the same hour,
one dense and one open, and the only true difference is the week that
happened in between. If the pivot reads with the sound off, the grade did
its job.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The trap dogma — this week's one non-negotiable habit.** Rig the smile
trap on day one (Shot 3) and then live by two rules: (1) the trap is
never touched — same frame, same tape, same exposure logic all week; (2)
**record at every alert window.** Every diaper change, the red button
before he's on the mat. Every post-feed sit-up, one tap on the Pocket 3
before the cloth goes over the shoulder. Each morning, a two-minute trap
inspection with the coffee: power bank charged and connected, card space,
frame undisturbed, a 5-second test clip. It costs almost nothing and it
is the entire difference between having the first smile and having a
story about it.

**The fallback doctrine, in writing so nobody relitigates it at 2am.** If
the true first smile happens off-camera — at 4am, mid-feed, facing the
wrong way — the protocol is: enjoy it completely, tell each other about
it, write the date down, and shoot smile three-or-four with full
production within the next day or two (Shots 7's coaxing method + 13's
setup, traps rolling). Zero guilt is not a consolation; it's the plan's
design margin. Smiles, once on, repeat and get easier. Withholding your
own delighted face to "save" the real one for camera is the only actual
way to fail this week.

**Batch by light and energy, not shot number.**
- *Day 1 (any nap):* Shot 3 — build both traps. Twenty minutes,
  changes everything.
- *Evenings, across the week:* Shots 1 and 4 in 20-second fragments,
  only ever with two adults present; Shot 5 once, on a night after a
  guarded-sleep-block handover; Shot 15 late in the week on a calm
  evening (attempt two evenings, keep the best).
- *Mornings (the bright window, ~8–11am):* the traps do their work
  (Shots 6 and 8 accumulate themselves); Shot 7 at one good alert
  window; Shots 9 and 13 after the smile switch flips.
- *Checkup day:* Shot 10 at the real departure (camera ready ten
  minutes early), Shot 11 during the solo stretch (static-wide
  fallback if Dad is alone, which he is — plan the RS3 arc for a day
  the other parent can operate, or embrace the tripod), Shot 12
  framed five minutes before she's due home.
- *Any bright midday nap:* Shot 14's slide. Walk the lane empty first.

**Directing around a peaking-crying baby — the order of operations.**
Soothe first, always; film only with a second adult on soothing duty;
20-second grabs, then the camera goes down. If the crying has an adult
at their edge, the film stops existing: baby on his back in the bare
crib, door, breathe, tag in — the same safety plan as the week guide,
and it outranks every frame in this file. Nothing in this film is worth
capturing badly enough to delay comfort, and the footage will be better
for it anyway: the film wants parents present, not parents performing
endurance.

**Checkup-day note, because the film is also the family.** Her
appointment is the headline of the week off-camera too. The shot list
treats it with one departure, one wide, one homecoming — total imposition
on her: about ninety seconds of being filmed doing things she was doing
anyway. If she'd rather the camera skipped any of it, it skips it; Shots
11 and 13 can carry the second half. The film salutes either way.

**Battery, cards, ND — the pit-stop, plus trap duty.** The nightly habit
grows one step this week: all batteries charging (a7CII spares, Pocket 3,
RS3 Mini, the trap's power bank), cards offloaded to two places, trap
footage triaged (keep smiles and almosts, archive the rest — never let
the trap card fill overnight), cards formatted in-camera only after the
second copy verifies. The a7CII sleeps in its 4K24 / 1/50 / S-Log3 /
ISO 800 / 5600K base state — Shot 9 exists because a grab-camera starts
correct. The variable ND lives on the 28mm this week; the second half's
flooded windows are where it earns its keep, and a missing ND changes
your aperture, never your shutter.

**Safety, restated because it's non-negotiable:** no light above or near
the baby where it could tip; nothing warm close to him; no camera or
gimbal ever passing over him — the trap stands at the changing table's
foot, the RS3 arcs and slides run lanes beside, never across; staged
sleep is always real safe sleep (back, bare bassinet); every setup around
a feeding or sleeping baby is short; and at the crying peak the standing
rule gets bolder type: **the camera is the first thing to be put down.**
He's the director. This week especially, he's also allowed to fire the
crew.
