## The story

**Open — someone is talking (Shots 1–2).** Fade in mid-sentence: a face,
enormous at 85mm, delivering one earnest coo to somebody just off-lens. No
context, no title, no explanation — because none of us got one either; one
day this week he simply started holding up his end. Then the film steps
back and shows the set: a chair, a window, a blanket, an olive tree. The
title lands here. Every dialogue film establishes its location once so it
can stop explaining and start cutting; ours does the same.

**Middle — the scene, covered properly (Shots 3–8).** The heart of the film
is one ordinary conversation between Mom and a seven-week-old, shot the way
cinema has shot two people talking for a hundred years: a master two-shot,
an over-the-shoulder, a clean single, the reverse, cutaways to the hands
that gesture through every remark. The joke is that the grammar fits
perfectly — serve-and-return cooing IS dialogue, turn-taking and all, and
the coverage doesn't condescend to it for a second. Inside the scene sit
the two milestone beats the week is actually about: the pause — a held
two-shot where Mom serves and then visibly waits, a beat longer than
comfort allows, until the answer comes, because the gap is where the
back-and-forth gets wired in — and the smile, his most reliable line now,
blooming at 40% speed as total agreement. Then the film widens the cast:
Dad takes an evening meeting in lamplight and a lower register; a
grandparent conducts a conference call from inside a propped-up phone; a
cat attends silently, taking minutes. And once, gloriously, he holds the
floor alone — a monologue delivered to the olive tree and the mobile,
subtitled by the edit with the deadpan it deserves: [extended remarks on
the ceiling fan].

**Close — the filibuster (Shots 14–15).** Three frozen mouth shapes — the
O, the A, the gape — a pronunciation chart for a language with three
speakers. And then the ending refuses to cut: the longest exchange of the
week, played in a single unbroken take of the master frame. Serve, answer,
serve, answer. After a film assembled from angles, the finale needs none —
the week's real news is that the conversation can carry itself now. The
picture fades while he's still mid-thought; his voice runs a beat into
the black. He gets the last word. He'll be getting it for years.

## Mood & look

The register is new for the series: still the A24 home movie — window key,
shallow primes, lifted blacks, halation on the lamps — but this is the
funny-tender installment, and the comedy is all structural. Nothing on
screen mugs; the joke lives in the form (full dialogue coverage lavished on
a person with no words, subtitles rationed to three-and-a-bit deadpan
translations, a cat framed like a board member) while the content stays
completely sincere. Play everything straight and the film is both funnier
and more moving than either alone.

Palette: the warm-amber/teal split as always, morning-bright in the
conversation corner, lamp-warm for Dad's evening scene, one deliberately
cool accent — the phone screen's glow in the conference call, left
uncorrected because that faint blue IS long-distance love in 2026. Grade
priority one is invisible coverage: the four angles of the Mom scene must
match so exactly that the audience never feels the days between setups.

Pace: quicker cutting than any week so far inside the covered scene —
that's what coverage is for — bought back with three long refusals to cut:
the pause, held; the smile, slowed; the filibuster, unbroken. A
conversation film breathes in its gaps. So does a conversation.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The menu rule, restated first:** fifteen shots is a menu, not a contract.
His schedule wins every collision. The difference this week is that the
film's main scene needs its setups to MATCH — so the plan below is mostly
a continuity scheme wearing a schedule's clothes.

**How one parent shoots a dialogue scene alone.** Every coverage setup is a
locked tripod: frame on the empty seat, set focus, roll, then walk into the
scene and be the scene partner. The camera operates itself; you operate the
conversation. Check the first minute of each setup before trusting it, then
stop checking and start talking.

**Coverage in passes — the continuity kit.** You will not get master, OTS,
single, reverse, and inserts in one alert window; you'll get one, maybe two
setups per session across two or three days, and the edit will stitch them
into "one" conversation. What makes that stitch invisible:

- *Same onesie.* Pick the week's costume on day one, keep it for every
  Mom-scene session, wash it nightly. This is the single loudest continuity
  variable — a onesie change between the OTS and the reverse reads
  instantly.
- *Same set.* Chair, pillow, blanket, muslin live in their marked spots;
  Shot 2's establishing frame is your reference photo. Tape the tripod feet
  for the master (Shots 3, 7, and 15 reuse those exact marks).
- *Same hour.* Mid-morning, every session — the window's angle is the
  scene's key light, and light direction is continuity too. A 9:30 master
  cut against a 4pm reverse looks like two different days because it is.
- *Same settings.* The a7CII stays parked at the scene state — 4K24, 1/50,
  S-Log3, ISO 800, WB 5600K — between sessions. Never auto-anything.
- *Same serve.* Keep your side of the conversation in one register across
  passes: soft voice, big smile, real pauses. Your performance is the other
  half of the match.

**The batch plan.**
- *Day 1, mid-morning:* Shot 2 (the set, during a nap) + Shot 3 (master) in
  one window. Tape everything.
- *Days 2–3, mid-morning:* one or two coverage setups per alert window —
  Shot 4, then Shot 1's single, then Shot 5 (the reverse needs the stick
  cheat, so set the bounce during the pre-session fuss). Shot 6's inserts
  and Shot 14's stills get stolen inside whichever session he's chattiest.
- *Any morning he's on form:* Shot 7 (the pause) — it's the master frame,
  so it can piggyback on any session at the tape marks. Shot 8 (the smile)
  on the clearest morning, for 60p's sake.
- *Calm evenings (two of them):* Shots 9 and 10, Dad's scene — same couch,
  same lamp, same clothes if it splits across nights. Week 7 sits just past
  the crying peak, and the guide-page truth applies to scheduling: the calm
  evenings are coming back, but if tonight melts down, the scene keeps till
  tomorrow.
- *Scheduled once:* Shot 11 — book the grandparent for a mid-morning call,
  phone charged, stand set, brightness at 60%.
- *Opportunistic all week:* Shot 12 (the cat attends when the cat attends)
  and Shot 13 (the monologue — any solo-chatty blanket session under the
  mobile).
- *Last good morning:* Shot 15, the filibuster — deliberately saved for the
  end of the week so the longest exchange is honestly the longest.

**Quiet house = usable dialogue.** This film's audio is its script: before
every session, fridge and HVAC off (set a phone reminder to turn them back
on), dishwasher done, phones silenced, iPhone recorder on the blanket edge,
one clap for sync. Thirty seconds of room tone before the gear comes down.

**The pit-stop habit, unchanged:** batteries charging nightly (a7CII
spares, RS3, Pocket 3 if it travels), cards offloaded to two places and
formatted in-camera only after the second copy verifies, ND living on the
50mm for the window work — a missing ND changes your aperture, never your
shutter.

**Safety, non-negotiable, week seven edition:** no light above or near the
baby where it could tip — the reverse's stick and bounce board sit two
meters back and low; nothing warm close to him; camera and tripod never
pass over him — the reverse OTS and the monologue's low rake both shoot
from BESIDE the blanket line, planned before he's on it; the mobile is
the only thing that hangs above him, by design; staged sleep is real safe
sleep, though this film has none — it's the awake-est film yet; and every
setup around him is short: build during the fuss, roll during the chat,
stop when he says the meeting's over. He's the director. This week he's
also the entire script department.
