## The story

**Open — the held breath (Shots 1–2).** The film starts before he does. A slow
push into a nursery that has everything except its person: the bare bassinet,
the olive tree, the teal frames, stacks of impossibly small folded clothes.
No music yet under the title — just room tone, a house holding its breath. We
linger here longer than feels comfortable in the edit, because the audience
(you, in ten years) needs to feel the waiting to feel the arrival.

**Middle — arrival, fog, and wonder (Shots 3–11).** The door opens and the film
inhales: the car seat crosses the threshold low and huge in the Pocket 3's
frame, the living room widens to receive him, and the house's original
residents — three cats — conduct their formal inspection at whisker height.
Then the tempo drops to the week's true speed: a window-lit feed that goes
nowhere and doesn't need to, slow-motion fingers and feet and that swirl of
hair, and the honest shot — a parent's thousand-yard stare over a sleeping
chest at dusk. A timelapse dissolves the day into night and the film enters
the small hours: the 3am hallway walk in silhouette, resolving into the
bedroom where the jade lamps pool their green-warm light on either side of
his bassinet. The fog and the wonder are the same shot list; that's the point.

**Close — morning, and the answer (Shots 12–15).** Morning arrives as warm
water: the first sponge bath by the kitchen window, outrage into peace. A
triptych of X100VI stills shows the gallery wall learning his face — his
print now hanging among the old life on the STAS rail. Both parents stand at
the bassinet doing the thing every new parent does (just watching), and then
the film returns to Shot 1's exact frame and exact push — same room, same
light, one lamp now on, bassinet now full, parents in the once-empty corner.
The house was waiting. Now it's his. Fade to black, his name, the date.

## Mood & look

Think of the film the way a memory plays back: soft-edged, warm where the
lamps were, a little slow, with the unimportant parts missing. The reference
register is the A24 home movie — shallow-focus domesticity, negative space,
compositions that would work as stills — crossed with the honest fatigue of
actual week one. Nothing is aspirational; everything is specific: THIS olive
tree, THESE jade lamps, THESE cats.

Palette: warm amber highlights (window glow, lamp cores) against teal-leaning
shadows, blacks lifted to milk, skin protected and gently evened. The jade
lamps contribute the film's one signature accent — a green-warm glow in the
night section that no LUT would invent and no other family owns. Daylight
scenes are bright but never crisp; night scenes are dark but never cold.
Halation blooms the practicals, grain keeps the digital sensor honest, and
the whole thing should feel like it was found in a drawer twenty years from
now, already precious.

Pace: long holds, cuts on stillness, two moments of total musical silence.
Ninety seconds that refuse to hurry — because the week refused to, and
because someday ninety seconds will feel far too short.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The rule above all rules: the shot list is a menu, not a contract.** Fifteen
shots across seven days is two-ish per day, and some days you will shoot
zero. That's the plan working, not failing. His schedule always wins; you're
not making a film this week, you're living a week that a film falls out of.

**Batch by room and light, not by shot number.**
- *Before he's home (or during an out-of-room nap):* Shots 1 and 2 in one
  morning session — the nursery in its waiting state, plus the tape mark on
  the floor for Shot 15. Twenty minutes total.
- *Arrival day:* Shot 3 (Pocket 3, one real take) and Shot 4 (a7CII, restaged
  five minutes later if needed). Charge everything the night before; this is
  the one unrepeatable day.
- *The mid-morning window (roughly 9–11am, when the nursery and living-room
  light is bright but indirect):* the daylight batch — 6, 7, 14 around feeds
  and naps, 13 any time, 12 on whatever morning the first bath happens.
- *Dusk/evening:* 5 (post-cat-dinner) and 8 (alternate evenings, shoot each
  other).
- *Night:* 9 started in late afternoon and left running; 10 and 11 on
  whichever night you have one drop of energy — they're the shortest setups
  in the film by design.

**Golden windows.** Learn your house's two: the mid-morning indirect window
and the last hour of light. Every "flat and lifeless" complaint in home
footage is really a "shot at noon" complaint.

**Battery, cards, ND — the pit-stop habit.** Every night while bottles get
washed: all batteries on chargers (a7CII spares, Pocket 3, RS3 Mini),
yesterday's cards offloaded to two places (laptop + drive), cards formatted
IN CAMERA only after the second copy is verified. Keep the a7CII pre-set to
the 4K24/1-50/S-Log3/ISO800/5600K base state so any grab-shot starts correct;
if you picked up the variable ND, it lives ON the 50mm — the window shots are
where it earns its keep, and a missing ND should change your aperture, never
your shutter.

**Safety, restated because it's non-negotiable:** no light above or near the
baby where it could tip, nothing warm close to him, no camera or gimbal ever
passing over him, staged sleep is always real safe sleep (back, bare
bassinet), and every setup around a feeding or sleeping baby is short — set
up during the fuss, shoot during the calm, stop the moment he says stop.
He's the director. You're just the crew.
