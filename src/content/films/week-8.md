## The story

**Open — ordinary, on purpose (Shots 1–4).** The film begins by lying to
nobody and telling nothing: a window-lit feed like any other morning, and
then the week's actual miracle — the real two-month social smile, caught in
a patch of sun, with the title landing quietly on top of it. Only then does
the day tip its hand, and only in objects: a diaper bag packed with unusual
care, and four items in a row on the kitchen table — health card, an
immunization booklet with a blank first page, the soother, the comfort
blanket that lives in arms and car seats and never in the crib. No one says
where we're going. The table already said it.

**Middle — the journey and the door (Shots 5–11).** The car tightens the
frame: his small face in the rear seat, the world sliding by, the passenger
parent's camera braced and unremarkable. The community health centre gets
one wide, plain sentence of a shot, and then the film goes small and
inward — his hand on a chest in the waiting room, a bag at a chair leg,
never anyone else's morning. The appointment itself is a closed door, held.
It is parents' hands wrapped around his, framed at lap height, nothing
clinical in the world. And when the moment comes, the film keeps its one
promise absolutely: no needle, no room, no depiction — an empty hallway, a
held frame, and the true sound of it, two seconds of cry dissolving into
low voices and then into the settling breath of a comfort feed. The device
is restraint, and restraint turns out to hold more than showing ever could.

**Close — the after (Shots 12–15).** The evidence, when it finally comes, is
nearly a joke: one small round bandage on a thigh the size of your wrist.
Then the aftercare sheet becomes the shot list — the heavy sleepy afternoon
draped over a parent's chest, the cool cloth laid on the sore spot, the
thermometer standing watch on the side table. And then evening: lamplight,
both parents, one drowsy half-smile that answers the morning's full one,
and the booklet on the table with its first page filled in — today's date
in a nurse's handwriting. He was brave. So, quietly, was everyone. Fade to
black; his name, the date, and one small line: *one needle, never shown.*

## Mood & look

The register is steadiness: this is the first film in the series where the
parents are protectors walking their person into something hard, and the
camera behaves accordingly — locked frames, patient holds, one single
tiptoe of a gimbal move all day. Home is warm and gets warmer as the film
goes; the clinic stays a few points cooler and flatter than everything
around it — not villainized, just *not home* — and that temperature gap is
the geography of the whole story. The film's emotional physics are simple:
the tighter the day gets, the smaller the frames get, until the entire
event compresses into a closed door and a sound. Then everything reopens,
shot by shot, back to couch-width by evening.

What the film refuses to do is its style. No needle, no exam room, no
staff, no other families, no dramatization of the cry — one honest
instance, at honest distance, or none at all. Restraint here isn't
squeamishness; it's the aesthetic argument that what happened *to him* is
two seconds long, and what happened *around him* — the hands, the packing,
the cloth, the booklet — is the actual story, and it takes ninety seconds
to tell.

Palette per the series: amber-warm highlights against teal-leaning shadows,
milky lifted blacks, halation blooming the practicals, grain unifying three
cameras into one memory. Shot 15's lamp should bloom hardest of anything in
the film. The bandage macro is allowed to be the sharpest, cleanest frame
of the ninety seconds — the one thing the day left behind deserves focus.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The rule above all rules, restated for a harder day than most:** the shot
list is a menu, not a contract — and today it's a menu you may barely open.
This film has been designed so that its emotional spine (Shots 1–6 and
11–15) happens entirely at home, in the car, or outside; everything inside
the clinic is optional texture the edit already knows how to live without.
If the whole clinic section is a "no," you still shot the film.

**The ethics of filming in a public health clinic — read twice, follow
exactly.** Ask first: at the front desk, plainly — "is it okay if I quietly
film just our own baby while we wait?" — and for the hands and comfort-feed
beats, ask the nurse the same way. Whatever the answer, these rules never
bend: never film staff; never film other families or their children; never
film clinical areas, equipment, or paperwork that isn't yours; no lights,
no reflectors, no gimbal, no rig — the Pocket 3 held low at lap height or
nothing. During the injections themselves, no camera rolls, period; the
most you carry is an audio memo in a shirt pocket, itself asked-about. And
if any answer is no, the answer is no with a smile: the camera goes in the
bag, and the film — as written — simply cuts from the doors to the after.
Version B is planned to the frame; a refusal costs you nothing but a
texture beat, so never let getting a shot compete with being a parent in a
room full of other people's hard mornings.

**Batching, around a real appointment:**
- *The evening before:* Shot 4 (the four-item insert row and its focus
  pulls) while he sleeps in the other room — the film's fussiest camera
  work, done with zero time pressure. Charge everything; load fresh cards.
- *Appointment morning:* Shots 1 and 2 in the normal morning rhythm (feed,
  then smile-hunting in the sun patch), Shot 3 as you actually pack, Shot 5
  at the buckle-in and — only if two adults ride — briefly on the road.
- *At the centre:* Shot 6 from beside your own car, ten seconds. Then the
  asks, and Shots 7–11 exactly as permitted or not at all. The camera-parent
  carries only the Pocket 3 inside; the a7CII stays in the car.
- *Home, the rest of the day:* 11 (home version) in the first minutes back
  if the clinic version didn't happen, 12 at a real diaper change, 13–14 in
  the real heavy afternoon, 15 after the bedtime feed. None of these are
  staged; they're the aftercare the guide week prescribes, filmed while you
  do it.

**Aftercare is the shoot's co-director.** The expected script for the
afternoon: sleepier, fussier, off his feed a little, maybe a mild fever and
a sore spot — all normal for a day or two. The film's beats ARE the
comfort measures (cuddles, cool cloth on the site, gently moving that leg,
lighter layers and extra feeds if he runs warm), so the filming should
never be in tension with the caring; if it ever is, the camera loses
instantly. Dose questions go to the pharmacist, not the internet, and
anything beyond mild-and-fading goes to Health Link 811 — the film wraps
early and nobody thinks about it again.

**Safety, the standing block, verbatim in spirit:** no light above or near
the baby where it could tip, nothing warm close to him, no camera or gimbal
ever passing over him, staged sleep is always real safe sleep (back, bare
crib — and the comfort blanket the film features never enters it), the
gimbal stays home except for one slow indoor push, and in the car the
driver drives, the camera is held or braced by the passenger parent only,
and nothing is ever mounted near an airbag. Short setups around a baby
who's had a hard day; he's the director, and today of all days he has
final cut.
