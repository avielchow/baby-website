## The story

**Open — the premise (Shots 1–2).** A comedy needs a straight man, and the film
opens on the best in the business: her, dead center, symmetrical, gloriously
unimpressed, while the title settles over the stare. Then the wide: the play
mat as a stage, two adults limbering up like it's a title fight, and — already
rolling in the corner of frame — the camera that will wait all week for one
sound. The audience knows the goal before a single bit is thrown.

**Middle — the act, the cracks, the big swing (Shots 3–10).** The attempts run
as a rule-of-three routine, each bit its own mini-scene with its own frame:
peekaboo played straight as vaudeville, the tummy raspberry shot close enough
to hear, the fake sneeze with its endless wind-up — and after every swing, the
same 85mm reaction frame, held a beat too long, reporting the same result:
nothing. Then the cracks. A chuckle fragment, one syllable long, gone before
anyone can celebrate. A cat strolls through the act, bats the dangling toy
once, and effortlessly out-performs everyone. A full smile-plus-squeal arrives
— everything except the laugh. So the act escalates to its final form: the
dad-dance, performed entirely off-screen while the camera holds on her
unmoved face, the film's biggest joke told without showing the joke.

**Close — honesty, payoff, button (Shots 11–15).** The intermission tells the
truth the guide week tells: at ten weeks a chuckle is early, a real laugh may
not be in stock, and delight — not footage — is the actual goal. And then,
because the standing camera never blinked through a week of golden alert
windows, it happens: THE LAUGH, first in silent 4K60 slow motion, the whole
face igniting frame by frame, then again at normal speed with sound, because
the sound is the point. The reverse angle catches two parents winning the
lottery. Flush with victory, they immediately re-run the winning bit — and
get nothing; she's moved on to her fist. The film returns to the opening
frame: deadpan restored, one slow blink, hard cut to black. She'll be here
all week.

## Mood & look

This is the series' comedy, but it wears the same A24-home-movie suit as
every other week — and that's the engine of the whole thing. The camera never
mugs: frames are symmetrical, level, formally composed, and completely
straight-faced, which makes the human silliness in front of them funnier and
the baby's indifference funnier still. Comedy grammar does the storytelling:
cut on action into the gags, hold the reactions past comfortable, escalate in
threes, and never — not once — cut away to explain a joke. The dance is
funnier unseen. The laugh is bigger heard.

Palette: the sunniest film of the twelve. Warm amber highlights and
teal-leaning shadows as always, but pitched a third of a stop brighter, with
half the usual vignette and a split-tone dialed back from wistful to warm —
matinee light, open midtones, milky blacks. The signature textures this week
are hers: the drool-shine, the chewed fist, the batting arms under the toy
arch. And the film's emotional whiplash lives in one edit: the silent
slow-motion laugh — pure image, time stretched, halation blooming — slammed
into the real-time replay with sound. Ninety seconds of deadpan formality,
detonated once.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The rule above all rules stands: the shot list is a menu, not a contract** —
and this week it has a corollary: **the laugh is a gift, not a deliverable.**
Nemours pegs the chuckle around the four-month mark; at ten weeks she is
early if it comes and perfectly on time if it doesn't. Run the bits because
they delight her, capture what happens, and cut the film with what she gives
— a chuckle fragment payoff is still a payoff. Never push a tired baby for
one more take. Overstimulated is the opposite of laughing.

**The standing-camera ritual (the film depends on it).** The a7CII lives on
its tripod at the mat's south edge all week, pre-framed for the 60p crop
(50mm frames like ~75mm — set the frame with 4K60 active), pre-set to
4K60/1-120/S-Log3/f2.8/ISO800/5600K. The Pocket 3 lives on the far shelf,
facing the parents. At every play session in a golden alert window: battery
check, card space check, both cameras to record, one sharp clap for sync,
iPhone in Voice Memos at the mat's edge — then forget all three exist and go
be ridiculous. The laugh will not schedule an appointment; the only winning
strategy is a camera that never blinks.

**Audio is the star this week — treat it like one.** The a7CII's internal
mics are honest but roomy at two meters, which is why the iPhone close-mic
and the Pocket 3's proximity carry the featured sounds. Before every
session, run the quiet-room protocol: fridge unplugged (leave your keys
inside it so you can't forget to replug), HVAC off, dishwasher off, phones
silenced, cat bells accounted for. Two minutes of ritual buys the cleanest
possible recording of a sound you will replay for the rest of your lives.

**Batch by energy, not by shot number.**
- *Any calm morning early in the week:* Shots 1 and 2 — the deadpan portrait
  (write the framing card) and the stage wide. Twenty minutes.
- *Play sessions, mid-morning golden windows (roughly 9–11am):* the attempt
  sequence — 3, 5, 6 as the bits rotate, 4 and 7 harvested in the gaps
  between bits, 9 when the squeal shows up. One or two bits per session,
  never the whole act; her attention is the budget.
- *Post-cat-dinner calm:* Shot 8, opportunistically, across however many
  evenings the cat requires.
- *One fresher-than-usual session:* Shot 10, the dance — it needs a
  performer with actual cardio left.
- *End of any long session:* Shot 11 — the exhaustion will be authentic.
- *Every session, automatically:* Shots 12 and 13 — that's the standing
  camera and the reverse doing their silent work.
- *After the laugh is in the can:* Shots 14 and 15 — the encore and the
  button, both banking on frames you taped earlier in the week. Leave all
  tape marks down until the card is wrapped.

**Battery, cards, ND — the pit-stop habit, upgraded.** Two always-rolling
cameras eat cards and batteries at twice the usual rate: every night, all
batteries on chargers, both cameras' cards offloaded to two places, formatted
in camera only after the second copy verifies. The 4K60 sessions are the
hungriest files of the series — clear the standing camera's card every
single night, because the one session you skip is the session she laughs.
The variable ND lives on the 50mm as always; window-bright sessions hold
f/2.8 with it, or close to f/4 without — the shutter never moves.

**Safety, restated because it's non-negotiable:** no light above or near the
baby where it could tip — the sticks in this film touch the floor behind
sofas and far walls only; nothing warm close to her; no camera or gimbal
ever passes over her — every tripod stands beyond the mat's edge and every
handheld angle comes from beside it; the bouncer stays on the floor with a
parent in arm's reach; bits stay soft — no loud noises, no sudden faces in
hers, peekaboo gentle, the sneeze a stage sneeze; the cat and the baby share
frames, never unsupervised proximity; and every session ends the moment she
says it ends. She's the director. She's also, this week, the entire cast —
the crew works for her.
