## The story

**Open — the mark (Shot 1).** No preamble: hands, morning light, and
letterboard tiles clicking into place until the board says the thing out loud
— ONE MONTH — with the date underneath. The film titles itself out of his own
props, and the board goes on to sit beside him in the portrait, so the opening
image is also the first object of the ritual. Cut hard from the finished board
into motion.

**The build (Shots 2–5).** The living-room corner transforms, shot with the
full making-of treatment it deserves: the beanbag arcs into place, the
backdrop stand clicks together, and then the film's one slow-motion indulgence
— seamless paper unfurling down the wall like a held breath finally let out.
The teddy bear takes the set for light tests while the Neewer flashes pop off
the ceiling (the film watches these as little environmental lightning strikes
— the video never chases them), and hands stretch the beanbag's cover taut,
sweeping the last wrinkle flat for someone very small. The joke and the
tenderness live in the same frames: this is an entirely serious photo studio,
built by two tired people, for a client who weighs nine pounds.

**The sitting (Shots 6–9).** He arrives on set carried, and the film's master
frame begins: the wide tableau of the whole studio — baby on the beanbag, one
parent kneeling with the X100VI, the other at his side, pops blooming the
ceiling — the exact composition that will be rebuilt from tape marks every
month until twelve of them cut together into their own film. Coverage lives
inside it: over the shoulder, his image small and composed on the camera's
screen while the real him breathes just past it; then the flashes go silent
and the film gives him the close-up week four has earned — eyes that track,
a face that studies faces, the almost-smile that is two weeks from being real.

**The measure and the pause (Shots 10–13).** Two measurements, taken like
vows: his hand around one parent finger, and his full length beside the bear
who just wrapped his shift as stand-in — nearly the same size today, a fact
with an expiry date the series exists to document. Then the honesty: week
four's crying arrives mid-session, the set stands empty while Mom paces the
window, and the film doesn't look away. When he settles, the parents look up
across their little studio and find each other, and nobody says "we made it a
month" because the look already did.

**Close — the portrait (Shots 14–15).** Evening. The studio is folded away and
the day has become a camera roll: two parents on the couch in lamp light,
scrolling, laughing at outtakes, going back, going back again — and then the
stillness when the one appears. The film cuts from the image on the little
screen to the image full-frame: the portrait, held, dead still, eight seconds.
One month old. Portrait no. 1. Fade to black; the end card makes the promise —
his name, the date, and the words that mean this will happen again in thirty
days.

## Mood & look

This film is brighter than its three older siblings — literally. Weeks 1–3
lived in window pools and lamp glow; week 4 spends its middle act on white
seamless paper that wraps light back into every shadow. Keep that airiness:
the studio scenes should feel like the inside of a lightbox with a family in
it, the grade's warm-amber/teal split dialed to half strength so the paper
stays white and his skin stays his. The flash pops are the signature texture
— never exposed for, always kept, bloomed in the grade until they read as
small suns over the set rather than clipped mistakes. Around the studio, the
house still looks like the house: the warm hallway, the lamp-lit couch, the
same rooms from the first three films, so the set reads as an island of
paper-white ceremony inside an ordinary, lived-in home.

The register is ceremonial-playful. The build is shot with real reverence and
cut with a light touch (the bear's light test is allowed to be funny); the
sitting is patient; the measurements are solemn in the way only tiny rituals
are; and the ending is quiet pride. The emotional shape is new for the series:
weeks 1–3 were about surviving and noticing — this one is the first film where
the family looks back on purpose, and builds the machine for looking back that
the next eleven months will run on.

Pace: brisk hands early, long holds late. The film accelerates through the
build, slows through the sitting, stops entirely at the portrait. A still
image as the final shot is the whole device — ninety seconds of motion earning
eight seconds of none.

## Prep & shoot-day plan

**The menu rule stands, with an asterisk.** The shot list is a menu, not a
contract — but this film, uniquely, has one load-bearing deliverable: the
portrait exists by the end of the week. Everything else can drop; Shot 15
cannot. Happily, it's also the shot with the most safety margin: if the
session collapses, the studio rebuilds from tape marks in ten minutes on any
better day.

**Split the work into three sessions.**
- *Session A — no baby (an evening or an out-of-room nap):* Shot 1 at the
  table, then the studio build — Shots 2, 3, 5 — and the full teddy-bear
  calibration, Shot 4. This is the long session (about an hour) and it's all
  pressure-free because the star isn't on set. End it by writing the settings
  card and laying every tape mark: "M1 · wide," "M1 · camera," "M1 · scale,"
  "M1 · bear," the sweep's floor line. The marks are the actual product of
  Session A; the footage is the bonus.
- *Session B — the sitting (the next fed, calm, alert mid-morning window):*
  Shots 6–11, in that order, with the Shot 7 wide rolling unattended
  throughout. Budget 20–30 minutes of his time, expect to use less. Shot 12
  isn't scheduled — it schedules itself; when it does, the spare parent picks
  up the Pocket 3 and otherwise the session simply pauses. Shot 13 closes the
  session whenever he's settled, whether or not everything else was captured.
- *Session C — the cull (that evening):* Shot 14 in lamp light, choosing for
  real. Export the winner to the archive that night — "portrait-01," two
  places — and if a print is in the cards, order it now while the ceremony's
  still warm.

**Flash rules, in one place, because he's four weeks old.** The Z2s are for
stills only and they never point at him: bounced off the ceiling or a wall
(or through diffusion), never bare toward his face. All power and framing
decisions get made on the teddy bear in Session A so the real sitting needs
the fewest pops possible — a hard budget of 10–15 for the whole session,
fired as single spaced pops with a soft spoken warning before each, never
bursts, never anything that resembles strobing. Watch him, not the histogram:
one startle is information, two startles ends flash for the day, and the
portrait finishes on window light without a moment's mourning. The a7CII
video never exposes for the flash — constant light only, with pops kept as
environmental blips.

**Set safety, restated for a studio week.** The beanbag is a supervised set
piece: he is never on it unattended, an adult hand stays within reach, he
lies on his back on the shallow center — never propped, never inclined — and
any sleepy portrait shows true safe sleep. Light stands and the backdrop
stand live outside his radius with legs fully spread and cables taped flat;
nothing that can tip stands within falling distance of the set, nothing warm
gets near him, and no camera — video or stills — ever shoots from directly
above him or passes over him. Set up during the fuss, shoot during the calm,
stop the moment he says stop. His schedule always wins; the studio can wait
thirty days, and in fact that's the entire idea.

**The ritual's paperwork.** Before the set folds away: photograph the taped
marks with the iPhone, tuck the settings card in the backdrop stand's bag,
and note the session in whatever the family calendar is — same corner, same
bear, same board, thirty days out. Next month the build takes ten minutes,
the framing matches to the centimeter, and the series quietly becomes the
most valuable thing this camera bag ever produces.
