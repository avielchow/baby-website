When we called in at 9:00 am on Friday, July 17th, they had room for us. After a week of waiting, that was it — the call. We were at the hospital by 11:00 am, waited in triage until about 6:00 pm, got our room in labor and delivery, and the induction began.

As the contractions got more intense, Jenn got the epidural — and the pain escalated incredibly quickly right before it went in. That was an intense moment. Once it was working, our incredible support friend and former nurse, Nhu, joined us. And then we waited: from 6:00 pm on July 17th to 6:00 pm on July 18th, a full twenty-four hours before active labor began.

That day is already a bit of a blur. It covered three nurse shifts, and with the one-to-one ratio plus training staff, we worked with six nurses — every one of them compassionate and incredible. Honestly, for the most part it was a calm, good time. Jenn spent the whole night chatting with Nhu and got a lot of sleep through the day. We had our comedy too. Jenn asked me for the time, scolded me for checking my phone when there was a clock right in front of us — and then proceeded to ask what time it was almost every hour until labor. At some point I sprayed AESOP refresher directly into her face. Between all that, we mostly worked on keeping Jenn comfortable, and we really had everything we could ask for: heated blankets, four great popsicle flavors, ice chips, hot broth on demand.

Active labor was something else. All we could really do was encourage Jenn through the contractions, use the peppermint spray, and get water into her between them. She pushed through two very intense hours — and then, after the last pushing positions, she hit a brick wall. She was shivering everywhere, and an intense amount of panic and fear crept in. She shut down. We covered her in warm blankets and waited for the OB.

When the OB arrived, he was clear: we needed forceps. Jenn was having a bit of a meltdown, and that half hour to an hour was the most emotional, intense, raw stretch of the whole thing. She was shaking so much. But the medical team gave us confidence, and somehow — somehow — Jenn pushed our boy out. The combination of fear, panic, joy, and exhaustion was the most intense thing I've ever felt, and I can't imagine what it was like for her. I am so amazed by what she went through to bring him into the world.

I remember the OB lifting him up and saying, *"Holy crap!"* We found out why a few minutes later: he was massive. Eight and a half pounds — the 90th percentile for Chinese babies. It was clear the forceps had really been the only option, and we're grateful for the entire team who got us through.

While the NICU staff took care of him, I stayed with Jenn — in those early moments I was just worried about Mom. Then those beautiful cries came from across the room, and a great wave of emotion and joy hit us both when we looked over and saw him: alive, well, and loud. Jenn holding him for the first time was a beautiful moment. Then Nhu and I started cleaning up to get ready for postpartum. We'd been at the hospital for thirty-six hours, running mostly on adrenaline.

In postpartum he had his first feed, with an amazing nurse guiding us through it. He latched almost on the first try and ate for fifteen, twenty minutes. It was magical, and we were so fortunate it went that well. The night after was not magical: he cried through most of it, and I slept about thirty minutes, on the floor. At 9:00 am Jenn's parents came to switch with me — I went home, showered, napped until 2:00, picked up flowers, sushi, and (priorities) a macro lens, and was back at the hospital by 8:00 pm with renewed energy. That night I could actually do things: calm him down, change diapers, swaddle, take care of Jenn and the baby both. I even found time to write the notes that became this entry.

And on the third day, we gave him his name: **Cody Holden Chow.**

We loved Cody instantly. Holden grew on us — the way he hung on inside Mom's belly through twenty-four hours of induction, the way he holds on for milk. It just fit more and more, so it became the middle name. We also may or may not have joked, several times, that if the induction lasted twenty-four hours we'd name him Holden. It did. It lasted long enough to cycle through three OBs — landing us on the one we'd always wanted, Kovid Lee.
